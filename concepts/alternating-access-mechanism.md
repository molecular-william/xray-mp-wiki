# Alternating-Access Mechanism

## Overview
The alternating-access mechanism is a fundamental principle of membrane transport. Transporters alternate between outward-facing and inward-facing conformations, exposing the binding site to opposite sides of the membrane. This prevents simultaneous access to both sides. For the mitochondrial ADP/ATP carrier, the mechanism involves coupled rotation of three domains about a central substrate-binding site fulcrum, with core elements rocking ~15 degrees and gate elements moving inward/outward relative to the pseudo-symmetry axis.

## Mechanism/Details
The alternating-access mechanism is a fundamental principle of membrane transport. Transporters alternate between outward-facing and inward-facing conformations, exposing the binding site to opposite sides of the membrane. For the NhaA Na+/H+ antiporter (Hunte et al. 2005, PDB 1YQY), the structure revealed the inward-open conformation with the substrate-binding site accessible from the cytoplasmic side, demonstrating the alternating access mechanism in an MFS transporter. For the mitochondrial ADP/ATP carrier, this involves rigid-body rotation of three core domains coupled with inward movement of gate elements, using the substrate-binding site as a fulcrum. The carrier cycles between cytoplasmic-open (c-state) and matrix-open (m-state) conformations, with each state locked by a specific inhibitor (CATR for c-state, BKA for m-state).
