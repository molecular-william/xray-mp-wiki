# Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS)

## Overview
The Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS) is one of the largest families of secondary transporters. Members typically have 12 transmembrane helices and use the rocker-switch mechanism for alternating access. They transport a wide variety of substrates including sugars, ions, nucleosides, and drugs. Notable members include the NhaA Na+/H+ antiporter (10 TM helices) and the POT (proton-dependent oligopeptide transporters) and NHS (nucleoside proton symporters) families.

## Mechanism/Details
The Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS) is one of the largest families of secondary transporters. Members typically have 12 transmembrane helices and use the rocker-switch mechanism for alternating access. They transport a wide variety of substrates including sugars, ions, nucleosides, and drugs. The NhaA Na+/H+ antiporter from E. coli (Hunte et al. 2005, PDB 1YQY) is a notable exception with only 10 TM helices and represents the first structure of a Na+/H+ antiporter. The MFS includes several subfamilies: the POT (proton-dependent oligopeptide transporter) family, the NHS (nucleoside proton symporter) family, and many others. The NHS family includes transporters like NupG from E. coli that specifically transport nucleosides coupled to proton gradients.
