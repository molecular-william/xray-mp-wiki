# Non-competitive Inhibition of Rhomboid Proteases

## Overview
Non-competitive inhibition is the characteristic mode of inhibition exhibited by substrate-mimicking peptide aldehydes against rhomboid intramembrane proteases. Unlike competitive inhibition where inhibitors compete with substrate for the active site, non-competitive inhibition allows the inhibitor to bind whether or not substrate is already bound. For rhomboid proteases, this reveals that the initial substrate-enzyme Michaelis complex does not involve substrate contact with the catalytic center, providing direct kinetic evidence for a two-stage catalytic mechanism.


## Mechanism/Details
Non-competitive inhibition by peptide aldehydes was demonstrated through real-time kinetic assays of [GLPG](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/glpg/) reconstituted in E. coli lipid liposomes. Key observations: (1) Km values remained unchanged across inhibitor concentrations (p = 0.16-0.80), (2) Vmax decreased in a dose-dependent manner (p = 2.1e-9 to 2.0e-13), consistent with non-competitive kinetics. This pattern was observed with tripeptide (Ac-RMA-CHO), tetrapeptide (Ac-VRMA-CHO), and hexapeptide (Ac-RKVRMA-CHO) aldehydes, and was also seen with the rhomboid protease AarA from Providencia stuartii. The non-competitive mode means peptide aldehydes can bind to the substrate-enzyme Michaelis complex, confirming that the initial substrate binding occurs at a peripheral site distant from the catalytic serine.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [GLPG](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/glpg/) (E. coli rhomboid): Tetrapeptide aldehyde Ac-VRMA-CHO showed non-competitive
  inhibition with Ki of 113 +/- 4 uM; Km unchanged, Vmax decreased dose-dependently
- [GLPG](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/glpg/) (E. coli rhomboid): Hexapeptide aldehyde Ac-RKVRMA-CHO was more potent than
  tetrapeptide, showing the same non-competitive inhibition pattern
- AarA (Providencia stuartii rhomboid): Same non-competitive inhibition confirmed
  the mechanism is conserved across rhomboid proteases despite <15% sequence identity
