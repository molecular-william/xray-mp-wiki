# Vitamin K Catalytic Cycle

## Overview
The vitamin K catalytic cycle is a biochemical cycle in the endoplasmic reticulum that sustains blood coagulation by regenerating reduced vitamin K hydroquinone (KH2) from vitamin K epoxide (KO) via the quinone intermediate (K). Vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR) catalyzes both reduction steps, each coupled to the oxidation of active-site cysteines. The cycle is essential for the gamma-glutamyl carboxylation of glutamate residues in vitamin K-dependent clotting factors. Vitamin K antagonists such as warfarin inhibit this cycle by binding to VKOR and blocking substrate access.


## Mechanism/Details
The catalytic cycle of VKOR proceeds through four distinct redox states accompanied by open-to-closed conformational changes:

1. **Partially oxidized state (State I)**: Approximately 50% of cellular HsVKOR is in the partially oxidized state with reduced Cys135 and Cys43-Cys51 disulfide. This is the catalytically competent starting state.

2. **Substrate binding and adduct formation (State II)**: Reduced Cys135 attacks the substrate (KO or K), forming a charge-transfer complex or covalent C-S adduct. This interaction induces the closed conformation through hydrogen bonding of the naphthoquinone ring to Asn80 and Tyr139.

3. **Electron transfer (State III)**: In the closed conformation, Cys43 is positioned close to Cys51-Cys132 for thiol-disulfide exchange. The resulting reduced Cys132 attacks the Cys135-substrate adduct, generating the fully reduced product (KH2).

4. **Product release and return to open state (State IV)**: The active site becomes fully oxidized (Cys43-Cys51 and Cys132-Cys135 disulfides), and the protein returns to the open conformation, allowing product release. The enzyme is then reduced by partner proteins (e.g., thioredoxin-like proteins) or glutathione to return to State I.

The closed conformation induced by substrate binding closely resembles that induced by warfarin binding. Warfarin exploits the same hydrogen-bonding interactions with Asn80 and Tyr139 that are required for substrate binding, acting as a competitive inhibitor that stabilizes the closed conformation without undergoing catalysis.
