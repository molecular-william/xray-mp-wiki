# Intramembrane Ion Coordination

## Overview
Intramembrane ion coordination refers to the phenomenon where metal cations (such as potassium, calcium, or other divalent/trivalent ions) are bound within the hydrophobic interior of a lipid bilayer by polar side chains of transmembrane protein helices. This represents a departure from the classical view that polar/charged groups are entirely excluded from the membrane core. Ion coordination sites within transmembrane helix interfaces provide additional stabilization to protein oligomers and can influence the specificity and stability of protein-protein interactions within the membrane.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [DAP12 (CD331)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/receptors-signaling/dap12/) — Potassium ion coordinated by aspartic acid and threonine side chains at the trimeric TM interface (PDB 4WOL). Seven oxygen atoms from Asp and Thr residues from three chains coordinate the K+ ion. MD simulations showed stability with one ionized aspartic acid.
- [DAP12 (CD331)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/receptors-signaling/dap12/) — Calcium ion coordinated at the tetrameric TM interface (PDB 4WO1). Two overlapping Ca2+ coordination sites with pentagonal bipyramidal geometry, each with 50% occupancy, including a network of water molecules.
