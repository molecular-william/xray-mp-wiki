# Retinal Isomerization in Microbial Rhodopsins

## Overview
Retinal isomerization is the fundamental photochemical event in microbial rhodopsins that initiates the photocycle and drives ion transport. The retinal chromophore, covalently bound to a conserved lysine residue via a Schiff base linkage, undergoes light-induced isomerization between all-trans and 13-cis configurations. In the dark-adapted state, thermal equilibrium between these isomers is established without energy input, while in the light-adapted state, photon absorption is required for isomerization. The protein environment modulates the energy landscape of retinal isomerization through subtle changes in the Schiff base electronic environment and internal water networks, enabling both thermal and photochemical isomerization pathways.

## Mechanism/Details
The isomerization of retinal is governed by the protein environment surrounding the Schiff base. In the dark-adapted state of AR3 (PDB: 6GUX, 1.3 A), the pentagonal H-bond network around the Schiff base is relatively ordered, with Wat402 well-resolved and Asp95 in a single conformation. This ordered network reduces the activation energy for retinal isomerization to 17.1 kcal/mol, allowing thermal equilibration between 13-cis (70%) and all-trans (30%) isomers at room temperature. In the light-adapted state (PDB: 6S6C, 1.1 A), the Schiff base nitrogen shifts by 0.5 A, destabilizing the water network: Wat402 becomes disordered and Asp95 adopts two conformations. This destabilization raises the activation energy to 21.5 kcal/mol, making thermal isomerization negligible and requiring photon absorption. The energy difference between cis and trans isomers is approximately ten times larger in the LA state (10.9 kcal/mol) than in the DA state (-1.9 kcal/mol).
