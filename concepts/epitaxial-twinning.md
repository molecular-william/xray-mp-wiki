# Epitaxial Twinning

## Overview
Epitaxial twinning is a crystallographic phenomenon in which two or more crystal lattices grow in a defined geometric relationship, sharing common crystallographic orientations. In epitaxial twinning, the lattices are not independent but instead show a clear geometric relationship, producing a characteristic diffraction pattern with a mix of well-separated spots (from one lattice) and precisely superimposed spots (from both lattices). This is one hallmark of epitaxial twinning and can complicate structure determination if not recognized.


## Mechanism/Details
Epitaxial twinning commonly occurs when many small crystals are included within a single loop during crystal mounting. Unlike random twinning, epitaxial twins show precise geometric relationships between spot predictions for the different lattices, resulting in a characteristic pattern of well-separated spots alongside precisely superimposed spots. This phenomenon can be identified during data collection by observing the diffraction pattern and indexing multiple lattices. Recognizing epitaxial twinning is important because it affects data processing strategies and may require specialized refinement approaches.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- M3 Muscarinic Acetylcholine Receptor: M3-wtT4L crystals showed evidence of epitaxial
  twinning. Many frames were dominated by a single lattice, but in some cases two
  distinct lattices could be indexed. The spot predictions for the two lattices
  showed a clear geometric relationship, giving a combination of well-separated and
  overlapping spots. This twinning was eliminated by using mT4L (minimal T4 lysozyme)
  instead of wtT4L, which provided only one T4L packing arrangement instead of two.
