# H⁺ Countertransport in P-type ATPases

## Overview
H⁺ countertransport in P-type ATPases refers to the coupled movement of
protons in the opposite direction to the primary pumped ion (e.g., Ca²⁺, Na⁺,
K⁺) during the catalytic cycle. In the SERCA1a Ca²⁺-ATPase, two or three H⁺
are pumped from the lumen to the cytoplasm per ATP hydrolyzed, opposite to the
direction of Ca²⁺ transport. The landmark 2.4-Å-resolution crystal structure
of SERCA1a in the Ca²⁺-free E2 state stabilized by BHQ and thapsigargin
(PDB 2AGV), combined with continuum electrostatic calculations with explicit
hydrogens, revealed that H⁺ countertransport is not merely a functional
accessory but a structural necessity. When Ca²⁺ is released, the empty
Ca²⁺-binding sites contain a cluster of carboxyl groups (Glu-58, Glu-309,
Glu-771, Asp-800) in the hydrophobic core of the membrane. Without
protonation, the charge imbalance would incur a huge energetic cost
(>100 kcal/mol). Water molecules also fill the space, but water alone cannot
substitute for metal ions because a water molecule is larger (atomic radius
1.4 Å vs. Ca²⁺ 0.99 Å) and can accommodate fewer oxygen atoms (four vs.
six to eight). Therefore, cation countertransport — either H⁺ or other
monovalent cations — is mandatory for maintaining the structural integrity
of empty ion-binding sites in all P-type ATPases.



## Mechanism/Details
In the classical E1/E2 alternating-access model of P-type ATPases, the
transmembrane ion-binding sites have high affinity for the primary ion (Ca²⁺,
Na⁺, H⁺) and face the cytoplasm in the E1 state, whereas they have low
affinity and face the opposite side of the membrane in the E2 state. Protons
bind to the ATPase in E2 and dissociate in the E1 state, in exchange with the
primary cation. In SERCA1a (Ca²⁺-ATPase), four residues become protonated
in the E2 state: Glu-58, Glu-309, Glu-771, and Asp-800. In the
Ca²⁺-bound E1·2Ca²⁺ state, only Glu-58 and Glu-908 are protonated. The
difference (two protons) is consistent with the experimentally observed
stoichiometry of 2 H⁺ countertransported per 2 Ca²⁺ transported. All
protonated residues in E2 coordinate Ca²⁺ in the E1 state, so Ca²⁺ binding
is effectively an exchange reaction with H⁺.

The conformational changes that accompany Ca²⁺ release and H⁺ uptake include:
peptide bond flipping of Glu-309 (ψ angle change of 168° from β-strand to
helix conformation), rotation of M6 by nearly 90° between Asn-796 and
Asp-800, and the movement of a phospholipid molecule into the V-shaped cleft
between M2 and M4 helices. Water molecules (five identified in the
Ca²⁺-binding cavity of E2) fill the space created by Ca²⁺ release, forming
hydrogen bonds that link transmembrane helices.

The same principle extends to other P-type ATPases: Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase and gastric
H⁺,K⁺-ATPase countertransport K⁺ instead of H⁺, with Asn-796 in SERCA changed
to Asp in these pumps to coordinate the larger K⁺ ion. This demonstrates that
while the identity of the countertransported ion varies, the fundamental
requirement for charge compensation at empty binding sites is universal.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [SERCA1a](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/serca1a/) — 2.4-Å E2(TG+BHQ) structure (PDB 2AGV) combined with continuum electrostatic calculations revealed four protonated residues in E2 state and established H⁺ countertransport as a structural requirement
- [Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase (Shark)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/shark-na-k-atpase/) — Countertransports K⁺ instead of H⁺; shares the same P-type ATPase superfamily mechanism with adapted binding site geometry
- [Gastric H⁺,K⁺-ATPase](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/gastric-hk-atpase/) — Countertransports K⁺ instead of H⁺; analogous structural adaptation at the cation-binding sites
