# Syringe-Like Injection Mechanism of Tc Toxins

## Overview
The syringe-like injection mechanism describes how Tc (toxin complex) toxins from bacterial pathogens such as Photorhabdus luminescens and Yersinia pestis deliver toxic enzymes into host cells. The TcA subunit forms a translocation channel that inserts into the host membrane like a syringe needle, while the TcB-TccC complex opens a gate to release the toxic domain into the channel. An entropic spring mechanism provides the driving force for membrane insertion.


## Mechanism/Details
Tc toxin complexes are composed of three subunits: TcA, TcB, and TcC. TcA forms a bell-shaped pentameric prepore structure that, upon pH-triggered shell opening, undergoes a conformational change driven by an entropic spring linker to inject its central channel into the host membrane. The TcB-TccC fusion protein forms a cocoon that encapsulates the toxic ADP-ribosyltransferase domain. Binding of TcB-TccC to TcA opens a six-bladed beta-propeller gate, creating a continuous channel from the cocoon interior through the TcA translocation pore. The toxic domain passes through this channel into the host cytoplasm, where it modifies target proteins (e.g., G-actin or RhoA) to disrupt cellular function. This mechanism differs from typical pore-forming toxins and represents a sophisticated molecular syringe for targeted protein delivery. The cationic nature of Tc toxins suggests a pH-independent translocation mechanism, contrasting with the protonation-driven Brownian ratchet used by other toxin systems.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Tc toxin from Photorhabdus luminescens: TcA forms a syringe-like translocation channel (4.0 A prepore, 9 A pore structures) that injects into host membranes, driven by an entropic spring linker; TcB-TccC opens a beta-propeller gate to deliver the toxic ADP-ribosyltransferase domain (Nature 2014)
