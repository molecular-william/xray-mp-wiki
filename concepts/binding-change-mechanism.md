# Binding-Change Mechanism

## Overview
The binding-change mechanism is the catalytic mechanism proposed for ATP synthase (F1F0-ATPase) in which the three catalytic subunits cycle through three distinct conformational states (open, loose, tight) at any given time. Proposed by Paul Boyer, the mechanism was confirmed at atomic resolution by the 1994 crystal structure of bovine mitochondrial F1-ATPase, which showed the three beta-subunits in different conformations with different bound nucleotides (ATP-analogue, ADP, and empty). The mechanism requires inherent asymmetry of the F1 complex and is implemented through rotation of the alpha3beta3 subassembly around the central gamma-subunit.

## Mechanism/Details
The binding-change mechanism proposes that the three catalytic sites of ATP synthase exist in three different states at any instant: open (O, very low affinity for ligands, catalytically inactive), loose (L, loosely binds ligands, catalytically inactive), and tight (T, tightly binds ligands, catalytically active). The three sites interconvert through energy-dependent conformational changes driven by proton transport through the F0 domain. The mechanism requires that F1-ATPase be an inherently asymmetric assembly. The tight site with bound ATP is converted to an open site releasing ATP, while a loose site with ADP and phosphate is converted to a tight site where ATP forms. Fresh substrates bind to an open site converting it to loose, and so on. The energy-requiring steps are substrate binding and product release. Strong support for this mechanism comes from the structure of bovine F1-ATPase complexed with efrapeptin (1996), which showed that the antibiotic inhibits both ATP synthesis and hydrolysis by binding to the empty beta-E subunit and blocking its conversion to a nucleotide-binding conformation, thereby preventing the cyclic interconversion of catalytic sites.
