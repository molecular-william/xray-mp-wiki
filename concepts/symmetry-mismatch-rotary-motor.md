# Symmetry Mismatch in Rotary Molecular Motors

## Overview
Symmetry mismatch between rotor and stator components is a recurring design principle in biological rotary molecular motors, including ATP synthase, the bacterial flagellar motor, and bacteriophage DNA packaging motors. In ATP synthase, the F1 catalytic head has pseudo-3-fold symmetry (alpha3beta3) while the c-ring rotor in F0 has 10-fold (or 8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15-fold depending on species) symmetry. This mismatch prevents the system from falling into deep energy minima that would occur with matching symmetries, thereby facilitating smooth rotation. The 1999 crystal structure of the yeast F1c10 subcomplex at 3.9 A resolution (Stock, Leslie, and Walker) provided the first direct structural evidence for this symmetry mismatch, revealing a ring of 10 c-subunits (not 12 as anticipated) in contact with the gamma and delta subunits of the F1 central stalk. The mismatch between 3-fold (F1) and 10-fold (c-ring) symmetry implies a non-integral H+/ATP ratio between 3 and 4, and requires elasticity in the gamma subunit to accommodate the different stepping of the c-ring (10 steps per rotation) and F1 (3 steps per rotation).


## Mechanism/Details
The principle of symmetry mismatch in molecular rotary engines was first discussed in relation to the DNA translocating machinery of bacteriophages (Hendrix, 1978), where the dodecameric head-tail connector rotates within the icosahedral capsid (5-fold symmetry axis). During phage assembly, DNA is injected through the sixfold symmetrical tail into the fivefold symmetric head, and the connector is believed to rotate relative to the head during packaging. Symmetry mismatch also appears in the bacterial flagellar motor, which is driven by proton-motive force similarly to ATP synthase. Additional examples include ATP-dependent proteases where a rotational mechanism contributes to protein unfolding. In ATP synthase, two distinct regions of asymmetric interaction exist: the gamma subunit rotating within alpha3beta3 (F1), and the c-ring rotating relative to the a subunit (F0). The 1999 yeast F1c10 structure directly showed that six or seven consecutive c-subunits (about two-thirds of the ring surface) contact the foot of the central stalk, with the remaining c-subunits being exposed and less ordered, supporting the model of a rotating ensemble.
