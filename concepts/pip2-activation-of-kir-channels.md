# PIP2 Activation of Inward Rectifier K+ Channels

## Overview
Phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2) is the primary agonist for classical inward rectifier (Kir2) potassium channels. PIP2 binding at the TMD-CTD interface induces a large conformational change: the CTD translates 6 A towards the TMD, contracting a flexible expansion linker into a compact helical structure, and the inner helix gate begins to open. This mechanism is analogous to neurotransmitter activation of ion channels at synapses. PIP2 is distinct from its roles as a second messenger (cleaved into DAG and IP3) and as a membrane localization signal.

## Mechanism/Details
PIP2 binds at the interface between the transmembrane domain (TMD) and the cytoplasmic domain (CTD) of Kir2 channels. The PIP2-binding site comprises a conserved non-specific phospholipid-binding region in the TMD (involving the RWR/KWR sequence motif) and a specific phosphatidylinositol-binding region in the CTD. The acyl chains, glycerol backbone and 1' phosphate of PIP2 interact with the TMD, while the 4',5'-phosphate-substituted inositol head group makes specific interactions with the CTD. On PIP2 binding, a flexible expansion linker contracts to a compact helical structure, the CTD translates 6 A and becomes tethered to the TMD, and the inner helix gate begins to open. Small head group anionic lipids like PPA that lack the inositol ring bind the TMD but fail to engage the CTD or open the channel, demonstrating the specificity of the PIP2 inositol head group interaction.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Chicken Kir2.2 — PIP2-bound structure (PDB 3SPI, 3.3 A) shows the CTD translated 6 A towards TMD and inner helix gate opening to 6.3 A. PPA-bound structure (PDB 3SPJ, 2.45 A) shows closed conformation (4.9 A gate) with CTD unengaged.
- GIRK2 (Kir3.2) — PIP2 is an essential cofactor for GIRK2 activation, binding at the TMD-CTD interface. G protein betagamma subunits and PIP2 together open both the G-loop and inner helix gates.
