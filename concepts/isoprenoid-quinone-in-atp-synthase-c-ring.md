# Isoprenoid Quinones inside the ATP Synthase c-Ring Pore

## Overview
The hypothesis that isoprenoid quinones (plastoquinone in chloroplasts,
coenzyme Q/ubiquinone in mitochondria, menaquinone in bacteria) are
present inside the central pore of the c-ring of F1Fo ATP synthases.
This is based on the observation of additional electron densities
forming circles at 5.4 Angstrom intervals inside all known high-resolution
c-ring structures, the unusually large hydrophobic thickness of the
internal pore surface (45.8 Angstrom), the universality of these
densities across species, and UV-Vis spectroscopic evidence consistent
with quinone isoprenoids.


## Mechanism/Details
**Evidence from the 2.3 Angstrom spinach chloroplast c14-ring structure:**

The high-resolution structure of the spinach chloroplast ATP synthase
c14-ring revealed three striking features:

1. **Additional electron densities inside the c-ring** - Fo-Fc difference
   electron density maps show three circular rings of density at 5.4
   Angstrom spacing, parallel to the membrane plane. The distance matches
   the spacing between methyl groups of an isoprenoid chain (~5 Angstrom).
   These densities are NOT characteristic of DNA, RNA, or typical membrane
   lipids (which have C-C single bonds at ~1.5 Angstrom spacing).

2. **Unusually large hydrophobic thickness** - The inner surface of the
   c-ring pore has a hydrophobic thickness of 45.8 Angstrom, approximately
   1.5 times larger than the external hydrophobic thickness (32.6 Angstrom)
   and typical lipid bilayers. The conical shape (30 Angstrom inner
   diameter at stroma, 23 Angstrom at center, 28 Angstrom at lumen) makes
   it unlikely that regular cylindrical lipids fill the pore.

3. **Highly asymmetric polar/apolar interfaces** - The hydrophobic region
   of the pore extends nearly to the stromal surface, with a very small
   polar part, unlike typical membrane protein surfaces.

**Universality across species:**

Similar circular additional densities are present in all available
high-resolution c-ring structures (Table S2 of the paper), including:
- Yeast mitochondrial c10-ring
- Bacillus pseudofirmus OF4 c13-ring
- Arthrospira platensis c15-ring
- Ilyobacter tartaricus c11-ring (Na+-driven)

The strength of these densities correlates with the harshness of
purification conditions: structures from intact ATP synthases using
mild conditions show stronger densities, while those exposed to harsh
detergents or high temperature show weaker densities.

**Correlation with isoprenoid quinone chain lengths:**

The hydrophobic thickness of the inner pore surface correlates well
with the chain lengths of the isoprenoid quinones found in each
organism's membrane:
- Chloroplast (45.8 Angstrom): plastoquinone-9 (PQ-9, 9 isoprene units)
- Mitochondria (~40 Angstrom): coenzyme Q10/ubiquinone-10 (UQ-10)
- Bacteria (~35-40 Angstrom): menaquinone-7 (MQ-7)

**Spectroscopic evidence:**

- Crystals had a strong yellow color
- UV-Vis spectra showed strong absorption between 400-500 nm
  (characteristic of isoprenoids)
- A peak at 335 nm does not correspond to beta-carotene or chlorophyll a
  but is consistent with neutral plastosemiquinone-9
- Differential UV-Vis spectroscopy with NaBH4 (reducing agent) showed
  a peak at 260 nm matching trimethyl benzoquinone (TMBQ), the polar
  moiety of plastoquinone-9

**Proposed functional roles:**

1. **Stabilization of the c-ring** - The isoprenoid molecules may fill
   the central pore, providing structural support to the ring of
   c-subunits and preventing collapse

2. **Ion leakage prevention** - By plugging the central pore, isoprenoids
   may prevent proton or sodium ion leakage through the c-ring, which
   would short-circuit the rotary mechanism

3. **ROS protection** - Isoprenoid quinones are known antioxidants;
   they may scavenge reactive oxygen species (ROS) that could damage
   the c-ring, particularly in mitochondria where the electron transport
   chain generates superoxide

4. **Reduced friction** - A layer of isoprenoid molecules covering the
   inner surface of the c-ring may reduce friction between the rotating
   c-ring and the central stalk/stator during rotation


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Spinach chloroplast ATP synthase c14-ring — 2.3 Angstrom structure showing three circles of additional density at 5.4 Angstrom spacing inside the pore, hypothesized to be plastoquinone-9
- Yeast mitochondrial ATP synthase c10-ring — Similar internal electron densities hypothesized to correspond to coenzyme Q10 (ubiquinone-10)
- Bacillus pseudofirmus OF4 c13-ring — Bacterial c-ring with internal densities hypothesized to correspond to menaquinone-7
