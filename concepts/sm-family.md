# SMR Family (Small Multidrug Resistance Family)

## Overview
The small multidrug resistance (SMR) family is a group of bacterial multidrug efflux transporters that serve as the proposed evolutionary progenitor of the drug/metabolite transporter (DMT) superfamily. SMR family members are characterized by a four-transmembrane segment architecture and function as homodimers to export toxic compounds from cells. The best-characterized member is [EMRE](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/emre/) from Escherichia coli, which exports a wide range of positively charged compounds (natural and synthetic) including [Ethidium - Fluorescent Intercalating Dye](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/ethidium/), tetraphenylphosphonium, and benzylamine.


## Mechanism/Details
SMR family transporters form homodimers, with each protomer containing four transmembrane alpha-helices. The dimeric architecture creates a substrate-binding site at the interface between the two protomers. Transport is thought to occur via an alternating-access mechanism, where the dimer cycles between inward-facing and outward-facing conformations. Despite their small size (approximately 110 amino acids per protomer), SMR transporters can recognize and export a remarkably diverse range of structurally unrelated compounds.
