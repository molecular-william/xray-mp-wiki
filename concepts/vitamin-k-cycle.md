# Vitamin K Cycle

## Overview
The vitamin K cycle is a biochemical pathway that recycles vitamin K between its reduced (hydroquinone) and oxidized (epoxide) forms. The cycle involves two key enzymes: gamma-glutamyl carboxylase (GGCX), which uses vitamin K hydroquinone as a cofactor to carboxylate glutamate residues on vitamin K-dependent proteins (producing vitamin K epoxide as a byproduct), and vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR), which reduces vitamin K epoxide back to vitamin K, completing the cycle. The cycle is essential for blood coagulation and other physiological processes. Warfarin and related anticoagulants disrupt the cycle by inhibiting VKOR, thereby depleting the supply of active vitamin K hydroquinone.

## Mechanism/Details
The vitamin K cycle operates as follows: (1) Vitamin K hydroquinone serves as an essential cofactor for GGCX (gamma-glutamyl carboxylase), which catalyzes the carboxylation of glutamate residues on precursor proteins to form gamma-carboxylated glutamate (Gla) residues. This reaction requires CO2 and O2 and produces vitamin K epoxide as a byproduct. (2) VKOR (vitamin K epoxide reductase) reduces the vitamin K epoxide back to vitamin K, completing the cycle. The reduction is thought to proceed through a vitamin K quinol intermediate. (3) Vitamin K is then available for another round of carboxylation by GGCX. The cycle is tightly regulated and can be disrupted by warfarin and related anticoagulants, which bind to VKOR and prevent the reduction of vitamin K epoxide. The Synechococcus sp. VKOR structure revealed the active site where ubiquinone binds and where the CXXC motif cysteines participate in the redox reaction. Mammalian VKOR is postulated to interact with PDI (protein disulfide isomerase) as a redox partner, which transfers electrons to VKOR for the reduction reaction.
