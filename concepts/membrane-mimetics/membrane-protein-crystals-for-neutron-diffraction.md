# Membrane-Protein Crystals for Neutron Diffraction

## Overview
Neutron macromolecular crystallography (NMX) can identify individual protons
in biological macromolecules, providing unique insight into protonation states,
hydrogen-bonding networks, and catalytic mechanisms. However, NMX requires
much larger crystals than X-ray crystallography due to the inherently lower
flux of neutron sources (<10^8 neutrons/mm^2/s vs >10^12 photons/100 um^2/s
at synchrotron beamlines). This concept describes strategies for growing
membrane-protein crystals large enough for neutron diffraction, as
exemplified by the calcium-transporting ATPase SERCA1.

A room-temperature X-ray structure of SERCA1 (PDB 6hef) was determined from
large crystals to validate that the crystallization strategies produced
diffraction-quality crystals suitable as a test case for future NMX studies.
The structure (3.54 A resolution, space group C2) shows no significant
differences from cryotemperature structures, confirming the feasibility of
room-temperature data collection.
