# Lipid Annulus in Membrane Protein Structures

## Overview
A lipid annulus is a belt of ordered lipid molecules surrounding a membrane protein within the bilayer, forming the first shell of lipid-protein interactions. While annular lipids are often disordered and lost during detergent purification, some crystal structures capture them as they adapt to the protein's surface features. The GlpG structure in bicelles revealed 14 ordered lipid molecules forming an asymmetric annulus around the rhomboid protease.

## Mechanism/Details
Lipid molecules in the annulus adapt to the protein's surface features, with acyl chains fitting into grooves and crevices. The hydrophobic thickness of the protein (distance between aromatic/charged boundary residues) determines the local bilayer thickness. Lipids can compress or stretch to match the hydrophobic match condition, resulting in bilayer thinning or thickening around the protein. In GlpG, the hydrophobic belt is ~23 Å wide, and lipids form an asymmetric bilayer with ordered lipids mainly on the cytoplasmic side. In contrast, detergents in delipidated structures occupy analogous positions but with shorter acyl chains (C9 vs C14), resulting in a thinner hydrophobic coverage.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- GlpG (E. coli rhomboid protease) — 14 ordered lipid molecules in DMPC bicelle structure (2XTV, 1.7 Å). Lipids cluster around TM1, L1, and TM3. Asymmetric bilayer with well-ordered lipids only on cytoplasmic side. 17 nonyl glucoside molecules occupy analogous positions in delipidated structure (2XTU).
- Bacteriorhodopsin — 18 tightly bound lipid chains form an annulus around the trimer, with contacts between trimers mediated almost exclusively by lipids.
- Aquaporin-0 (Aqp0) — Ordered 2D lipid-containing arrays with lipid molecules surrounding the protein; C2-C2 distance of glycerol backbone measured as ~27 Å.
