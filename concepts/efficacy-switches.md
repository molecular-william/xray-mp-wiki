# Efficacy Switches in GPCR Signaling

## Overview
Efficacy switches are molecular mechanisms by which specific residues in G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) control the functional selectivity (biased signaling) of receptor activation. In the delta-opioid receptor (δ-OR), sodium-coordinating residues within the allosteric sodium binding site act as efficacy switches: mutations at these positions (Asp95, Asn131, Asn310, Asn314) can transform classical antagonists into potent beta-arrestin-biased agonists or augment constitutive beta-arrestin signaling.


## Mechanism/Details
The allosteric sodium ion binding site in class A GPCRs contains several residues that coordinate a structurally bound sodium ion. In the δ-OR, these include Asp95 (2.50), Asn131 (3.35), Ser135 (3.39), Asn310 (7.45), and Asn314 (7.49). Mutating these residues alters the receptor's signaling balance between G-protein and beta-arrestin pathways. The Asn131 (3.35) mutation to alanine abolishes sodium binding entirely and creates constitutive beta-arrestin signaling exceeding wild-type maximal activation. The Asn131 (3.35) mutation to valine retains sodium binding with lower affinity, producing a different signaling phenotype. Mutations at Asp95 (2.50), Asn310 (7.45), and Asn314 (7.49) transform classical δ-OR antagonists (naltrindole, naltriben, BNTX) that show no beta-arrestin activity at wild-type receptors into potent beta-arrestin-biased agonists. These sodium-coordinating residues thus function as molecular efficacy switches, controlling the conformational landscape that determines pathway-selective signaling.
