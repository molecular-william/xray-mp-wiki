# Pore-Forming Toxins

## Overview
Pore-forming toxins (PFTs) are a large class of bacterial virulence factors that disrupt the plasma membrane of host cells by forming transmembrane pores. More than 25% of all cytotoxic bacterial proteins are PFTs, found in virtually all important classes of pathogens including Gram-negative bacteria (*Escherichia coli*, *Aeromonas hydrophila*, *Vibrio cholerae*) and Gram-positive pathogens (*Staphylococcus aureus*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Listeria monocytogenes*). PFTs are classified as alpha-PFTs or beta-PFTs based on the structure they form after penetrating the host cell membrane. Alpha-PFTs (including the cholesterol-dependent cytolysin family) typically form alpha-helical pores, while beta-PFTs form beta-barrel pores. CDCs represent the largest family among bacterial PFTs. PFTs oligomerize on the membrane surface into ring-shaped structures that insert into the lipid bilayer, resulting in ion flux changes, cell lysis, and diverse host cell signaling responses including activation of inflammatory pathways, autophagy induction, and cytoskeletal reorganization.


## Mechanism/Details
PFTs share a common mechanism of action despite structural diversity: (1) soluble toxin monomers bind to specific receptors on the host cell membrane (e.g., cholesterol for CDCs); (2) monomers oligomerize into ring-shaped prepore complexes of variable size (up to 50 monomers for CDCs, forming pores approximately 300 A in diameter); (3) a conformational change drives membrane insertion of the pore-forming elements; (4) the mature pore allows uncontrolled ion and molecule flux across the membrane. Prelytic concentrations of PFTs trigger diverse host cell responses including p38 MAPK activation, c-Jun N-terminal pathway activation, NALP3 inflammasome induction, autophagy, reactive oxygen species suppression, SUMOylation dysregulation, endoplasmic reticulum stress, and histone tail modifications. The specific cellular outcomes depend on the toxin type, concentration, and host cell context.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Cholesterol-dependent cytolysins (CDCs) — largest PFT family; include LLO, SLO, PFO
- Alpha-hemolysin from *Staphylococcus aureus* — beta-barrel PFT forming heptameric pores
- Aerolysin from *Aeromonas hydrophila* — beta-PFT related to CDCs
- Streptolysin O (SLO) from *Streptococcus pyogenes* — model CDC for PFT research
