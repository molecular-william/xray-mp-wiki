# Low-Barrier Hydrogen Bond

## Overview
A low-barrier hydrogen bond (LBHB) is a very short, strong hydrogen bond (typically 2.3–2.5 Å donor-acceptor distance) in which the potential energy barrier for proton transfer between donor and acceptor is low enough that the proton is effectively delocalized (shared) between the two heavy atoms. LBHBs play essential roles in enzymatic catalysis and proton transport in biological macromolecules. True-atomic-resolution structures of bacteriorhodopsin (BR) at 1.05–1.53 Å resolution have experimentally demonstrated LBHBs in the proton release group (PRG) where a very short H-bond between E194 and E204 (2.4 Å) indicates delocalized proton sharing. Additional very short H-bonds (2.4 Å each) observed near S193 and P77 in the M state suggest transient proton storage at the protein surface in delocalized form. The presence of LBHBs is supported by the experimentally observed continuous infrared band in the 1,800–2,000 cm⁻¹ range.


## Mechanism/Details
A low-barrier hydrogen bond forms when the pKa values of the donor and acceptor are closely matched, creating a symmetric or nearly symmetric potential well with a low or absent energy barrier for proton transfer. In this regime, the proton is delocalized (shared) between the two electronegative atoms, rather than being localized on one or the other. Key characteristics include:

- Donor-acceptor distances of 2.3–2.5 Å (vs. ~2.7–3.2 Å for normal H-bonds), as classified by Jeffrey's scheme.
- A single-well or low-barrier potential energy surface for the proton.
- Continuous infrared absorption bands (1,800–2,000 cm⁻¹), indicative of delocalized protons and electron holes.
- Enhanced proton transfer rates compared to stepwise transfer across a higher barrier.

In bacteriorhodopsin, true-atomic-resolution structures reveal LBHBs in the PRG across multiple photocycle states:

- Ground and K states: Triple conformation of the PRG. The major conformation (~50% occupancy) features a very short H-bond between E194 and E204 (2.4 Å), indicating a delocalized proton. Two minor conformations (~25% each) show longer distances (2.9 Å), suggesting localized proton on one residue.
- L state: Single conformation with an extremely short H-bond (2.3 Å) between E194 and E204, with additional short H-bonds between R82 and E194 (2.4 Å) and between E204 and w408' (2.5 Å), suggesting the excess proton is delocalized within a short proton wire R82–E194–E204–w408' just before release.
- M state: Two very short H-bonds (2.4 Å each) near S193 and P77 at the protein surface, suggesting the released proton is transiently trapped in delocalized form at the membrane surface before appearing in the bulk (consistent with ~1 ms surface residence time).
