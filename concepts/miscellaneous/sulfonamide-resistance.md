# Sulfonamide Resistance

## Overview
Sulfonamide resistance is a major clinical problem that emerged rapidly following the introduction of sulfonamide antibiotics in the 1930s. N. meningitidis developed resistant strains within 2 years, and resistance in Plasmodium falciparum is strongly correlated with sulfonamide treatment. Sulfonamides are antimetabolites structurally similar to p-aminobenzoic acid (PABA) that competitively inhibit the folate biosynthesis pathway in bacterial pathogens. Two mechanisms of sulfonamide resistance are known: (1) target-based mutations in dihydropteroate synthase that reduce sulfonamide binding, and (2) efflux-based resistance where transporters actively export sulfonamides from the cell. The A. borkumensis [YDAH](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/slc-transporters/ydah/) transporter represents a novel efflux-mediated resistance mechanism. [YDAH](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/slc-transporters/ydah/) binds four sulfonamides with dissociation constants ranging from 0.41 uM (sulfanilamide) to 7.04 uM (sulfamethazine) and exports them from cells in a PMF-dependent manner. Mutations in conserved channel residues (D180A, N390A, W400A, P418A, R426A, D429A, N433A) render cells hypersensitive to sulfonamides, confirming the role of [YDAH](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/slc-transporters/ydah/) in resistance.
