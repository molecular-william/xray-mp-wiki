# Constitutive Active GPCR Mutations

## Overview
Constitutive active mutations (CAMs) in G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) are
mutations that increase the basal signaling activity of the receptor in the
absence of an agonist. These mutations can arise spontaneously and cause
diseases (e.g., rhodopsin mutations in retinitis pigmentosa, thyroid-stimulating
hormone receptor mutations in hyperthyroidism) or be engineered to study
receptor activation mechanisms. CAMs typically disrupt constraining interactions
that stabilize the inactive state, reposition key microswitch residues, or
stabilize the active-state conformation through new interactions such as
hydrophilic networks.


## Mechanism/Details
Constitutive activity can arise through several molecular mechanisms:

1. Disruption of the hydrophobic lock: Mutations that loosen the packing
   between transmembrane helices (e.g., TM3-TM6 interactions in class A GPCRs)
   allow TM6 to move outward more readily, creating space for G protein binding.

2. Formation of stabilizing interactions: Mutations such as I92N in the
   adenosine A2A receptor introduce new hydrogen bonds or hydrophilic
   interactions with key conserved residues (e.g., Trp6.48 of the CWxP motif)
   that stabilize the active or intermediate state.

3. Removal of constraining residues: Mutations that eliminate key salt bridges
   or van der Waals contacts that hold the receptor in the inactive state,
   such as mutations in the DRY motif or the sodium-binding pocket.

4. Alteration of microswitches: Mutations affecting the transmission switch,
   PIF motif, or CWxP motif can shift the conformational equilibrium toward
   the active state.

The I92N mutation in the adenosine A2A receptor (PDB 7ezc) exemplifies the
second mechanism: it forms a hydrophilic interaction network with Trp6.48,
Cys5.46, and Asn7.45 that is absent in the wild-type receptor, stabilizing
the intermediate state and favoring the conformational transition toward
the active state.
