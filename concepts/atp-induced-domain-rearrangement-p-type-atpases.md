# ATP-Induced Domain Rearrangement in P-type ATPases

## Overview
In P-type ATPases such as SERCA, ATP binding alone — without Ca2+ or phosphorylation — can induce substantial rearrangements of the three cytoplasmic domains (A, actuator; N, nucleotide-binding; P, phosphorylation). The 3.0 A crystal structure of [SERCA2a (Cardiac Sarco/Endoplasmic Reticulum Ca2+-ATPase)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/serca2a/) in the E2-ACP state revealed that ATP binding places the N-domain in the same position relative to the P-domain as in the E1-ACP-2Ca2+ state and rotates the A-domain to an E1-like position. The gamma-phosphate of ATP is properly delivered to the phosphorylation site (Asp351), yet phosphoryl transfer does not occur because the P-domain remains in the unbent configuration — a built-in safety mechanism that prevents nonproductive catalysis without Ca2+.


## Mechanism/Details
ATP binds to the N-domain of SERCA and crosslinks the N- and P-domains via interactions with Arg489 (alpha-phosphate) and Arg560 (beta-phosphate). This induces a deeper inclination of the N-domain relative to the P-domain, pushing the A-domain toward the membrane via contacts around Thr171. The A-domain sits on the P7 helix of the P-domain, forming a pivot for subsequent tilting motions. Key features of the ATP-induced rearrangement include: (1) the adenine ring of ATP stacks coplanarly with Phe487, (2) the triphosphate chain adopts an extended conformation stabilized by Arg489 and Arg560, and (3) a beta-turn (Ala154-Asp157) in the A-domain docks into a socket formed by Thr724-Val726 and Val705 in the P-domain. Despite correct positioning of ATP, phosphoryl transfer is prevented because the P-domain central beta-sheet remains split into two staggered halves, and Asp351 is turned away from the gamma-phosphate. The P-domain must bend (aligning the beta-sheet and rotating Asp351 toward the gamma-phosphate) for catalysis, which only occurs upon Ca2+ binding and associated conformational changes.
