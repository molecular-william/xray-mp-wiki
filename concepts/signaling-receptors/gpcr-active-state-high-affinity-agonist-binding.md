# GPCR Active State High-Affinity Agonist Binding

## Overview
A mechanistic principle explaining why G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) in the active (G protein-coupled) state have higher affinity for agonists compared to the inactive state. Structural comparison of active and inactive states of the beta1-adrenergic receptor bound to identical ligands reveals that the orthosteric binding site contracts by 24-42% upon activation, hydrogen bonds shorten, and atomic contacts between receptor and ligand increase by up to 30%.


## Mechanism/Details
The higher agonist affinity of the active state in GPCRs arises from three measurable structural changes in the orthosteric binding pocket upon receptor activation:

1. Binding site contraction: The volume of the orthosteric binding site decreases by 24-42% when comparing inactive and active states of beta1AR bound to the same ligand. The largest decrease (42%) was observed for the full agonist isoprenaline, while the partial agonists showed smaller decreases (24-34%). This contraction brings the receptor side chains closer to the bound ligand.

2. Hydrogen bond strengthening: Side chain-to-ligand distances decrease significantly in the active state. For example, Asn310(6.55) makes a weak hydrogen bond (3.5 A) to the para-hydroxyl group of isoprenaline in the inactive state, which shortens to 2.8 A in the active state. Similarly, distances to Ser215(5.46) and Ser211(5.42) decrease by 0.7-0.8 A for salbutamol and dobutamine.

3. Increased atomic contacts: The number of atomic contacts between receptor and ligand increases by up to 30% in the active state compared to the inactive state. This provides additional stabilizing interactions that contribute to higher binding affinity.

Notably, the changes in ligand-receptor contacts are not conserved across different agonists despite similarity in chemotypes. There is a weak correlation between binding site volume decrease and ligand efficacy, but no correlation between efficacy and the magnitude of affinity increase or the increase in atomic contacts.

Mutagenesis experiments show that while partial occlusion of the binding pocket entrance (mediated by residue 7.35 and ECL2 residue) affects ligand association rates, it does not fully account for the increased agonist affinity in the active state. The contraction of the entire binding pocket, rather than just occlusion of the entrance, appears to be the primary structural mechanism for high-affinity agonist binding.
