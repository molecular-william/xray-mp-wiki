# Intracellular Allosteric Antagonism at Chemokine Receptors

## Overview
Intracellular allosteric antagonism is a mechanism of GPCR inhibition where
small molecule ligands bind to an allosteric pocket on the intracellular side
of the receptor, distinct from the orthosteric (chemokine) binding site. In
chemokine receptors (CCR2, CCR7, CCR9, CXCR1, CXCR2), this pocket is located
between transmembrane helices 1, 2, 3, 6, and the turn between TM7 and helix 8
(TM7-H8 turn). The TM7-H8 turn acts as a conserved pharmacological hotspot
that binds sulfonamide, thiadiazole-dioxide, thiazole-dioxide, or
cyclobutene-dione motifs found in various chemokine receptor ligands.
Because intracellular allosteric pockets are more compact and less conserved
than orthosteric sites, they offer potential for higher subtype selectivity
in drug development.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- ccr7 — Cmp2105 binds the intracellular allosteric pocket of CCR7, stabilizing an inactive conformation. The TM7-H8 turn is a key contact point for the sulfonamide group, with residues Tyr326(7.53), Gly330(8.47), Val331(8.48), Lys332(8.49), and Phe333(8.50) forming the binding interface.
- ccr5 — Maraviroc (a CCR5 allosteric inverse agonist and HIV entry inhibitor) binds an intracellular allosteric pocket overlapping with the Gi protein binding site.
