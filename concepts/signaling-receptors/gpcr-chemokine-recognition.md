# GPCR-Chemokine Recognition (CRS1/CRS1.5/CRS2)

## Overview
Chemokine receptors (CKRs) are class A G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) that control cell migration in development, immunity, and disease. The crystal structure of CXCR4 in complex with the viral chemokine antagonist vMIP-II at 3.1 Å revealed the molecular details of chemokine:receptor recognition. The binding interface is characterized by three regions: chemokine recognition site 1 (CRS1), an intermediate region CRS1.5, and chemokine recognition site 2 (CRS2). The structure also provided insights into the specificity of CC vs CXC chemokine pairing with their respective receptors.


## Mechanism/Details
In the CXCR4:vMIP-II complex, the chemokine interacts with the receptor via a contiguous 1330 Å² interface. CRS1 involves the CXCR4 N-terminus (residues 23-SMKEP-27) packing against the chemokine N-loop and β3-strand. CRS1.5 is an intermediate region where receptor residues 27-PCFRE-31 bind to chemokine residues 8-PDKCC-12, forming an anti-parallel β-sheet. CRS2 involves the chemokine N-terminus binding deep in the receptor transmembrane pocket, making hydrogen bonds with D97(2.63), D262(6.58), and E288(7.39). The stoichiometry is 1:1 (one chemokine per receptor). The CXCR4 N-terminus adopts an orientation almost perpendicular to the membrane, differing from previous small-molecule-bound structures. The extracellular half of helix I shifts outward by ~2.4 Å to accommodate chemokine binding. CC chemokines cannot bind their receptors as dimers because their dimerization interface coincides with CRS1.5, while CXC chemokines can bind as dimers because their β1-strand dimerization interface does not overlap with receptor contacts.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Human CXCR4 Chemokine Receptor — CXCR4 bound to vMIP-II (viral CC chemokine antagonist) reveals 1:1 stoichiometry and the three-part CRS1/CRS1.5/CRS2 binding scheme. PDB ID: 4RWS. The structure explains CXCR4's remarkable ability to recognize diverse ligands including small molecules, peptides, and proteins.

- Human CXCR4 Chemokine Receptor (modeled with CXCL12) — Modeling of the CXCR4:CXCL12 complex suggests the CXC chemokine uses a bent CC motif to direct its N-terminus towards receptor helices V/VI, enabling hydrogen bonding between chemokine R8 and receptor D262(6.58). This contrasts with CC chemokine binding where the straightened CC motif directs the N-terminus along the receptor N-terminus towards helix I.
