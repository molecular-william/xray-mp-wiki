# MATE Transporter Family

## Overview


## Mechanism/Details
The multidrug and toxic compound extrusion (MATE) family consists of secondary transporters that function as electro-neutral Na+ or H+/organic cation antiporters. H+-driven MATE proteins are ubiquitously conserved across all three domains of life. MATE proteins are classified into three types by phylogenetic analysis: NorM-like (prokaryotic group D), DinF-like (prokaryotic group E), and eukaryotic. In plants, MATE proteins are involved in diverse physiological functions including antibiotic efflux, vacuolar sequestration of alkaloids and flavonoids, leaf senescence, iron homeostasis, aluminum tolerance, and regulation of local auxin biosynthesis. The crystal structure of NorM from Vibrio cholerae (Kim et al. 2010) revealed the first prokaryotic MATE structure with a bilobate 12-TM architecture and a cation-binding site centered on D371. The crystal structure of CasMATE from Camelina sativa revealed the first eukaryotic MATE structure with a negatively charged internal pocket for ligand binding.
