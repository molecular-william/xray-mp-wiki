# Allosteric Site in NSS Transporters

## Overview


## Mechanism/Details
The neurotransmitter/sodium symporter (NSS) family of transporters, which includes the serotonin transporter (SERT), dopamine transporter (DAT), and norepinephrine transporter (NET), possesses an allosteric binding site distinct from the primary substrate/inhibitor binding site (S1). The allosteric site is located at the periphery of the extracellular vestibule, interposed between extracellular loops 4 and 6 and transmembrane helices 1, 6, 10 and 11. Occupancy of the allosteric site sterically hinders ligand unbinding from the central site, thereby slowing the dissociation rate of inhibitors. In the SERT-(S)-citalopram structure, a second (S)-citalopram molecule was observed bound to the allosteric site, approximately 13 A from the central site. The malleability of the allosteric site — as evidenced by different ligands (SERT-bound (S)-citalopram, maltose detergent head group in paroxetine structure) inducing distinct conformational changes — suggests that occupancy of this site might not necessarily abrogate transport activity, opening the possibility of a spectrum of small molecules ranging from inhibiting to enhancing transport. EL6, which displays one of the highest regions of diversity among human SERT, DAT, and NET sequences, is a key determinant of the unique allosteric site architecture in SERT, consistent with the observation that allosteric ligands of SERT do not modulate DAT or NET.
