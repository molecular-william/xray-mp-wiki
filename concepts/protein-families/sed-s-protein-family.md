# SEDS Protein Family

## Overview
The Shape, Elongation, Division, and Sporulation (SEDS) proteins are a large family of ubiquitous and essential transmembrane enzymes with critical roles in bacterial cell wall biogenesis. SEDS proteins function as peptidoglycan polymerases, catalyzing the glycosyl transfer reaction that concatenates lipid II disaccharide pentapeptide headgroups onto growing peptidoglycan chains. This role was previously attributed exclusively to class A penicillin binding proteins (aPBPs). SEDS proteins are even more widely distributed than aPBPs across bacterial species and represent promising targets for next-generation antibiotic development.


## Mechanism/Details
SEDS proteins share a characteristic ten-pass transmembrane fold, first revealed by the crystal structure of [Thermus thermophilus RodA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/rod-a/) (PDB 6BAR, 2.9 A). The fold features a large central cavity with conserved polar residues and an absolutely conserved salt bridge between Glu108 and Lys111 that is essential for catalytic activity. A hydrophobic groove between transmembrane helices 2 and 3 is proposed to bind lipid-anchored substrates. SEDS proteins interact with class B penicillin binding proteins (bPBPs) through an interface at TM8 and TM9, forming complexes that coordinate glycan strand elongation (catalyzed by SEDS) with peptide crosslinking (catalyzed by bPBPs). Key SEDS family members include RodA (cell elongation), FtsW (cell division), and SpoVE (sporulation). The prototypical SEDS member RodA from Thermus thermophilus was structurally characterized using evolutionary coupling enabled [Molecular Replacement](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/structure-determination/molecular-replacement/), revealing the molecular basis for SEDS peptidoglycan polymerase activity.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [Thermus thermophilus RodA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/rod-a/) — First SEDS protein structurally characterized (PDB 6BAR, 2.9 A). Crystal structure revealed unique ten-pass transmembrane fold with central catalytic cavity. Mutagenesis confirmed catalytic role of conserved cavity residues including Glu108, Lys111, Asp255, and Asp152.
- Bacillus subtilis RodA — RodA homolog from Gram-positive B. subtilis. Homology model constructed based on T. thermophilus RodA structure. Mutational analysis in B. subtilis confirmed essentiality of central cavity residues (Q114, E117, K120, D280) and lipid binding cavity residues (I113, F118, A171).
- Escherichia coli FtsW — SEDS protein involved in cell division. Proposed to function as a lipid II flippase. Forms a complex with divisome bPBP FtsI at the TM8-TM9 interface, analogous to the RodA-bPBP interaction.
