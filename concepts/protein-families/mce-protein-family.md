# MCE Protein Family

## Overview
The MCE (mammalian cell entry) protein superfamily comprises highly conserved proteins found in double-membraned bacteria and eukaryotic chloroplasts. MCE proteins are defined by the presence of one or more conserved MCE domains and adopt a seven-stranded beta-barrel fold. They assemble into hexameric rings with central hydrophobic channels. MCE proteins form diverse architectures based on the number of tandem MCE domains: single-domain proteins like [E. coli MlaD MCE Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/structural-adhesion/mlaD/) form hexameric rings, while multi-domain proteins like [E. coli YebT Tube-like MCE Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/structural-adhesion/yebT/) (seven domains) and [E. coli PqiB Syringe-like MCE Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/structural-adhesion/pqiB/) (three domains) stack into elongated tubes and needle-and-syringe assemblies that can span the periplasmic space. The MCE family is implicated in phospholipid transport between the inner and outer membranes of Gram-negative bacteria and in maintaining outer membrane lipid asymmetry.

## Mechanism/Details
The MCE (mammalian cell entry) protein superfamily is defined by the presence of one or more conserved MCE domains. MCE proteins are ubiquitous among double-membraned bacteria (including Gram-negative bacteria and mycobacteria) and eukaryotic chloroplasts. They have no sequence similarity to other proteins of known structure or function. The core MCE domain adopts a seven-stranded beta-barrel fold. MCE proteins form hexameric assemblies with central channels, and the number of stacked rings in the assembly corresponds to the number of tandem MCE domains in the primary sequence. MCE proteins are implicated in lipid transport between the inner and outer membranes of bacteria and in the maintenance of outer membrane lipid asymmetry.
