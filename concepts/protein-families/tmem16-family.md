# TMEM16 Family (Anoctamin Protein Family)

## Overview
The TMEM16 family (also known as anoctamin family) is a group of eukaryotic membrane proteins with diverse functions. In vertebrates, the family encompasses ten members with high sequence conservation. Despite their close relationship, TMEM16 proteins combine different functions: some members are Ca2+-activated chloride channels (e.g., TMEM16A/Ano1, TMEM16B/Ano2), while others function as Ca2+-activated lipid scramblases (e.g., TMEM16F/Ano6, TMEM16C, D, G, J). The family was identified when TMEM16A was discovered as the long-sought Ca2+-activated chloride channel in 2008. All TMEM16 proteins share a common homodimeric architecture with ten transmembrane helices per subunit and a conserved intramembrane Ca2+-binding site.


## Mechanism/Details
All TMEM16 proteins share a common mode of Ca2+ activation despite their functional diversity. Ca2+ activates from the intracellular side at sub-micromolar concentrations. The Ca2+-binding site is located within the hydrophobic core of the membrane, formed by residues on alpha-helices 6, 7, and 8, coordinated by conserved acidic residues (glutamates, aspartates) and an asparagine. Two Ca2+ ions are bound, separated by approximately 4.2 A. The intramembrane location of the binding site explains the voltage-dependence of Ca2+ activation, as the ion must cross a fraction of the transmembrane electric field to reach the binding site. Each subunit contains a hydrophilic membrane-traversing cavity (subunit cavity) that is proposed to serve as the catalytic site for either ion conduction (in channels) or lipid translocation (in scramblases). The functional dichotomy between channels and scramblases may be explained by different dimeric arrangements: scramblases use a subunit-cavity-away arrangement (like [nhTMEM16 (TMEM16 Lipid Scramblase from Nectria haematococca)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/miscellaneous/nhtmem16/)), while channels may use a subunit-cavity-to-subunit-cavity arrangement forming an enclosed pore.
