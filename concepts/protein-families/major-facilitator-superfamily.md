# Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS)

## Overview
The Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS) is one of the largest and most
ancient families of secondary active transporters, ubiquitously distributed
across all kingdoms of life. MFS transporters typically consist of 12
transmembrane helices arranged in two N-terminal and C-terminal domains of 6
helices each, forming a central cavity for substrate transport. They catalyze
the transport of a wide variety of substrates — including sugars, drugs,
amino acids, ions, and lipids — via uniport, symport, or antiport mechanisms
driven by electrochemical gradients. In bacteria, MFS transporters are mainly
used for nutrient uptake and extrusion of deleterious compounds. In humans,
they include important drug transporters such as glucose transporters
(GLUTs) and multidrug resistance proteins.


## Mechanism/Details
MFS transporters operate through an alternating access mechanism in which
the protein alternates between inward- and outward-facing conformations.
The N-terminal and C-terminal domains undergo a rocker-switch type motion
around a central substrate-binding site, alternately exposing the cavity to
either side of the membrane. Substrate binding on one side induces a
conformational change that occludes the cavity, followed by opening to the
opposite side for substrate release. This transport cycle can be coupled to
ion gradients (proton or sodium) for secondary active transport.

The MFS fold is characterized by the MFS motif (G(X)8G(X)3GP(X)2GG) found
in the C-terminal domain, which contributes to the conformational switching.
Despite the diversity of substrates transported by MFS members, the core
structural architecture and transport mechanism are highly conserved. The
S. aureus LtaA flippase (PDB 6S7V) is an MFS transporter that catalyzes
proton-coupled flipping of a lipid-linked disaccharide, demonstrating that
the MFS fold can be adapted for transporting amphiphilic lipid substrates
across the membrane.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [GlpT](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/mfs-transporters/glpT/) — E. coli GlpT is the glycerol-3-phosphate/Pi antiporter whose 3.3 A crystal structure (PDB 1PW4) provided the first high-resolution structural paradigm for MFS transporters. The structure revealed the 12-helix pseudo two-fold architecture, a central substrate-binding site with two arginines (Arg45 and Arg269), and the inward-facing (Ci) conformation. GlpT operates by a single-binding site alternating-access mechanism via a rocker-switch type of domain movement.
- [LtaA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/mfs-transporters/ltaa/) — S. aureus LtaA is an MFS transporter that functions as a proton-coupled flippase for the lipid-linked disaccharide anchor-LLD, essential for lipoteichoic acid biogenesis. Its outward-open structure (PDB 6S7V, 3.3 A) reveals an amphiphilic cavity with distinct hydrophilic and hydrophobic pockets adapted for lipid substrate recognition.
