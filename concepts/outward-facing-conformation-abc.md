# Outward-Facing Conformation in ABC Transporters

## Overview
The outward-facing conformation is a structural state of ABC (ATP-Binding Cassette) transporters in which the translocation pathway is open to the extracellular or periplasmic side of the membrane and closed to the cytoplasm. This state is coupled to ATP binding at the nucleotide-binding domains (NBDs) and is responsible for drug or substrate release.

## Mechanism/Details
In multidrug ABC exporters, the alternating access mechanism involves at least two distinct conformations. In the ATP-bound outward-facing state, the transmembrane domains (TMDs) form a polar cavity open to the extracellular medium, with low affinity for substrate. ATP hydrolysis and release of ADP and Pi switches the transporter to an inward-facing conformation with a high-affinity binding site accessible from the cytoplasm. The Sav1866 structure with AMP-PNP confirmed that ATP binding to the NBDs induces an outward-facing TMD conformation through coupling helices at the NBD-TMD interface.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Sav1866 (Staphylococcus aureus multidrug ABC transporter) — Structure with AMP-PNP (PDB 2ONJ, 3.4 Å) directly confirmed ATP-bound outward-facing conformation. Nearly identical to ADP-bound structure (PDB 2HYD, RMSD 0.097 Å), suggesting ADP alone stabilizes this state in detergent.
- Human Mdr1/P-glycoprotein — Homolog of Sav1866. The outward-facing conformation exposes a polar cavity for drug release; sequence and architectural similarity suggests the same mechanism applies.
