# Channelrhodopsin Photocycle

## Overview
The channelrhodopsin photocycle is a sequence of light-induced conformational
and protonation states that channelrhodopsins pass through upon illumination.
Light absorption triggers all-trans to 13-cis isomerization of the retinal
chromophore, initiating a cascade of structural changes that lead to ion pore
opening and cation conductance. Key intermediates include P1(520) with
isomerized retinal, P2(390) with deprotonated Schiff base, and P3(520) with
reprotonated Schiff base. The DC-pair (Asp-Cys) in the retinal binding pocket
is critical for photocycle transitions and ion conductance. The 2.4 A crystal
structure of ChR2 revealed the structural basis for the photocycle, showing
how the retinal Schiff base controls and synchronizes three gates (ECG, CG,
ICG) separating four cavities (EC1, EC2, IC1, IC2) through extended
hydrogen-bonding networks.


## Mechanism/Details
Upon photon absorption, the retinal chromophore isomerizes from all-trans to
13-cis configuration. This triggers a series of spectroscopically distinct
intermediates: P1(520) (early intermediate with isomerized retinal),
P2(390) (deprotonated Schiff base state where the proton is transferred to
the acceptor D253), and P3(520) (reprotonated Schiff base state stabilized
by deprotonated D156 in the DC gate). The DC gate, comprising C128 and D156
connected via a water-mediated hydrogen bond, controls the open-channel
lifetime by influencing the reprotonation kinetics of the Schiff base.

The structures reveal that the Schiff base is hydrogen-bonded to E123 and
D253, forming the central gate (CG). The CG is connected to the extracellular
gate (ECG, centered on R120) and the intracellular gate (ICG, centered on
R268) through extended H-bond networks. Opening requires synchronized
disruption of all three gates. Residues E90, K93, E97, and E101 on helix 2
form a stabilizing H-bond network; upon retinal isomerization, this network
is broken, leading to helix 2 reorientation and gate opening.

The C128T mutant, with a prolonged open-state lifetime, replaces the
water-mediated C128-D156 bond with a direct hydrogen bond, altering the
Schiff base environment. This mutation increases the distance between T127
and T128 (0.7 A longer than T127-C128), lengthens the E123-Schiff base
bond (2.6 to 3.1 A), and introduces an additional water molecule (w2").
These changes affect the stabilization of the deprotonated Schiff base
intermediates, controlling gating kinetics.
