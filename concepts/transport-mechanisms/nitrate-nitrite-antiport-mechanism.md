# Nitrate/Nitrite Antiport Mechanism

## Overview
The nitrate/nitrite antiport mechanism is the transport mechanism used by NNP family transporters to exchange nitrate for nitrite across biological membranes. The mechanism couples substrate recognition to the transport cycle through concomitant movement of transmembrane helices and key residues (Y263, R305) in the substrate-binding site. Conserved Gly residues in TM7, TM10, and TM11 enable helix bending required for the alternating access cycle. The mechanism is tightly coupled, requiring counteranion presence for transport.

## Mechanism/Details
The nitrate/nitrite antiport mechanism is the transport mechanism employed by NNP (nitrate/nitrite porter) family transporters. In this mechanism, the transporter couples nitrate uptake with nitrite extrusion (or vice versa) across the membrane. The mechanism involves: (1) substrate recognition in a conserved binding site formed by aromatic residues (F49, F147, F267) forming pi-pi stacking with nitrate, and arginine residues (R89, R305) forming hydrogen bonds and salt bridges with nitrate oxygen atoms; (2) conformational changes involving bending of TM helices (TM7, TM10, TM11) at conserved Gly residues (G268, G363-367, G408, G414, G417-418); (3) alternating access between inward-open, occluded, and outward-open states; (4) coupling of substrate binding/release to conformational changes via key residues Y263 and R305 that bridge the N and C bundles. The negative charge of nitrate relaxes electrostatic repulsion between R89 and R305 in the occluded state, enabling transport. MD simulations show the apo occluded state spontaneously transitions toward inward-open within 10 ns when nitrate is absent, confirming tight coupling between substrate binding and conformational change.
