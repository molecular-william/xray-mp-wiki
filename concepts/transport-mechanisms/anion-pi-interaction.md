# Anion-&#x3C0; Interaction in Ion Transport

## Overview
Anion-&#x3C0; interactions are non-covalent interactions between an anion
(negatively charged ion) and the &#x3C0;-electron system of an aromatic or
conjugated molecular group. In the context of membrane protein ion
transport, an anion-&#x3C0; interaction between a transported chloride ion
and the conjugated &#x3C0;-system of the retinal chromophore was identified
in the light-driven chloride pump NmHR (CIR) from Nonlabens marinus.
This interaction supports transient chloride ion binding across a major
bottleneck in the transport pathway, enabling the anion to pass over the
retinal chromophore during the photocycle.

## Mechanism/Details
The anion-&#x3C0; interaction in NmHR involves the transient Cl352-binding
site at 1 us after photoactivation. The chloride ion is stabilized by: (1)
electrostatic interaction with the protonated Schiff base (PSB) of retinal
(3.1 A to N&#x3B6;), (2) interaction with the &#x3C0;-system of the retinal
chromophore (3.1 A to C14 and C15), and (3) a newly adopted rotamer of
Thr102 (2.5 A to O&#x3B3;). Quantum-chemical calculations show that the
stabilization is dominated by the electrostatic component between chloride
and PSB, with an additional contribution from the C14-C15=N fragment of
the retinal. The negative charge of the anion polarizes the &#x3C0;-electron
density of the chromophore, resembling the common interaction between
anions and &#x3C0; electrons of aromatic rings. The polarization effect is
evident from electrostatic potential maps showing a negative potential
extending up to the &#x3B2;-ionone ring of the retinal. In the presence of
the chloride in the Cl352-binding site, the computed QM/MM excitation
energy red shift is 4 nm (versus 164 nm without chloride), demonstrating
the specific electronic effect of the anion-&#x3C0; interaction on retinal
spectroscopy. This interaction allows the chloride to traverse the retinal
bottleneck, which is a major barrier in the ion transport pathway. In
contrast, bacteriorhodopsin and KR2 (sodium pump) use deprotonation of the
Schiff base to overcome this bottleneck, demonstrating that rhodopsin
pumps with different substrate affinities adopt diverse strategies for ion
transport across the retinal.
