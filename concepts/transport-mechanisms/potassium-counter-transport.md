# Potassium Counter-Transport in P-type ATPases

## Overview
Counter-transport (antiport) of K⁺ ions is a functional hallmark of certain P-type ATPases. Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase counter-transports K⁺ ions in exchange for Na⁺ ions (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP cycle), while the homologous Ca²⁺-ATPase (SERCA) counter-transports H⁺ rather than K⁺. The crystal structure of Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase at 2.4 Å resolution (Shinoda et al., 2009) revealed why these homologous pumps have different counter-transport specificity.

## Mechanism/Details
The K⁺ binding sites in Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase consist of two transmembrane sites (I and II) formed by residues on M4, M5, M6, and M8. Key features enabling K⁺ selectivity include: (1) Use of a main-chain carbonyl (Val785 on M5) for coordinating K⁺ at site I, rather than a carboxyl side chain — this is critical for defining ion selectivity. (2) Pro785 on M5 causes unwinding of the M5 helix, enabling the main-chain carbonyl to face the binding cavity. (3) The β-subunit provides an additional main-chain carbonyl (Gly855) to coordinate K⁺ at site II — Ca²⁺-ATPase lacks a β-subunit and cannot provide this ligand. (4) The binding cavity is highly confined, with the two K⁺ ions only 4.6 Å apart, sharing two side-chain oxygen atoms (Asp811 and Asn783) but with no bridging carboxyl group (in contrast to Ca²⁺-ATPase where Asp800 bridges the two Ca²⁺ ions). (5) A cholesterol molecule shields the unwound part of M7 from the bulk lipid and may stabilize the K⁺ binding conformation. These features explain why Ca²⁺-ATPase — which has almost identical coordinating residues except for Asn783 — cannot bind K⁺ and instead counter-transports H⁺.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Na⁺,K⁺-ATPase (shark) — Counter-transports 2 K⁺ per ATP cycle. K⁺ binding sites revealed at 2.4 Å resolution (PDB 2ZXE).
- Ca²⁺-ATPase (SERCA1a) — Counter-transports H⁺ instead of K⁺ despite nearly identical coordinating residues. Lacks β-subunit and Pro785 to enable K⁺ coordination.
