# Cardiolipin Transport in Gram-Negative Bacteria

## Overview
[Cardiolipin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/cardiolipin/) (CL) transport in Gram-negative bacteria involves the movement of the
anionic phospholipid [Cardiolipin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/cardiolipin/) from the inner membrane (IM), where it is
synthesized, to the outer membrane (OM), where it contributes to OM biogenesis and
pathogenesis. The inner membrane protein [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) (YejM) has been identified as a CL
transporter, regulated by the PhoPQ two-component system. The crystal structures of
the soluble periplasmic domain of [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) from Salmonella typhimurium and Escherichia
coli revealed a hydrophobic binding pocket between the second and third structural
layers that accommodates CL, with a flexible loop region acting as a conformational
lid that opens to allow CL binding and release. The full-length [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) structure (Fan
et al., 2020, mBio) at 2.7 A resolution with bound CL revealed two CL-binding sites,
a long deep cleft spanning the membrane and periplasmic domains, and a putative
catalytic dyad (D104-H114) in the DUF3413 family TM domain.


## Mechanism/Details
The [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) periplasmic globular domain (residues 191-586) adopts a fold resembling
arylsulfatases and lipoteichoic acid synthases, but lacks their enzymatic activities.
The domain consists of three structural layers: a beta-sheet core (first layer),
a mixed alpha/beta region (second layer), and an alpha-helical bundle (third layer). CL binding
occurs in a hydrophobic pocket located between layers 2 and 3, lined by key aromatic
residues including F292, F349, F362, and F519.

A flexible loop spanning residues D347-Q370, located between helices alpha4 and alpha6,
exhibits high B-factors and acts as a conformational lid. In the EcPbgA structure,
this loop closes over the hydrophobic pocket (with residues P365-R368 forming beta-strand 4),
while in the StPbgA structure, this region is disordered and away from the pocket,
representing the open conformation. The transition between open and closed states
likely controls CL access to the binding pocket. A double cysteine mutant S251C/D303C,
designed to lock the lid in a closed conformation via disulfide bond formation, impairs
cell growth, supporting the functional importance of this lid.

The full-length structure reveals the complete CL transport architecture. CL binds at
two sites: site 1 in the TM domain (residues R50, W43, L47, I51, Y54 at TM1-TM2 junction)
and site 2 at the membrane-periplasm interface (residues I189, W190, Y210, M212, R215,
R216, F217, L218 in the linker region alpha2-alpha3 loop). Site 2 is the physiologically
relevant site. A long deep cleft extends from the TM domain into the periplasmic domain,
allowing CL to traverse from the membrane into the periplasmic space. The C-terminal
residues 581-584 fill the deepest part of the cleft at the TM-periplasmic interface.

This transport mechanism differs from the UPS1/Mdm35-mediated phospholipid transfer
in mitochondria, which uses a concaved 7-antiparallel beta-sheet to wrap phosphatidic
acid molecules. The [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) structure does not form a similar cavity between its first
and second layers, indicating a distinct transport mechanism.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) — [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) from S. typhimurium and E. coli is the first structurally characterized
bacterial protein involved in phospholipid transport from the IM to the OM.
Its structure revealed the hydrophobic CL binding pocket and conformational
lid mechanism. The full-length structure (PDB 6V8Q) shows two CL-binding sites
and a deep cleft for CL translocation.
