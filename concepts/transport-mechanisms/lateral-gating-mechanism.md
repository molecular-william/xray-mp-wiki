# Lateral Gating Mechanism in Rhomboid Proteases

## Overview
The lateral gating mechanism is a feature of rhomboid intramembrane proteases that controls substrate access to the membrane-embedded active site. First revealed by the crystal structure of E. coli GlpG (Wang et al., 2006), the mechanism involves a V-shaped lateral opening between transmembrane segments S1 and S3 that faces the lipid bilayer, providing a route for transmembrane substrate proteins to enter the active site. This opening is blocked by the membrane-embedded loop L1, which functions as a dynamic gate. The lateral gating model is fundamental to understanding how intramembrane proteolysis occurs within the hydrophobic environment of the lipid bilayer.

## Mechanism/Details
The rhomboid protease GlpG has a V-shaped opening between transmembrane helices S1 and S3 that faces laterally towards the lipid bilayer. This opening is wide enough to accommodate an alpha-helix and is the likely route by which transmembrane substrate proteins enter the active site. The lower portion of the opening is shallow and hydrophobic, permitting favorable interactions with hydrophobic substrates while posing no major restrictions on sequence. The upper portion connects to the hydrophilic cavity containing the catalytic Ser201/His254 dyad. In the resting (closed) state, the membrane-embedded loop L1 blocks the lateral opening. L1 has an amphiphilic characteristic: its upper and inward-facing surfaces are polar while its lower surfaces are hydrophobic, consistent with its peripheral membrane location. This amphiphilic property enables L1 to function at the membrane-water interface without causing denaturation or aggregation. The WR motif (Trp136/Arg137) at the membrane-water interface of L1 modulates gate dynamics. L1 contains a stretch of four consecutive 3_10 helices, an unusual feature suggesting a metastable, dynamic structure consistent with a gating function. Upon substrate binding, L1 is proposed to change conformation, opening the lateral gate and allowing the substrate transmembrane segment to bend and enter the active site. An alternative gating model was proposed by Wu et al. (NSMB 2006, PDB 2NRF), in which helix alpha5 serves as the gating element. In this model, observed in one of two GlpG molecules in the asymmetric unit, helix alpha5 bends away from the active site to create a large cleft between helices alpha2 and alpha5 that accommodates substrate entry. The L5 loop connecting helices alpha5 and alpha6 regulates water access to the active site, acting as a cap with open, flexible, and closed states. The alpha5 helix is one of the least conserved helices in the rhomboid family, supporting its role as a mobile gating element that can move without requiring global conformational changes.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- GlpG (E. coli rhomboid protease) — The L1 loop between TM1 and TM2 forms the lateral gate, blocking the V-shaped opening between S1 and S3. Contains the conserved WR motif (Trp136/Arg137). Mutation of these residues abolishes or reduces activity, supporting their role in gate dynamics.
