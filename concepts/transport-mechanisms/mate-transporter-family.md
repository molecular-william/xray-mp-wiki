# MATE Transporter Family

## Overview


## Mechanism/Details
The multidrug and toxic compound extrusion (MATE) family consists of secondary transporters that function as electro-neutral Na+ or H+/organic cation antiporters. H+-driven MATE proteins are ubiquitously conserved across all three domains of life. Comprehensive phylogenetic analysis has expanded the MATE family classification from three subfamilies (NorM, DinF, eMATE) to six major clades: eMATE-1, eMATE-2, NorM, DinF, aMATE-1 (archaeal MATE-1), and aMATE-2. The aMATE-1 subfamily, represented by [Aq_128 (Aquifex aeolicus MATE Family Multidrug Resistance Transporter) — aMATE-1 Subfamily Prototype](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/aq_128/) from Aquifex aeolicus, comprises transporters from epsilon-Proteobacteria and Archaea that lack conserved acidic residues essential for transport in other subfamilies. In plants, MATE proteins are involved in diverse physiological functions including antibiotic efflux, vacuolar sequestration of alkaloids and flavonoids, leaf senescence, [IRON](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/additives/iron/) homeostasis, aluminum tolerance, and regulation of local auxin biosynthesis. The crystal structure of NorM from Vibrio cholerae (Kim et al. 2010) revealed the first prokaryotic MATE structure with a bilobate 12-TM architecture and a cation-binding site centered on D371. The crystal structure of [CasMATE (Camelina sativa MATE Transporter)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/casmate/) from Camelina sativa revealed the first eukaryotic MATE structure with a negatively charged internal pocket for ligand binding. The crystal structures of [PFMATE](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/pfmate/) from Pyrococcus furiosus (at 2.1-3.0 A resolution) captured the transporter in both straight (outward-open) and bent conformations, revealing the rocker-switch mechanism driven by TM1 bending at Pro26. [PFMATE](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/pfmate/) functions as an H+-driven drug antiporter and its structures with macrocyclic peptides (MaD5, MaD3S, MaL6) demonstrated how the drug-binding pocket can be targeted for inhibition. [CLBM](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/clbm/), a MATE family transporter from E. coli, exports the genotoxin colibactin and shows a larger binding pocket compared to [PFMATE](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/pfmate/) and [NorM-VC (Vibrio cholerae NorM) - MATE Family Multidrug Efflux Transporter](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/norM-vc/), indicative of its ability to transport a large precolibactin molecule. The crystal structure of [Aq_128 (Aquifex aeolicus MATE Family Multidrug Resistance Transporter) — aMATE-1 Subfamily Prototype](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/aq_128/) (PDB 6Z70, 2.0 A), the aMATE-1 subfamily prototype, revealed an Na+-dependent drug transporter with substrate and cation binding sites located in the N bundle rather than the C bundle. [Aq_128 (Aquifex aeolicus MATE Family Multidrug Resistance Transporter) — aMATE-1 Subfamily Prototype](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/aq_128/) lacks the conserved aspartate residue (replaced by Ser40) that drives the proposed protonation-dependent TM1 bending mechanism in DinF transporters, and TM1 remains straight across pH 5-8, suggesting a distinct transport mechanism.
