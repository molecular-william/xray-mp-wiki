# Fluc Family of Fluoride Ion Channels

## Overview
The Fluc family comprises F- specific ion channels that protect prokaryotes and lower eukaryotes from environmental fluoride toxicity. Fluc channels are built as dual-topology homodimers of small ~120-130 residue subunits (4 transmembrane helices each) arranged in antiparallel transmembrane orientation. They display a unique double-barreled architecture with two separate ion-conduction pathways and employ a conserved Phe-box motif for F- coordination.


## Mechanism/Details
Fluc channels function as electrodiffusive F-specific ion channels. Each channel complex contains two separate pores running along the sides of the dimer, each built by residues from both subunits. F- permeation occurs along a 'polar track' of dipolar H-bond donors, with F- ions coordinated by conserved phenylalanine residues (Phe80, Phe83 in Ec2) through edge-on aromatic-anion interactions (the 'Phe-box' motif). The two pores are well-separated with no ion transfer between them. Monobody proteins bind to the aqueous vestibules and occlude both pores simultaneously. The dual-topology, antiparallel assembly ensures that the two pores adopt opposite orientations in the membrane.

Fluc channels are phylogenetically distinct from the CLC superfamily of F-/H+ antiporters, which also export F- but use energy from the proton gradient. The discovery of Fluc channels was driven by the finding of F-specific riboswitches in bacterial genomes, revealing a widespread microbial physiology of resistance to environmental F- toxicity.
