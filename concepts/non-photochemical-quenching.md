# Non-photochemical Quenching (NPQ) in LHC-II

## Overview
Non-photochemical quenching (NPQ) is a photoprotective mechanism in plants that dissipates excess excitation energy as heat, preventing damage from high-light conditions. The major light-harvesting complex II (LHC-II) plays a central role in NPQ by providing structural pathways for energy dissipation. A structure-based NPQ model was proposed from the 2.72 A X-ray crystal structure of spinach LHC-II, revealing how DGDG-mediated trimer aggregation, xanthophyll-cycle carotenoids, and specific chlorophyll pairs work together to quench excess excitation energy.


## Mechanism/Details
The structure-based NPQ model for LHC-II involves multiple components. Upon aggregation of LHC-II trimers mediated by digalactosyl diacylglycerol (DGDG), efficient non-photochemical energy-transfer pathways are established so that excitation energy can escape from one trimer to adjacent trimers. The energy migrates to trapping sites where non-radiative dissipation occurs. Key trapping sites include: (1) the xanthophyll-cycle carotenoid at the monomer-monomer interface, which accepts singlet energy from at least three nearby chlorophylls; (2) the Chl a 613-Chl a 614 pair at the trimer-trimer interface, which has a centre-to-centre distance below 10 A and mutual orientation resembling an irregular distribution, consistent with a statistical pair energy trap; and (3) conformational changes triggered by protonation of lumen-exposed acidic residues (Glu 94, Asp 111, Glu 207, Asp 211) under low lumenal pH conditions, which move and reorient linker chlorophylls (Chl a 614 and Chl b 605) at the trimer-trimer interface to promote quenching. The xanthophyll cycle converts violaxanthin to zeaxanthin through antheraxanthin, with zeaxanthin acting as a direct quencher of excess excitation by accepting singlet energy from chlorophyll.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Spinach LHC-II (2.72 A structure): DGDG-mediated trimer aggregation
  establishes energy-transfer pathways; xanthophyll-cycle carotenoid and Chl a
  613-Chl a 614 pair serve as quenching sites.
- Pea LHC-II (2.5 A structure): Violaxanthin in its binding site can be replaced
  by zeaxanthin via the xanthophyll cycle; zeaxanthin accepts energy from nearby
  chlorophylls and dissipates it as heat.
- LHC-II in liposomes containing DGDG: NPQ observed upon incorporation,
  consistent with DGDG-mediated trimer aggregation model.
