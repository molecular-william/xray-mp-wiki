# Gas-Conducting Aquaporins

## Overview
Gas-conducting aquaporins are a proposed subclass of the aquaporin superfamily
that, in addition to water, facilitate the passive movement of small gas
molecules such as CO₂, H₂S, or NH₃ across cell membranes. The concept was
first structurally grounded by the 1.68-Å crystal structure of AqpM from
the archaeon Methanothermobacter marburgensis, which revealed a unique
selectivity filter with isoleucine (Ile187) replacing the highly conserved
histidine residue found in water-selective aquaporins. This substitution
makes the channel wider (2.54 Å at the narrowest point, vs. 1.86 Å in Aqp1)
and more hydrophobic, suggesting adaptation for conducting larger, less polar
permeants. M. marburgensis uses CO₂ as its sole carbon source and H₂S as a
terminal electron acceptor, making these gas molecules biologically relevant
candidate permeants. Gas-conducting aquaporins may represent a distinct
subdivision of the aquaporin family, alongside water-selective aquaporins
and glycerol-conducting aquaglyceroporins.



## Mechanism/Details
Gas molecules cross cell membranes primarily by passive diffusion through the
lipid bilayer, but aquaporins can provide facilitated diffusion pathways with
higher conductance and selectivity control. The mechanism of gas conductance
through aquaporins differs fundamentally from water conductance:

1. Gas molecules such as CO₂ or H₂S are non-polar or weakly polar, so they
   do not require the same hydrogen-bond coordination network as water.
2. The classical aquaporin proton exclusion mechanism (bipolar water
   orientation established by NPA motifs and helix dipoles) may not apply
   to non-polar gas permeants.
3. The selectivity filter dimensions and hydrophobicity are the primary
   determinants of gas conductance: the channel must be wide enough
   (≥2.5 Å) and sufficiently hydrophobic to allow gas molecules to pass
   without being trapped by polar interactions.
4. Unlike water transport which involves single-file hydrogen-bonded chains,
   gas molecules may traverse the channel more rapidly without forming
   extended hydrogen-bond networks.

In AqpM, the substitution of the polar His with hydrophobic Ile at the
selectivity filter creates a wider (2.54 Å) and more hydrophobic constriction
than the archetypal water-selective aquaporins. The presence of three ordered
water molecules at the selectivity filter in both high- and low-resolution
structures of AqpM suggests that water and gas permeation may coexist.

Open questions include whether gas conductance is regulated by gating
mechanisms, whether water and gas compete for the same pore, and whether
the gas conductance has physiological significance in organisms beyond
archaea. The structural similarity of H₂S to H₂O (similar molecular size,
polarity) makes selectivity between these two molecules particularly
challenging, and AqpM's intermediate filter dimensions may represent a
compromise for conducting both types of permeant.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [AqpM](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/other-ion-channels/aqpm-methanothermobacter-marburgensis/) — 1.68-Å structure of the first archaeal aquaporin; Ile187 substitution creates a wider, more hydrophobic selectivity filter (2.54 Å); proposed to conduct H₂S or CO₂ in addition to water
- [Rh protein (Ammonium Transporter)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/other-ion-channels/rh-protein-nitrosomonas-europaeae/) — Rh proteins are structural homologs of aquaporins that conduct NH₃, demonstrating that the aquaporin fold can be adapted for gas transport
