# Steric Trigger Mechanism in Rhodopsin Signaling

## Overview
The steric trigger mechanism describes how a specific hydrogen-bonded threonine-tyrosine pair in the retinal-binding pocket of sensory rhodopsins creates a steric conflict with the retinal C14 group during photoisomerization, which is essential for initiating signal transduction to the cognate transducer protein. This mechanism was first elucidated in sensory rhodopsin II (SRII) and demonstrated to be functionally sufficient by the A215T mutation in bacteriorhodopsin, which converts the proton pump into a phototaxis sensor.

## Mechanism/Details
In sensory rhodopsin II (SRII) and the signaling-competent BR_A215T mutant, a conserved threonine residue (Thr204 in SRII, Thr215 in BR_A215T) forms a hydrogen bond with a neighboring tyrosine (Tyr174 in SRII, Tyr185 in BR_A215T). Upon photoisomerization of the all-trans retinal to 13-cis, the threonine side chain creates a steric conflict with the C14 methyl group of retinal. This steric clash is transmitted through the threonine-tyrosine hydrogen-bonded pair to the protein surface, ultimately altering the conformation of the transducer-interacting interface. Disruption of either residue (Tyr174Phe or Thr204Ala) in SRII abolishes signaling and eliminates the steric conflict. In BR_A215T, the introduced threonine assumes a nearly identical position relative to the retinal as Thr204 in SRII, demonstrating that the steric trigger alone — without the Arg72 flip found in SRII — is sufficient for phototaxis signaling. The tyrosine's role is auxiliary: it maintains the threonine in the proper orientation for the steric trigger to occur.
