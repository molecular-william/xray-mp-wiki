# PilZ Domain (c-di-GMP Binding Domain)

## Overview

The PilZ domain is a conserved protein domain that binds the bacterial second messenger bis-(3'-5')-cyclic diguanylic acid (c-di-GMP). PilZ domains are found in diverse bacterial proteins and mediate the effects of c-di-GMP signaling on processes such as biofilm formation, virulence, motility, and cellulose synthesis. The PilZ domain typically folds into a six-stranded beta-barrel structure that accommodates the c-di-GMP molecule.

## Mechanism/Details

The PilZ domain is localized within the C-terminal intracellular domain of certain cellulose synthases, including BcsA from Rhodobacter sphaeroides. In BcsA, the PilZ domain is positioned right next to the GT-A fold glycosyltransferase domain. The C-terminus of BcsA extends from the last transmembrane helix (TM8) via a short beta-strand that forms a two-stranded beta-sheet with the IF3-TM7 loop, before folding into the six-stranded beta-barrel of the PilZ domain (amino acids 585-675). The beta-barrel points away from the GT domain at approximately 90 degrees.

Binding of c-di-GMP to the PilZ domain strongly stimulates the enzymatic activity of BcsA, linking cellulose production to biofilm formation. Bacterial cells sense and regulate c-di-GMP levels through diguanylate cyclases (DGCs) and phosphodiesterases (PDEs), allowing dynamic control of cellulose synthesis in response to environmental conditions.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work

- **BcsA from Rhodobacter sphaeroides** — The PilZ domain of BcsA binds c-di-GMP to regulate cellulose synthase activity. The domain forms a six-stranded beta-barrel (residues 585-675) positioned at approximately 90 degrees from the GT-A fold domain. c-di-GMP binding strongly stimulates cellulose synthesis activity of the purified BcsA-BcsB complex.
