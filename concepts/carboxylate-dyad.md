# Carboxylate Dyad in Oligosaccharyltransferase

## Overview
The carboxylate dyad is a catalytic motif in oligosaccharyltransferase (OST) enzymes consisting of two conserved acidic residues (Asp and Glu) that coordinate a divalent metal ion and together activate the carboxamide group of the acceptor Asn side chain for nucleophilic attack on the lipid-linked oligosaccharide donor. In archaeal [A. fulgidus AglB-L Oligosaccharyltransferase](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/aglB/), the dyad comprises Asp47 (in the GND motif of external loop 1) and Glu360 (in the TIAE motif of external loop 5). In eubacterial PglB, the equivalent residues are Asp56 and Glu319. The dyad twists the planar carboxamide group of Asn into a tetrahedral geometry, enabling the amide nitrogen lone pair to attack the C1 carbon of the reducing-end sugar.

## Mechanism/Details
The carboxylate dyad mechanism operates through a bipartite interaction: the side-chain carboxylate groups of both acidic residues simultaneously contact the carboxamide group of the acceptor Asn side chain while coordinating a divalent metal ion (Mg2+ or Mn2+). This triple interaction twists the planar carboxamide into tetrahedral geometry, activating the amide nitrogen for nucleophilic attack. The metal ion is not directly catalytic but is structurally essential for proper dyad formation. [EDTA](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/additives/edta/) chelation abolishes activity by removing the metal ion. Single alanine mutations (D47A or E360A in [AFAGLB](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/afaglb/)) retain reduced but significant activity in the cross-linked complex, demonstrating that either carboxylate group can partially compensate. The double mutation (D47A/E360A) completely abolishes activity, confirming both residues are essential for catalysis. The dyad is conserved across all domains of life, indicating a universal mechanism for N-linked glycosylation.
