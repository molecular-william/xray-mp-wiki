# Dual-Mode Inhibition of ABC Transporters

## Overview
Dual-mode inhibition refers to a mechanism where a single small-molecule inhibitor simultaneously blocks transporter function through two distinct allosteric mechanisms. This was first observed in the ABC transporter [MSBA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/msba/), where quinoline-based inhibitors (G907, G247, G592) exhibit two modes of antagonism: (1) trapping [MSBA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/msba/) in an inward-facing, LPS-bound conformation by wedging into a conserved transmembrane pocket, preventing transition to the outward-facing state; and (2) structural and functional uncoupling of the nucleotide-binding domains (NBDs), inducing asymmetric NBD conformation not previously seen in ABC transporter structures.


## Mechanism/Details
Mode 1 (Transmembrane trapping): The quinoline inhibitor binds to a conserved allosteric pocket in the transmembrane domain of [MSBA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/msba/), wedging between transmembrane helices. This stabilizes the inward-facing conformation and prevents the conformational changes needed for LPS transport to the periplasm.
Mode 2 (NBD uncoupling): Upon inhibitor binding, the two NBDs of the [MSBA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/msba/) homodimer adopt an asymmetric arrangement, with one NBD more closed and the other more open relative to the canonical ATP-bound dimer. This asymmetry uncouples ATP binding/hydrolysis from the transport cycle, halting LPS flipping.
