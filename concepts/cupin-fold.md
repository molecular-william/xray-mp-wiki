# Cupin Fold

## Overview
The cupin fold is a structural motif found in a large superfamily of proteins characterized by a beta-barrel fold composed of two antiparallel beta-sheet domains. Cupin proteins are predominantly oligomeric bacterial enzymes that typically bind metal ion cofactors at their catalytic sites. The cupin fold has also been identified as the structural scaffold of the human sigma-1 receptor ligand-binding domain, representing an unusual repurposing of this ancient fold for ligand recognition rather than enzyme catalysis.


## Mechanism/Details
The cupin fold consists of two structurally similar beta-barrel domains arranged in a sandwich-like configuration. In canonical cupin enzymes, the active site is located at the interface between the two domains and typically coordinates metal ions such as manganese, iron, or zinc. DALI structural comparisons of the sigma-1 receptor ligand-binding domain reveal significant similarity to cupin fold proteins (Z-scores 8.2-10.8), including structures with functions ranging from ethanolamine breakdown to hydroxynitrile lyase activity. However, the sigma-1 receptor repurposes this fold by burying its ligand-binding site at the center of the beta-barrel, creating a hydrophobic cavity occluded from solvent rather than a metal-coordinating catalytic site. This is the first reported example of a cupin-like fold functioning as a ligand-binding domain in a human transmembrane receptor.
