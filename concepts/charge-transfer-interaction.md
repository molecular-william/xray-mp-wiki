# Charge-Transfer Interaction

## Overview
A charge-transfer interaction is a non-covalent interaction between an electron donor (such as a cysteine thiolate) and an electron acceptor (such as a quinone ring), resulting in partial charge separation and characteristic color changes. In the DsbB enzyme from Escherichia coli, a charge-transfer interaction between the Cys44 thiolate and ubiquinone Q8 is a key intermediate in the de novo disulfide bond formation mechanism. This interaction produces a characteristic pink color (absorbance maximum at 500 nm) and precedes the nucleophilic attack of Cys44 on the quinone ring. An analogous charge-transfer interaction is found in glutathione reductase (cysteine thiolate to FAD) and dihydrolipoyl dehydrogenase, suggesting a conserved mechanistic principle across different redox enzymes.


## Mechanism/Details
The charge-transfer interaction in DsbB involves the unpaired Cys44 thiolate in the active site and the planar, aromatic quinone ring of ubiquinone Q8. The invariant Arg48 side chain stabilizes the negative charge of the charge-transfer state. The Cys44 residue is located at the N-terminus of an alpha-helix, suggesting that the helix dipole provides additional stabilization to the Cys44 thiolate. Upon formation of the charge-transfer interaction, all atoms of the quinone ring refined to a slightly larger distance from the second transmembrane helix compared to the inactive DsbB(Cys130Ser) variant, by approximately 0.2 A, similar to the shift observed in glutathione reductase.
