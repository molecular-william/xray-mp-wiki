# Nucleobase-Cation-Symport-1 (NCS1) Family

## Overview
The Nucleobase-Cation-Symport-1 (NCS1) family is a group of membrane transport proteins that mediate the uptake of nucleobases and related metabolites using cation symport. NCS1 transporters are essential components of salvage pathways for purines, pyrimidines, and their derivatives, and are found in bacteria, fungi, and plants.


## Mechanism/Details
NCS1 transporters contain 12 transmembrane helices, of which 10 are arranged in two inverted repeats of five helices (an inverted topology repeat commonly observed in many transporter and channel families). The core fold consists of a central four-helix bundle formed by broken transmembrane helices 1 and 6 associated with helices 2 and 7, coated by an outer layer of six additional helices.

The substrate-binding site is located at the breaks in discontinuous TMs 1 and 6, facing TMs 3 and 8. The cation-binding site is at the C-terminal end of TM1a interacting with TM8. The fold is structurally similar to that of the LeuT (NSS family) and vSGLT (SSS family) transporters despite low sequence identity, indicating a shared evolutionary ancestor.

NCS1 transporters operate via an alternating access mechanism. Substrate binding from the extracellular side induces closure of the outward-facing cavity, forming an occluded state. The reciprocal opening and closing of outward- and inward-facing cavities is synchronized by helices 3 and 8, which connect the two cavities on opposite sides of the membrane.
