# Camera Iris Gating

## Overview
Camera iris gating describes a mechanism of channel opening where transmembrane helices tilt and rotate outward from the central pore axis, analogous to the opening of a mechanical camera iris diaphragm. This creates an expanding central pore by moving pore-lining side chains away from the channel lumen.


## Mechanism/Details
In the MscS mechanosensitive channel, the seven TM3a helices that line the central pore undergo a coordinated conformational change during gating. In the closed state, these helices are arranged diagonally to the sevenfold symmetry axis, with the hydrophobic side chains of L105 and L109 projecting into the pore lumen. The helices are tightly packed against each other in a specific "knobs and holes" pattern of glycine and alanine residues.

Upon channel opening (as captured in the MscS A106V structure), the TM3a helices pivot around a conserved glycine hinge (G113) and become parallel to the sevenfold axis and the membrane normal. This rotation causes each TM3a helix to move outward from the center, pulling L105 and L109 away from the pore lumen. The seven helices simultaneously rotate and separate, expanding the pore from 4.8 Å (closed) to ~13 Å (open) — an increase of 8 Å.

In the open state, the TM3a helices are no longer close-packed but physically separated, with gaps that are not wide enough for molecule passage but may interact with lipids. The coordinated movement is accompanied by a rigid-body clockwise rotation of TM1 and TM2 helices. The A110-L115 interaction acts as a switch defining the open and closed states, with G113 serving as the pivot point.

The iris analogy is apt because, like camera iris blades, multiple structural elements move simultaneously in a coordinated radial fashion. This contrasts with other gating mechanisms such as the hinged-lid mechanism seen in some ligand-gated channels.
