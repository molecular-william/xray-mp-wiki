# Grana Stacking Mechanism

## Overview


## Mechanism/Details
Grana stacking is the process by which thylakoid membranes in plant chloroplasts
adhere to form tightly appressed stacks (grana), greatly increasing the
membrane surface area for light harvesting. The major light-harvesting complex
(LHCII) plays an essential role in mediating this adhesion.

Based on the crystal packing of spinach LHCII (PDB 4LCZ) crystallized under
physiological-relevant cation conditions (10-30 mM Zn2+, 200 mM Na+, pH 6.5),
a refined model of grana stacking was proposed. Unlike the "up-and-down" packing
in earlier pea LHCII crystals (2BHW), the 4LCZ structure shows a face-to-face
arrangement of LHCII trimers with a large stromal contact area, closely resembling
the interlayer overlapping pattern revealed by electron tomography.

Key features of the model:
- Cations (Na+, Zn2+) bridge negatively charged stromal surface residues
  between adjacent layers, providing the primary attraction force
- The N-terminal segment of LHCII contributes additional positively charged
  residues for electrostatic interactions
- The distance between stromal surfaces of neighboring grana stacks is ~22 A
- Nine conserved stromal-exposed residues (mostly negatively charged) are
  evenly distributed across the LHCII surface, rendering it brush-like and
  flexible
- This model explains why grana stacking can be reversibly controlled by
  cation concentration

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [Spinach Light-Harvesting Complex II (LHC-II)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/photosynthesis/spinach-light-harvesting-complex-ii/) — The X-ray crystal structure of spinach LHCII at 2.7 A resolution (PDB 4LCZ) reveals a face-to-face stacking pattern of LHCII trimers that mimics in vivo grana stacking. Eight zinc ions and three cacodylate ions, plus five sodium ions were identified per trimer. Three Zn2+ ions partially replace the central Mg2+ of Chl a614, while cations on the stromal surface (five Na+, two Zn2+) mediate ionic bridges between negatively charged residue pairs from neighboring layers, constituting the primary attraction force. Nine conserved stromal-exposed residues coordinate cations or form hydrogen bonds between layers.
