# PZ Pocket: Inhibitor-Induced Binding Pocket in POTs

## Overview
The PZ pocket is a previously undescribed mostly hydrophobic pocket in proton-dependent oligopeptide transporters (POTs) that is induced upon binding of the potent inhibitor Lys[Z(NO2)]-Val (LZNV). The pocket is absent in the ligand-free (apo) state and forms through conformational changes including rotamer changes of F318, Y35, and Y159, as well as a rigid body movement of the N-terminal six-helix bundle by ~5°. The PZ pocket accommodates the Z(NO2) moiety of LZNV, with a π-stacking interaction between F318 and the 4-nitrophenyl group. The pocket is comprised of 9 residues: F311, Q313, A314, F318, F386, M389, S412, I413, and L416, most of which are conserved in bacterial peptide transporters and in human PEPT1 and PEPT2. The occlusion of the PZ pocket by the Z(NO2) moiety acts like a wedge, impeding the transition to the inward-open state and thereby blocking the transport cycle.

## Mechanism/Details
LZNV inhibits POTs through a two-step mechanism: (1) the dipeptide backbone of LZNV binds to conserved residues in the substrate-binding pocket, engaging the same polar/ionic interactions as dipeptide substrates (R31, Y35, K133, N163, E420); (2) the Z(NO2) moiety induces formation of the initially absent PZ pocket via rotamer changes of specific side chains (F318, Y35, Y159). The Z(NO2) moiety anchored deep in the C-terminal bundle acts as a wedge that blocks the conformational transition from the inward-facing occluded state to the inward-open state. Unlike transportable substrates, LZNV does not trigger the distinct α-helix bending observed in inward-facing occluded structures, placing the LZNV-bound state as an intermediate in the transition from inward-open to inward-facing occluded.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- yepept — The LZNV-bound structure of YePEPT K314A (PDB 7PZ3, 3.1 Å) revealed the PZ pocket in atomic detail. The apo structure (PDB 7PZ4, 2.93 Å) confirmed the pocket is absent in the ligand-free state.
- human-pept1 — Human PEPT1 residue Q300 (corresponding to YePEPT K314) and the adjacent glycine (corresponding to YePEPT P315) suggest the PZ pocket is accessible in the mammalian homologues. The double humanised variant YePEPT K314Q-P315G showed concentration-dependent stabilisation by LZNV, supporting the relevance of the PZ pocket mechanism to human peptide transporters.
- human-pept2 — Human PEPT2 residue Q319 (corresponding to YePEPT K314) and the adjacent glycine indicate the PZ pocket is similarly accessible in PEPT2.
