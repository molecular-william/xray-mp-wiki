# CRD Cholesterol Binding Site of Smoothened

## Overview
The CRD cholesterol binding site is a shallow groove on the surface of the cysteine-rich domain (CRD) of Smoothened (SMO) that binds cholesterol and oxysterols such as 20(S)-hydroxycholesterol (20(S)-OHC). Identified in the crystal structure of human SMO (PDB 5L7D, 3.2 Å), this site is distinct from the deep 7TM sterol-binding pocket. Cholesterol bound at this site occupies a central position at the interface between the CRD, linker domain, and TMD, stabilizing the inactive state of SMO.

## Mechanism/Details
In the human SMO structure, cholesterol adopts an extended conformation with its tetracyclic sterol ring bound in a shallow hydrophobic groove on the CRD surface. The cholesterol 3β-hydroxyl group is incorporated in a hydrogen-bonding network with Asp95, Trp109, and Tyr130 of the CRD, optimally positioning the α-face of the sterol ring system to make a stacking interaction with the indole ring of Trp109. The iso-octyl tail of cholesterol is oriented by residues Leu491, Ala492, and Ile496 of TMD ECL3 and Val210 of the linker domain, positioning the cholesterol at the CRD-linker domain-TMD interface. Mutations predicted to disrupt this hydrogen-bonding network (Asp95Ala, Tyr130Phe in human; Asp99Ala, Tyr134Phe in mouse) impair SMO's ability to bind 20(S)-OHC and to transmit both SHH and 20(S)-OHC signals. Vismodegib binding to the TMD site induces a conformational change propagated to this CRD site, resulting in rearrangement of the sterol-binding site and loss of cholesterol. The CRD cholesterol binding site is highly conserved in vertebrates but less conserved in Drosophila, consistent with the observation that fly SMO does not bind oxysterols.
