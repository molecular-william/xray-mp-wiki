# Horizontal Helix Spring Mechanism

## Overview
The horizontal helix spring mechanism is a structural mechanism discovered in the bacterial VKOR ([Vitamin K](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/cofactors/vitamin-k/) epoxide reductase) homologue from Synechococcus sp. (ssVKOR). A short alpha-helix (residues Gly55 to Ser62) located at the hydrophobic active site of VKOR, between transmembrane helices TM1 and TM2, undergoes reversible winding and unwinding conformational changes that promote electron transfer between the thioredoxin (Trx) domain and the VKOR catalytic domain. Cys56, located at the exact N-terminal end of this helix, is physically dragged back and forth by the helix motions, allowing it to alternatively interact with Cys50 (in the Trx domain) or Cys130 (in the VKOR active site), which are separated by 16 Angstroms.


## Mechanism/Details
The horizontal helix acts as a molecular spring that facilitates electron transfer during the redox cycle of VKOR. In the wound conformation (observed in the Cys50Ala mutant at 2.8 A), the helix adopts a typical alpha-helical structure with four hydrogen bonds from Gly55 to Ser62. In the unwound conformation (observed in the Cys56Ala mutant at 3.6 A), the C-alpha trace is no longer alpha-helical with only two potential hydrogen bonds. The unwound conformation gives Cys56 the flexibility to move between the two active sites. Inserting residues between Cys56 and the horizontal helix significantly lowers electron transfer efficiency but does not affect [Vitamin K](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/cofactors/vitamin-k/) reduction at the active site, confirming that the physical proximity of Cys56 to the helix is essential for the mechanism. The wound conformation is probably more energetically favorable because residues in the horizontal helix form more hydrogen bonds and have more interactions with the membrane at this state. Compared with other disulfide-bond-forming enzymes, motion of a horizontal helix is a unique feature observed in the VKOR enzyme. The horizontal helix is partially buried in the membrane, with residues on the aqueous side (Asp57, Leu58, Ser61, Ser62) accessible to membrane-impermeable probes and residues on the membrane side (Val59, Leu60) not efficiently labeled. Warfarin-resistant mutations identified in human VKOR map to or close to the horizontal helix and appear to induce a conformational change that weakens its membrane association, altering the putative warfarin-binding pocket.
