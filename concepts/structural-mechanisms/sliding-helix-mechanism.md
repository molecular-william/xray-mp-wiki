# Sliding Helix Mechanism

## Overview
The sliding helix mechanism is a model for voltage-dependent gating in voltage-gated ion channels (VGICs), including voltage-gated sodium (NaV), potassium (KV), and calcium (CaV) channels. In this mechanism, the S4 transmembrane helix containing positively charged gating charges slides vertically across the membrane electric field in response to membrane depolarization, while also rotating. The gating charges exchange ion pair partners with negative charges on neighboring helices as they move through a hydrophobic constriction site (HCS).


## Mechanism/Details
The sliding helix mechanism was proposed based on structural and functional studies of voltage-sensing domains. Key features include vertical translation of S4 (approximately 10-15 A), rotation allowing ion pair exchange, passage through a hydrophobic constriction site on S2, and conformational changes in S4 secondary structure (from 3(10) helix in resting state to loop plus alpha helix in activated state). Structural evidence from the [NAVAB](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/navab/) resting-state structure supports this model over the transporter or paddle mechanisms.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [NAVAB](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/navab/) Bacterial Voltage-Gated Sodium Channel — Resting-state structure (PDB 6P6X) shows S4 drawn intracellularly with 3(10) helix conformation, supporting sliding helix mechanism. Activated state shows S4 moved outward by 11.5 A with loop plus alpha helix conformation.
- Voltage-sensitive phosphatases — Distantly related proteins with VS modules that also follow the sliding helix mechanism.
- Eukaryotic NaV and CaV channels — Conserved sliding helix mechanism expected based on [NAVAB](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/navab/) ancestral structure.
