# Rocker-Switch Mechanism

## Overview
The rocker-switch mechanism is an alternating access transport mode in which two parallel helical bundles associate to form a shared binding site at their interface. Substrate binding at this interface from one side of the membrane drives domain closure, alternately exposing the binding site to opposite sides of the bilayer. This mechanism is characteristic of Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS) transporters.


## Mechanism/Details
In rocker-switch transporters, the protein is composed of two symmetry-related homologous domains arranged as two parallel helical bundles. These bundles associate to form a shared binding site at their interface. Substrate binding on one side of the membrane drives domain closure, rotating the two domains toward one another, which alternately exposes the substrate-binding site to each side of the membrane.

The rocker-switch mode is distinct from the gated-pore mode in several ways:
- Rocker switches involve larger conformational changes, with two domains rotating toward each other
- The interdomain association is parallel rather than antiparallel
- The binding site is at the interface of two bundles rather than at broken helices
- Two MFS members have been visualized in their inward-facing (Ci) conformation at high resolution by X-ray crystallography

Like gated pores, rocker-switch transporters use electrochemical gradient-driven membrane transport. However, the larger conformational changes required for rocker-switch transport may make these proteins more challenging targets for crystallization.
