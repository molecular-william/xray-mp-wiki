# pH-Dependent Gating in Membrane Proteins

## Overview
pH-dependent gating is a regulatory mechanism in membrane transport proteins
where changes in cellular pH (proton concentration) modulate the opening and
closing of the protein's pore or channel. This mechanism is particularly
relevant in physiological processes where metabolic activity alters local pH.
In human aquaglyceroporin AQP10, a histidine residue (His80) acts as the pH
sensor: at low pH, His80 becomes doubly protonated, triggering conformational
changes that open a glycerol-specific gate at the cytoplasmic interface. This
mechanism is unique among aquaporins, as AQP10 becomes stimulated by pH
reduction rather than inhibited, in agreement with lipolysis-induced
acidification in adipocytes.


## Mechanism/Details
The pH-dependent gating mechanism in AQP10 is mediated by a cytoplasmic gate
formed by residues H80, F85, R94, E27, V76, and S77. His80 serves as the pH
sensor. At neutral to basic pH, His80 is mono-protonated and the gate remains
closed to glycerol while permitting water passage. At low pH (during lipolysis),
His80 becomes doubly protonated, enabling interaction with Glu27. This triggers
a cascade of structural changes: the nearby Phe85 repositions, the V76-S77 loop
rearranges, and the cytoplasmic gate opens to allow glycerol flux. Molecular
dynamics simulations identified four main conformational clusters (closed to
fully open) correlating with His80 protonation states, with calculated pKa
values ranging from 3.6 to 7.1 depending on conformation.

Key features of pH-dependent gating include:
- A titratable residue (typically histidine, aspartate, or glutamate) that
  senses pH changes
- A gating region that undergoes conformational change upon protonation/
  deprotonation
- Substrate specificity: the gate may selectively control flux of specific
  substrates (e.g., glycerol vs. water in AQP10)


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
