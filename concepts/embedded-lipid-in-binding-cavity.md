# Embedded Lipid in the Binding Cavity

## Overview
The presence of an embedded lipid within the substrate-binding cavity of a
multidrug transporter was first directly observed in the 2.9 A crystal
structure of LmrP from Lactococcus lactis (PDB 6T1Z). A
phosphatidylglycerol (PG) molecule was found occupying the binding pocket
adjacent to the bound substrate Hoechst 33342. This finding suggests a
mechanism for multidrug polyspecificity in which a malleable lipid
component within the binding cavity provides conformational plasticity
to accommodate structurally diverse substrates.


## Mechanism/Details
In the LmrP structure, the embedded PG lipid stabilizes the outward-open
substrate-bound conformation through interactions with residues in the
N-lobe of the protein. Molecular dynamics simulations showed that the
anionic lipid maintains the Arg14-Asp142 salt bridge, which dissociates
in the absence of the lipid. The lipid alkyl chain within the N-lobe
(between TM1 and TM4) is well-ordered, while the other chain in the
central cavity is disordered, consistent with its proposed role as a
flexible hydrophobic element.

The embedded lipid appears to contribute to substrate-specific transport
activity: mutations that perturb lipid binding (S52Y, T56Y, N116Y)
reduce transport of Hoechst 33342, tetracycline, and erythromycin but
not clindamycin. This substrate-dependent effect indicates the lipid
does not simply stabilize the protein but actively participates in
substrate recognition in a selective manner.

Native mass spectrometry confirmed that LmrP binds a single PG molecule
tightly enough to survive gas-phase analysis, and that this binding is
specifically disrupted by the N116Y mutation within the lipid-binding
site.

The hypothesis proposes that compared to protein side chains, the
embedded lipid has greater conformational plasticity, providing a more
malleable hydrophobic environment to accommodate structurally diverse
substrates. This mechanism may extend to other multidrug transporters,
although previous structures had not identified lipids within binding
pockets, possibly due to their intrinsically flexible nature.
