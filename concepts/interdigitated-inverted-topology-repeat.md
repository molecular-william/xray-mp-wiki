# Interdigitated Inverted Topology Repeat (IITR)

## Overview
The interdigitated inverted topology repeat (IITR) is a protein fold in which two repeat sequences (N-repeat and C-repeat) insert into the membrane with opposite orientation, related by an approximate twofold rotation axis in the membrane plane. Individual repeats consist of helices that interdigitate, creating a complicated folding pattern reminiscent of domain-swapping. This topology has been identified primarily in transporters and channels (e.g., MFS, MIT, and DHA families) and was first observed in an enzyme in BacA/UppP.

## Mechanism/Details
In the IITR fold, each repeat starts with a re-entrant helix pair (inner arch) followed by three transmembrane helices (outer arch). The repeats interdigitate such that the two structural domains consist of swapped segments: domain 1 includes the N-repeat's inner arch and the C-repeat's outer arch, while domain 2 includes the C-repeat's inner arch and the N-repeat's outer arch. The repeats are related by a C2 axis of pseudosymmetry in the membrane plane. This topology enables alternating access between inward-facing and outward-facing states via a rocker-switch-like motion of the two domains, as exploited by transporters and channels.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [BacA (UppP)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/baca/) — First enzyme found to adopt the IITR fold. The N-repeat (residues 1-150) and C-repeat (residues 151-273) interdigitate to form two structural domains with the active site at their interface. The fold is open to the periplasm on one side and closed on the other.
