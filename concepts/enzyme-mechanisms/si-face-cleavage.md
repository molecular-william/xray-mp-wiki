# si-Face Cleavage in Rhomboid Proteases

## Overview
Rhomboid proteases cleave substrates from the si-face (small interface) of the scissile peptide bond, rather than the re-face typical of most soluble serine proteases. This stereochemistry was first predicted from the open-cap conformation of E. coli GlpG (Wang & Ha, PNAS 2007), based on the geometry of the putative oxyanion hole and the lateral substrate entry pathway between TM helices S2 and S5. The si-face attack mechanism is shared with other serine peptidases having a Ser-Lys catalytic dyad, including signal peptidase (SPase), Lon protease, LexA peptidase, and VP4 protease.

## Mechanism/Details
The open-cap conformation of E. coli GlpG (Wang & Ha, PNAS 2007, PDB 2IC8-based model at 2.5 A) revealed that the scissile peptide bond would be attacked from the si-face by the catalytic serine (Ser-201). This is based on the location of the oxyanion hole (formed by the backbone amide of Ser-201 and side chains of His-150 and Asn-154) relative to the Ser-His dyad. In classic serine proteases such as trypsin and chymotrypsin, the nucleophilic attack occurs on the re-face of the scissile bond. The si-face mechanism of rhomboid proteases positions the substrate such that the attacking serine approaches from the opposite side. This stereochemical difference is important for designing specific rhomboid protease inhibitors that target the transition state. A cocrystal of SPase with a beta-lactam inhibitor later confirmed this si-face stereochemistry for the broader class of Ser-Lys dyad proteases.
