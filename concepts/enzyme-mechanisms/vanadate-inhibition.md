# Vanadate Inhibition of P-type ATPases

## Overview
Vanadate is the hallmark inhibitor of the P-type ATPase family. Crystal structures of vanadate-inhibited SERCA1a (PDB 5A3Q, 5A3R, 5A3S) at 3.05 Å resolution revealed that monomeric orthovanadate (VO₃⁻) binds at the catalytic site as a trigonal bipyramidal complex mimicking a pentavalent dephosphorylation intermediate, while decameric vanadate (V₁₀O₂₈⁶⁻) inhibits by competing with nucleotide binding.

## Mechanism/Details
Vanadate exhibits a dual inhibitory mechanism against P-type ATPases: (1) Orthovanadate (VO₃⁻) structurally resembles phosphate and binds at the phosphorylation site, adopting a trigonal bipyramidal geometry analogous to the pentavalent transition state for phosphate ester hydrolysis. It is coordinated between the catalytic Asp351 and an attacking water molecule, flanked by Mg²⁺ and three water molecules. This mimics the dephosphorylation transition state and blocks the E2→E1 conformational transition. (2) Decavanadate (V₁₀O₂₈⁶⁻), a decameric species favored at low pH, competes with ATP for binding at the nucleotide-binding site with remarkably high affinity (KD 2.7 nM in the Ca₂E1 state). Orthovanadate binding occurs exclusively in the E2 (Ca²⁺-free) state, while decavanadate binds to both Ca₂E1 and E2·VO₃⁻ states.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- SERCA1a (rabbit sarcoplasmic reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase) — Orthovanadate binds at the phosphorylation site as a dephosphorylation transition state analog (PDB 5A3Q). Decavanadate binds at the nucleotide-binding site with high affinity.
