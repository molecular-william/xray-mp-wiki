# SERCA E2-P Dephosphorylation and Counterion Occlusion

## Overview
The dephosphorylation of the SERCA calcium pump E2-P state is coupled to occlusion of proton counterions in the transmembrane domain. The crystal structure of rabbit SERCA1a in complex with aluminum fluoride (E2-AlF4-), which mimics the transition state of E2-P hydrolysis, reveals a closed conformation in which four carboxylate residues (Glu58, Glu309, Glu771, Asp800) are buried within the membrane without bound Ca2+ or positively charged amino acids nearby. These carboxylates are necessarily protonated, representing the occluded state of the H+ counterions that were taken up from the lumen in exchange for Ca2+. Hydrolysis is catalyzed by the conserved TGES motif (Thr181-Gly182-Glu183-Ser184) of the A domain, which positions a water molecule for inline nucleophilic attack on the aspartyl-phosphate (Asp351). The Glu183 side chain acts as a general base, abstracting a proton from the attacking water. This associative SN2-like mechanism is identical to that of phosphoryl transfer from ATP, revealing a fundamental symmetry in the P-type ATPase reaction cycle: Ca2+ occlusion is coupled to phosphorylation, while H+ occlusion is coupled to dephosphorylation.

## Mechanism/Details
Structural features of the E2-AlF4- transition state (analogous to
E2-P dephosphorylation):

1. **Dephosphorylation site architecture**: The planar AlF4- group sits
   between Asp351 (P domain) and a water molecule positioned by the
   main-chain carbonyl of Thr181 and the side chain of Glu183 from the
   TGES motif of the A domain. Glu183 provides general base catalysis.
   Mg2+ completes the octahedral coordination. The TGES motif occupies
   the same spatial position as ADP in the E1~P·ADP:AlF4- form, showing
   that the same catalytic machinery is repurposed for the reverse
   reaction.

2. **Proton occlusion**: The four Ca2+-binding carboxylates (Glu58,
   Glu309, Glu771, Asp800) are sequestered in a hydrophobic environment
   without counterions. Their protonated state is energetically
   necessary. Biochemical data confirm this is a proton-occluded state:
   the E2-AlF4- complex has extremely low affinity for Ca2+ (four orders
   of magnitude lower than E2), requiring millimolar Ca2+ for
   reactivation, consistent with occluded access to the lumen.

3. **Luminal gate closure**: A layer of positively charged residues near
   the lumen-exposed surface electrostatically repels luminal Ca2+,
   contributing to the closed state.

4. **Five-helix cluster and K+ site**: A cluster of five small helices
   (three from the P domain, two from linkers) forms around an integral
   K+ binding site that stimulates dephosphorylation. Residues from
   P-domain helices P5 and P6, together with Gln244 from the A-M3
   linker, coordinate the K+ ion.

5. **Conformational coupling**: The P5/P6 helices act as a sensor of
   phosphate release. Upon dephosphorylation, they switch orientation,
   displacing the M2-A and A-M3 linkers and moving the TGES motif away
   from the phosphorylation site. This unlocks the A domain, allowing
   the N domain to rotate ~25° back toward the P domain, which enhances
   cytoplasmic Ca2+ binding.

The overall transport cycle follows a two-door mechanism:
- Phosphoryl transfer from ATP locks the cytoplasmic door on Ca2+
- ADP release opens the luminal door for Ca2+/H+ exchange
- E2-P hydrolysis locks the luminal door on H+
- Inorganic phosphate release opens the cytoplasmic door for H+/Ca2+ exchange
