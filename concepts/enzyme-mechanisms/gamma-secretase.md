# Gamma-Secretase Complex

## Overview
Gamma-secretase is an intramembrane protease complex responsible for the generation of amyloid-beta (Abeta) peptides from the amyloid precursor protein (APP). Aberrant accumulation of Abeta leads to amyloid plaque formation in Alzheimer's disease. The complex consists of four components: presenilin (PS, the catalytic aspartyl protease), presenilin enhancer 2 (Pen-2), anterior pharynx-defective 1 (Aph-1), and nicastrin. The complex contains 19 transmembrane helices arranged in a horseshoe-shaped assembly with a thick end and a thin end. Nicastrin is the largest component and the putative substrate-recruiting subunit, accounting for approximately two-thirds of the apparent molecular mass of the intact human gamma-secretase.

## Mechanism/Details
Gamma-secretase cleaves type I integral membrane proteins within their transmembrane domains. PS contains nine transmembrane helices with two catalytic aspartate residues located in TM6 and TM7.

The complex is thought to consist of two subcomplexes:
- Subcomplex 1: Pen-2 and PS1-NTF (N-terminal six TMs), bearing 8 TMs, occupying the thin end
- Subcomplex 2: PS1-CTF (C-terminal three TMs), Aph-1, and nicastrin, possessing 11 TMs, occupying the thick end

Nicastrin functions as the substrate-recruiting component via its large extracellular domain (ECD), which contains a putative substrate-binding pocket on the large lobe that is shielded by a lid from the small lobe in the inactive conformation. Upon activation, the lid is proposed to open via relative rotation of the lobes around a central hydrophobic pivot, allowing substrate access.

The structure of the gamma-secretase complex has been studied by cryo-EM at 4.5 A resolution, revealing the overall horseshoe-shaped TM architecture. The crystal structure of the nicastrin ECD from D. purpureum at 1.95 A provided an improved atomic model for human nicastrin and enabled revised TM assignments within the complex.
