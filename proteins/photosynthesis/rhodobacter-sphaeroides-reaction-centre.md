# Rhodobacter sphaeroides Photosynthetic Reaction Centre

## Overview

The photosynthetic reaction centre from Rhodobacter sphaeroides is a membrane protein complex that performs light-driven electron transfer across the bacterial cell membrane. It consists of L, M, and H subunits and binds four bacteriochlorophyll molecules, two bacteriopheophytins, two ubiquinones, and one non-heme iron atom. The structure was solved at 2.35 Å resolution from crystals grown in lipidic cubic phase (PDB ID: 1ogv), representing the first type I crystal packing reported for a purple bacterial reaction centre. The structure reveals a cardiolipin-binding site mediating crystal contacts and a chloride-binding site within the membrane-spanning region.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Lipid-mediated crystal contacts in type I packing

A cardiolipin molecule was identified at the protein-protein interface within the membrane-spanning region, mediating crystal contacts between symmetry-related molecules. The cardiolipin head-group forms a salt-bridge with Lys202 of the L-subunit from a symmetry-related molecule, forming a quasi-dimeric structure. This supports the suggestion that cardiolipin could aid dimerisation of reaction centres.

### Chloride-binding site in membrane-spanning region

A chloride-binding site was identified near the QB binding site within the membrane-spanning region, coordinated by Arg M132, Arg M136, and backbone nitrogen atoms of Gly M53 and Gly M56. The site is near a water channel connecting the mobile quinone (QB) binding site with the protein surface, suggesting a possible role in protein-protein interactions with the light-harvesting complex I, cytochrome bc1 complex, or PufX.

### Compression of transmembrane region in lipidic environment

Comparison of the lipidic cubic phase structure with six detergent-based structures revealed a slight compression of the entire transmembrane-spanning region towards the 2-fold pseudo-symmetry axis. This compression was attributed to surface pressure effects within the lipid-rich environment of type I crystals, as lipid bilayers exert lateral pressure on embedded membrane proteins.
