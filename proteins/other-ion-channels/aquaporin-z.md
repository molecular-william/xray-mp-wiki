# Aquaporin Z (AqpZ)

## Overview

[Aquaporin](/xray-mp-wiki/concepts/transport-mechanisms/aquaporin/) Z (AqpZ) is the
bacterial homolog of human aquaporin 1 and serves as a
water-selective channel in Escherichia coli. AqpZ is a
homotetramer of four functional monomeric channels, each
containing six transmembrane helices and two half-spanning
helices forming an hourglass-shaped pore. The 2.5 Å X-ray
structure (PDB 1RC2) was the first atomic resolution structure
of a recombinant aquaporin, revealing the molecular basis for
water versus glycerol selectivity through a combination of
steric pore restriction and specific amino acid substitutions
in the selectivity filter. A 3.2 Å tetrameric structure (tAqpZ)
in space group P4_1 22 revealed two distinct Arg-189 side chain
conformations among the four monomer channels, corresponding to
open and closed states at the narrowest channel constriction.
The open conformation (monomer A) allows water binding via a
tridentate H-bond, while the closed conformation (monomers B-D)
occludes the channel by H-bonding between Arg-189 and Thr-183.
Mutant structures (F43W, H174G/T183F, F43W/H174G/T183F) further
revealed that the selectivity filter (SF) region is surprisingly
similar in size to WT AqpZ, allowing dissection of SF polarity
versus size effects on water and glycerol conduction.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Selectivity filter steric and chemical mechanisms for water vs. glycerol discrimination

The selectivity filter of AqpZ, formed by residues F43, H174, R189, and the carbonyl of T183, is the narrowest point in the channel (~2 A diameter). Comparison with GlpF (the E. coli glycerol facilitator) reveals the basis for substrate selectivity: GlpF's larger, more hydrophobic selectivity filter (F43W, H199G, T200F) accommodates glycerol, while AqpZ's smaller, more hydrophilic filter excludes it. A bound water molecule in AqpZ's selectivity filter (2.7 A to H174, 3.0 A to T183, 2.6 A to R189) confirms preference for a small hydrophilic substrate. The absence of glycerol in the AqpZ structure (despite the presence of isopropanol in vestibules) contrasts with GlpF where ordered glycerol was found at three channel sites.

### Global orientational tuning: mechanism for proton exclusion at the NPA region

The NPA motif asparagines (N63, N186) act as hydrogen-bond donors to the central water molecule in the channel, locking it in a conformation where it can only donate hydrogen bonds. This prevents the central water from accepting a proton, breaking the Grotthuss proton conduction chain. The positive dipoles of helices M3 and M7, aimed at the central water, further disrupt the proton wire. Carbonyls of F62 and V185, running parallel to the channel axis near the NPA region, may reorient water as it passes the quasi-2-fold axis, as supported by molecular dynamics simulations.

### A secondary constriction at the NPA motif narrows the pore in aquaporins

Residues L15 (helix M1) and F145 (helix M5) project into the pore at the NPA region, creating a secondary constriction with 3.0 A diameter in AqpZ, compared to 4.0 A in GlpF and 3.5 A in AQP1. The central water at this position has a shorter hydrogen-bond distance (~2.8 A vs ~3.5 A) than GlpF without glycerol. This constriction pattern correlates with substrate selectivity: aquaglyceroporins have leucine at both positions 15 and 145, while aquaporins have an aromatic at one of the two positions.

### CalphaH...O hydrogen bonds facilitate tight helix packing in membrane proteins

The strict tertiary conservation of aquaporin structures underscores unique features of helix packing in membrane proteins. Transmembrane helices in AqpZ are packed at distances close enough for CalphaH...O hydrogen-bond formation (<3.5 A), facilitated by glycine residues. This interaction is observed extensively in membrane proteins and contributes to the tight helical bundle architecture.

### Tetramer interface stability correlates with hydrophobic packing

The AqpZ tetramer has the largest protomer-protomer interface (3,340 A^2) among the three structurally characterized aquaporins, owing to 11 aromatic residues at the interface (vs. 5 in GlpF, 3 in AQP1). This correlates with biochemical stability: AqpZ is a stable tetramer even in mild denaturing conditions, while GlpF tends to aggregate and AQP1 is well-behaved. The tetramer pore remains ~7-10 A in diameter and contains electron density for multiple molecules, though its functional significance remains unknown.

### Selectivity filter polarity, not size, dominates water conduction at narrow pore radii

Sequential mutation of AqpZ SF residues to GlpF counterparts (F43W, H174G/T183F, F43W/H174G/T183F) resulted in decreased water permeability but no increase in glycerol conduction. Crystal structures showed mutant SF pore sizes similar to WT AqpZ (SF areas: 3.9, 2.0, 2.5, 3.5 A^2 for WT, F43W, H174G/T183F, F43W/H174G/T183F; vs 9.3 A^2 for GlpF). Because size was held constant, this revealed that SF hydrophilicity dominates water conduction energetics when the pore approaches the size of a water molecule.

### Structural elements outside the SF determine pore size

The narrow SF pores of AqpZ mutants compared to GlpF result from differences in helix packing and loop architecture outside the pore. Helix M2 orientation differs by 1 A at residue 43, shifting the indole ring of Trp into the pore. The M6-M7 linker conformations differ between AqpZ and GlpF, affecting the positioning of C-terminal water-binding carbonyls. The invariant SF arginine (Arg189 in AqpZ, Arg206 in GlpF) adopts different conformations: 'down' in AqpZ (stabilized by C loop interactions) vs. 'up' in GlpF, contributing to narrower pores.

### Three-regime model for aquaporin selectivity

A general model for AQP selectivity is proposed based on three regimes of decreasing channel size. Regime 1 (large pore, >8 A): SF resembles bulk water, allows ion conduction, not physiologically relevant. Regime 2 (AQGP, ~2 A radius): solute permeability sterically allowed and facilitated by hydrophobic interactions with the carbon backbone of alditols. Regime 3 (orthodox AQP, <1.4 A radius): water-sized pore where polarity dominates; channel hydrophilicity determines water conduction rate.

### Two distinct Arg-189 conformations in the tetrameric AqpZ structure reveal open and closed channel states

The 3.2 A tetrameric AqpZ (tAqpZ) structure in space group P4_1 22 revealed two distinct Arg-189 side chain conformations among the four monomeric channels. In monomer A, the guanidino group of Arg-189 points toward the periplasmic vestibule, opening the constriction to accommodate a water molecule through a tridentate H-bond with His-174 NE2, Thr-183 carbonyl oxygen, and Arg-189 NE (WA2). This represents the open channel state. In monomers B-D, the Arg-189 guanidino group bends over to form an H-bond with the carbonyl oxygen of Thr-183, occluding the channel (closed state). The difference in Arg-189 displacements correlates with a strong electron density found between first transmembrane helices (Phe-10) of two open channels (A-A interface), suggesting asymmetrical subunit interactions stabilize the observed conformations. Alteration between the two Arg-189 conformations disrupts the continuous flow of water, regulating the open probability of the water pore. The Arg-189 conformational equilibrium is associated with two alternative hydrogen bond networks between Arg-189 and Glu-138, with Glu-8 also contributing through a rigid chain of hydrogen bonds mediated by the NPA-NPA interlocking network. Arg-189, Glu-138, and Glu-8 are among the most conserved residues in the AQP protein family.
