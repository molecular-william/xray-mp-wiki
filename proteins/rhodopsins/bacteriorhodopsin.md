# Bacteriorhodopsin (bR) from Halobacterium salinarum

## Overview

Bacteriorhodopsin (bR) is a light-driven proton pump from the archaeon
Halobacterium salinarum. It is the best-characterized microbial rhodopsin
and serves as a model system for understanding retinal protein function.
bR consists of seven transmembrane helices forming a retinal-binding pocket.
The retinal chromophore is covalently linked to Lys216 via a protonated
Schiff base. Light absorption triggers all-trans to 13-cis isomerization
of retinal, initiating a photocycle that drives unidirectional proton
translocation across the membrane. The ground state structure has been
determined at an unprecedented resolution of 1.29 A (PDB 5ZIL) using
low-dose X-ray data collection at 15 K, enabling visualization of
individual atoms and accurate hydrogen bonding geometries in the
proton transfer pathway.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### First high-resolution X-ray structure of bacteriorhodopsin at 2.5 A

The 2.5 A X-ray structure of bR (Pebay-Peyroula et al., 1997) represents the first high-resolution structure of this archetypal membrane protein, crystallized from a monoolein-based lipidic cubic phase (LCP). The structure revealed 82% of the 248 residues (7-156 and 167-225), the all-trans retinal chromophore, and 144 water/lipid positions. Space group P6_3 with one monomer per asymmetric unit. Retinal is positioned between Trp86 and Trp182 in a slightly distorted and twisted all-trans conformation. Residues Lys216 (Schiff base), Asp85, and Asp96 were unequivocally identified. Arg82 was found at a distance incompatible with a salt bridge to Asp85. Glu204 side chain points away from the proton channel, differing from the earlier EM model. Eight water molecules were located in the putative proton channel between helices B, C, and G. Four well-defined waters include Wat1 (H-bonded to Trp182, 6.7 A from Schiff base on cytoplasmic side), Wat5 (H-bonded to Asp96, 10 A from Schiff base), and waters near Asp85/Asp212. Asp96 is 10 A from the Schiff base — too large for direct proton transfer. The high pKa of Asp96 (>11) is explained by limited solvent accessibility due to Phe42. Wat2 binds to the backbone carbonyl of Lys216, causing helix G deviation from standard geometry. A water pocket near Asp85, Tyr57, Asp212, and Arg82 forms a hydrogen bond network facilitating proton translocation from Asp85 to Arg82 and Thr205. The structure confirmed that bR in the crystalline state undergoes the main photocycle steps based on FTIR spectroscopy in the cubic phase.

### Proton transfer pathways in bacteriorhodopsin at 2.3 A resolution

The 2.3 A structure of bR (Luecke et al., 1998) revealed several key features of the proton translocation mechanism. The crystals exhibited nearly perfect merohedral twinning (P6_3 space group with pseudo-622 Laue symmetry), requiring detwinning during data processing. A key finding was water molecule W402 bridging the Schiff base nitrogen and Asp85 (2.9 A from NZ and 2.6 A from OD1 of Asp85), providing a direct hydrogen-bonded chain for the first proton transfer step. The proton release pathway was identified as Arg82 → W403 → Glu204 → Glu194. W403 is hydrogen-bonded to the NE of Arg82 and is within 3.5 A of OE2 of Glu204. The alternative pathway through Thr205 and Glu9 was found to have less favorable distances and orientation. On the cytoplasmic side, Asp96 is located 12 A from the Schiff base, with no ordered water molecules detected between them. Thr46 hydroxyl is hydrogen-bonded to the peptide carbonyl of Phe42 (2.7 A) and is 3.5 A from Asp96. Asp212 is connected by a three-dimensional hydrogen-bonded network to Tyr57, Tyr185, and Arg82, explaining its unusually low pKa (<2). Two large cavities were identified by a probe-accessible surface search: one in the Arg82/Glu204/Glu194 region (3-4 potential water sites) and a smaller cavity between Asp96 and the Schiff base (1-2 potential water sites). Comparison with the three existing models (Grigorieff et al. EM, Kimura et al., and Pebay-Peyroula et al.) showed major differences in water positions near the Schiff base and in the extracellular proton-conducting pathway.

### Ultrafast retinal isomerization dynamics

TR-SFX at 250 fs to 10 ps time delays reveals retinal twists into a highly twisted 13-cis conformation within 0.24 ps. The temporal evolution of the C13=C14 dihedral angle separates into three phases: initial torsional motion (0-500 fs), transition to J state and hot all-trans ground state (500-750 fs), and relaxation to J/K states (1-10 ps).

### Vibrationally coupled photoactive network

The photoexcited retinal and surrounding residues (Trp86, Tyr185, Met118, Asp85, Asp212, Arg82, Glu194, Glu204) and water molecules (Wat401, Wat402, Wat403, Wat404, Wat406, Wat407) form an extended vibrationally coupled network. Oscillatory motions with frequencies of 80-125 cm^-1 are observed in retinal torsions, water displacements, and side chain movements.

### Multiphoton excitation effects in bR

Sequential two-photon absorption at high laser intensities (>30 GW/cm^2) leads to excitation of Trp86 via a charge-transfer state. This multiphoton process is non-productive for isomerization and reduces quantum yield. The linear regime for single-photon excitation is below 30 GW/cm^2.

### Charge-transfer states between retinal and aromatic residues

Quantum chemistry (XMCQDPT2-CASSCF) reveals charge-transfer (CT) excited states involving Tyr185 and Trp86 as electron donors to the retinal protonated Schiff base. These CT states are energetically close to the retinal S1 state and may play a role in funneling energy to the isomerization coordinate.

### 1.29 A ground state structure with individual atom resolution

The bR ground state structure at 1.29 A resolution (PDB 5ZIL), obtained with low-dose X-ray data collection at 15 K to suppress radiation damage, enables visualization of individual atoms. The structure includes a previously unmodeled EF loop (Thr157-Glu161) and reveals the true twist of the retinal polyene chain, differing from previous lower-resolution models. Multiple conformations were observed for 8 residues (e.g., Leu15, Met209) and for the proton release group (Glu194/Glu204).

### Accurate hydrogen bonding in the proton pathway

The high-resolution structure reveals accurate hydrogen bonding geometries in the proton transfer pathway. Water molecules in the proton path (Wat402, Wat403, Wat404, Wat405) show non-tetrahedral coordination that may store energy for proton pumping. A CH...O type hydrogen bond between retinal C14 and Asp212 O-delta1 (3.30 A) influences color tuning through charge distribution on the polyene. The hydrogen bond distances between Asp96-Thr46 (2.66 A) and Asp115-Thr90 (2.50 A) are significantly longer than in 1C3W.

### Dual conformations of the proton release group

Glu194 and Glu204 in the proton release region show two conformations with occupancies 0.55 and 0.45. The Glu194-Glu204 distance is 2.40 A (major) and 2.67 A (minor). The unusually short 2.40 A distance in the major conformation is consistent with a low-barrier hydrogen bond. Three water molecules (Wat403, Wat404, Wat405) also adopt dual positions in relation to the Glu194/Glu204 conformations.

### Lipid-mediated stabilization of the BR trimer

The 2.9 A crystal structure of the BR-lipid complex revealed six haloarchaeal lipids bound in the BR trimer. Three single phytanols occupy a hydrophobic crevice between helices AB and DE of neighboring BR monomers on the cytosolic side. Three additional lipids face the extracellular side forming a continuous patch in the central compartment of the BR trimer. The glycolipid S-TGA-1 (3-HSO3-Galp-beta1-6Manp-alpha1-2Glcp-alpha-1-archaeol) binds specifically at the interface of two BR monomers, with its triglycoside head group intercalating the BC loops. Key hydrogen bonds between T67, Y64, L66 and the glycoside sugars, plus a salt bridge between the sulfate group and K129, provide specific lipid-protein recognition. The lipid positions explain the crucial role of S-TGA-1 for purple membrane stability (PDB 1BRR).

### Local membrane thinning by the central lipid patch

The BR trimer partitions the lipid bilayer into two discontinuous compartments: a central one with space for six lipids and an outer bulk phase with 24 lipids per trimer. The head-group boundary of the central S-TGA-1 lipid patch is shifted by more than 5 A toward the membrane center compared to the bulk lipid phase, creating a region of local membrane thinning. This arises from the unique interactions between the BR trimer and S-TGA-1. The membrane thinning causes a steeper electric field gradient across the membrane, which may affect the proton pathway in BR. This phenomenon suggests that internal membrane patches with unique compositions and physicochemical characteristics may be widespread among oligomeric membrane proteins such as light-harvesting complexes and F0 portions of F-type ATPases.
