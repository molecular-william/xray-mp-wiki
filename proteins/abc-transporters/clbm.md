# ClbM — MATE Transporter from Escherichia coli

## Overview

ClbM is a MATE (Multidrug and Toxic Compound Extrusion) family transporter from Escherichia coli involved in the export of precolibactin, the genotoxin produced by the pks pathogenicity island. ClbM is localized to the inner membrane and transports the large precolibactin molecule from the cytoplasm to the periplasm, where it is cleaved by ClbP to generate the active genotoxin colibactin. The crystal structure of ClbM (2.7 Å) reveals a MATE transporter fold with a large binding pocket capable of accommodating the bulky precolibactin substrate. A rubidium-bound structure (3.3 Å) shows cation coordination through π-cation interactions with Tyr277 and Phe292.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### MATE family transporter structure

ClbM adopts the MATE transporter fold with 12 transmembrane helices. Structural alignment with [Pfmate](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/pfmate/) (PDB 3VVN), DinF, [Norm Vc](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/norM-vc/) (PDB 3MKT), and NorM-NG reveals conserved topology. The binding pocket of ClbM is much larger than those of [Pfmate](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/pfmate/) or [Norm Vc](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/norM-vc/), consistent with its role in transporting the bulky precolibactin molecule.

### Cation binding via π-cation interactions

The rubidium-bound structure (4Z3P) shows that ClbM coordinates Rb+ through π-cation interactions with Tyr277 and Phe292, similar to the mechanism observed in [Norm Vc](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/norM-vc/). This cation-binding site is conserved among MATE transporters and is important for the transport mechanism.

### Role in colibactin export and genotoxicity

ClbM transports precolibactin from the cytoplasm across the inner membrane to the periplasm, where the serine protease ClbP cleaves it to generate active colibactin. Disruption of clbM reduces colibactin production and attenuates genotoxicity in host cells, as measured by γH2AX staining and comet assays. ClbM function is not required for bacterial growth, attachment, or invasion.
