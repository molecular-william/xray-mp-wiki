# MexA multidrug efflux pump periplasmic adaptor

## Overview

MexA is the periplasmic adaptor (membrane fusion protein) component of the MexAB-OprM tripartite multidrug efflux pump from Pseudomonas aeruginosa. The crystal structure was determined at 3.0 A resolution, revealing an elongated molecule composed of three linearly arranged subdomains: a 47 A alpha-helical hairpin, a lipoyl domain, and a six-stranded beta-barrel. In the crystal, hairpins of neighboring MexA monomers pack side-by-side to form twisted arcs. Based on the structure and packing, a model was proposed for the key periplasmic interaction between the outer membrane channel OprM and the adaptor protein in the assembled drug efflux pump, where a ring of nine MexA molecules forms a sheath around the alpha-helical barrel of OprM, potentially inducing or stabilizing the open state.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Elongated modular architecture of the adaptor protein

MexA adopts an elongated sickle-shaped structure with three linearly arranged subdomains. The N-terminal alpha-helical hairpin is approximately 47 A long and contains four heptad repeats per helix. The central lipoyl domain resembles the biotinyl/lipoyl carrier domains found in 2-oxo acid dehydrogenase complexes. The C-terminal six-stranded beta-barrel completes the structure. The modular architecture allows adaptor proteins to use a conserved alpha-helical hairpin to mediate interactions with conserved outer membrane exit ducts (OprM/TolC family), while using the variable-length C-terminal domains to interact with diverse inner membrane transporters.

### Crystal packing and oligomeric state

In the crystal, the alpha-helical hairpins of neighboring MexA monomers pack side-by-side to form twisted arcs, with 13 copies of MexA in the asymmetric unit showing essentially the same conformation (maximum rmsd 1.17 A, 230 C-alpha atoms). A modeled ring of nine MexA monomers is sufficiently large to form a sheath around the open-state model of the TolC alpha-helical barrel. The conserved nature of residues mediating interactions between helical hairpins suggests that formation of such a ring might be a conserved mechanism for adaptor protein function. The physiological oligomeric state remains uncertain, with evidence for dimeric, hexameric, and trimeric forms from various studies.

### Model for tripartite efflux pump assembly

Based on the MexA structure and its packing in the crystal, a model was proposed for the assembled MexAB-OprM efflux pump. A ring of nine MexA molecules could form a sheath around the alpha-helical barrel of the OprM outer membrane channel, bridging the inner membrane transporter [MEXB](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/mexb/) and the outer membrane channel OprM. During dynamic assembly, the hairpins of MexA could pack against the coiled-coils of the OprM alpha-helical barrel, inducing or stabilizing the open state. This ring would also surround the inner membrane transporter and provide a seal to stabilize the complex, generating a conduit spanning the entire periplasm. The proposed assembly would result in a transient 870-kDa transenvelope complex with a continuous export pathway over 270 A in length.

### Original 2.4 A crystal structure of MexA reveals spiral 13-mer assembly

The first crystal structure of MexA (Akama et al., JBC 2004) was determined at 2.40 A resolution by single isomorphous replacement using a mercury derivative. Crystals of full-length MexA with N-terminal fatty acylation and Azu-His6 tag were grown by hanging-drop vapor diffusion using PEG 1000 and Jeffamine-M600 as precipitant. The structure revealed an unforeseen spiral assembly of six and seven protomers joined at one end by a pseudo 2-fold axis forming a 13-mer. The hexamer and heptamer each form a funnel-like structure with a tubular rod at one side and a widely opened flared top at the other. The tridecamer is shaped like a woven rattan cylinder with a large internal tubular space and widely opened flared ends.

### Three-domain architecture of the MexA monomer

Each MexA protomer consists of three visible domains: (i) an N-terminal alpha-helical hairpin (alpha-domain, residues 74-102 and 108-133) forming a left-hand twisted coiled-coil approximately 47 A long; (ii) a central globular beta-domain consisting of eight short beta-sheets with topology similar to biotinyl/lipoyl carrier proteins; and (iii) a C-terminal globular alpha+beta-domain composed of seven short beta-strands and one short alpha-helix. A fourth disordered domain containing the N- and C-terminal regions was poorly resolved. The alpha-helical hairpin shows a turn of about +60 degrees relative to the beta-domain, giving the monomer a sickle shape.

### Assembly models for the MexAB-OprM tripartite pump

Based on the MexA structure, two models were proposed for the MexAB-OprM assembly. In the sleeve model, MexA molecules form a sleeve around the distal domains of MexB, with the alpha-helical domains interacting with OprM. In the 3-fold MexA-dimer model, three pairs of MexA dimers interact with the OprM trimer, with the alpha-helical hairpin contacting the OprM periplasmic alpha-barrel domain at a 20-degree helix contact angle, the central beta-domain bridging MexB and OprM at their contact site, and the alpha+beta-domain interacting with the distal domain of MexB. The latter model is supported by subunit stoichiometry data showing 5-6 MexA molecules per MexB-OprM trimer in intact cells.
