# Thermus thermophilus A-ATPase Peripheral Stalk (PS2)

## Overview

The Thermus thermophilus A-ATPase peripheral stalk (PS2) is a heterodimer of subunits E and G that serves as a stator component of the A-type ATPase/synthase complex. It counteracts the torque generated by rotation of the central stalk during ATP synthesis or hydrolysis. The crystal structure at 2.25 A resolution reveals an unusual right-handed coiled-coil architecture with a hendecad (11-mer) repeat at the N-terminus transitioning to a quindecad (15-mer) repeat at the C-terminus, enabling the stalk to accommodate bending and twisting motions that complement the wobbling of the catalytic headgroup during rotary catalysis.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Right-handed coiled-coil architecture enables peripheral stalk flexibility

The peripheral stalk tail consists of a right-handed parallel coiled-coil formed by subunit G and the N-terminus of subunit E, extending 120 Angstroms. The N-terminal region features a hendecad (11-mer) repeat producing a large pitch and nearly parallel helices, while the C-terminal region switches to a quindecad (15-mer) repeat at residue Ala66 of subunit G, tightening the pitch. This arrangement creates an elbow-like kink in subunit G that brings its C-terminus to the opposite side of subunit E relative to the N-terminus.

### Flexion accommodates radial wobbling of catalytic head

The peripheral stalk can flex primarily in a plane corresponding to a radial direction in the intact A-ATPase. The N-terminal hendecad repeat region, not in contact with the (AB)3 head, allows flexing in a purely radial direction where inertia is least, due to decreased cross-sectional area. This flexion accommodates the radial wobbling movement of the catalytic (AB)3 head as it progresses through its catalytic cycle.

### Twisting constrains azimuthal motion for torque counteraction

The quindecad region of the coiled-coil causes the stalk to twist as it flexes, owing to its increased pitch in the region contacting subunit B. This twisting allows B-peripheral stalk subunit contacts to remain constant during catalysis, stabilizing the complex during rotation. The stalk is at least 10-fold stiffer azimuthally than its rotor counterpart, enabling it to counteract torque generated by central stalk rotation.

### Swivelling head domain complements catalytic head wobbling

The tighter pitch at the C-terminus, accentuated by the elbow in subunit G, allows swivelling of the peripheral stalk head, much like a cardanic pendulum. This swivelling accommodates the tilting of the catalytic domain as the (AB)3 head wobbles through its catalytic states. Normal mode analysis confirms that mode 7 corresponds to flexion and mode 8 to twisting, both in the direction that accommodates the intact complex wobbling.

### Inherent flexibility of peripheral stalk subunit sequences

The crystal structures of PS1 and PS2 were derived from protein expressed and crystallized in the absence of the intact complex, yet their movements follow the precession movement of the L-ring closely. This indicates that the flexibility is inherent within the sequences of the peripheral stalk subunits, not induced by the intact complex context.
