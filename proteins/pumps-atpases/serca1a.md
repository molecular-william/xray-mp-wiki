# SERCA1a (Sarcoplasmic Reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase 1a)

## Overview

SERCA1a is the Ca²⁺-ATPase of fast-twitch skeletal muscle sarcoplasmic reticulum. It is a P-type ATPase that pumps calcium ions from the cytoplasm back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum using ATP hydrolysis, thereby relaxing muscle cells. The enzyme consists of a single 994-residue polypeptide chain with ten transmembrane helices and three cytoplasmic domains (A, N, and P). This was the first high-resolution structure of a P-type ATPase, revealing the mechanism of Ca²⁺ binding and large domain motions during the transport cycle. A landmark 2.4-Å-resolution structure of the Ca²⁺-free E2 state stabilized by BHQ (dibutyldihydroxybenzene) plus thapsigargin (PDB 2AGV) visualized water and phospholipid molecules in the transmembrane region, showing that a phospholipid occupies the V-shaped cleft between M2 and M4 as a wedge. Continuum electrostatic calculations with explicit hydrogens revealed that H⁺ countertransport is a consequence of the requirement to maintain structural integrity of the empty Ca²⁺-binding sites — cation countertransport is likely mandatory for all P-type ATPases. Later crystal structures of vanadate-inhibited SERCA1a in the E2 state (PDB 5A3Q, 5A3R, 5A3S) at 3.05 Å resolution revealed orthovanadate bound as a trigonal bipyramidal dephosphorylation intermediate and identified a novel chloride-binding site near the catalytic site. Additional structures include the E2·AMPPCP complex with a thapsigargin derivative bearing a 12-Boc-aminododecanoyl extension (PDB 2by4, 1.7 Å resolution), revealing accommodation of bulky substitution at O-8 between TM helices M3–M5, and the co-crystal structure of the selective SERCA inhibitor CAD204520 bound to SERCA at 3.4 Å, showing ligand binding at the membrane interface between helices M1–M4. A landmark study using X-ray solvent contrast modulation resolved the entire first layer of phospholipids surrounding the transmembrane helices of SERCA in four different states (E1·2Ca²⁺, E1·AlF₄⁻·ADP·2Ca²⁺, E2·AlF₄⁻(TG), and E2(TG); PDB 5XA7-5XAB), revealing that the entire protein tilts during the reaction cycle, governed by a belt of Trp residues functioning as membrane floats, and that Arg side chains extend from the cytoplasm to exploit phospholipids as anchors for conformational switching.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Ten transmembrane helices with Ca²⁺-binding sites surrounded by M4-M6 and M8

The transmembrane region comprises ten α-helices (M1–M10). The two Ca²⁺-binding sites are located side by side, 5.7 Å apart, and surrounded by four transmembrane helices (M4, M5, M6, and M8). Helices M4 and M6 are unwound for efficient coordination geometry. M5 is located at the center of the molecule and extends from the lumenal surface to the center of cytosolic domain P.

### Two high-affinity Ca²⁺-binding sites with distinct coordination geometries

Site I is located between M5 and M6 helices with contributions from M8. It is coordinated by Asn 768, Glu 771 (M5), Thr 799, Asp 800 (M6), and Glu 908 (M8) side-chain oxygen atoms. Site II is formed almost on helix M4 by main-chain carbonyl oxygen atoms of Val 304, Ala 305, and Ile 307 (M4) and side-chain oxygen atoms of Asn 796, Asp 800 (M6), and Glu 309 (M4). The PEGL motif in the unwound region of M4 is a key motif. Asp 800 is coordinated to both Ca²⁺ atoms, making its correct positioning critical.

### Three cytoplasmic domains (A, N, P) with distinct functions

The cytoplasmic headpiece consists of three well-separated domains. Domain P (central) contains the phosphorylation site Asp 351 and has a Rossman fold with a seven-stranded parallel β-sheet, resembling L-2-haloacid dehalogenase (HAD). Domain N (largest) binds the adenosine moiety of nucleotides (e.g., TNP-AMP) in a positively charged pocket. Domain A (smallest, ~16 K) comprises ~150 residues forming a distorted jelly roll structure and contains the conserved TGES motif (Thr 181) at its edge.

### Large domain motions during the transport cycle

Comparison with a low-resolution electron density map of the enzyme in the absence of Ca²⁺ (tubular crystals with decavanadate) reveals large domain movements. Domain A undergoes the largest movement, nearly a 90° rotation. Domain N comes close to domain P in the phosphorylated states. The phosphorylation site (Asp 351) is more than 25 Å away from the bound nucleotide, indicating that domain closure must occur during ATP hydrolysis. The hinge region between domains N and P involves Gly 354 and the DPPR motif.

### Helix unwinding enables Ca²⁺ coordination and transport pathway

The unwinding of transmembrane helices serves two purposes: first, to realize efficient Ca²⁺ coordination geometry; second, to provide rows of oxygen atoms for guiding Ca²⁺ to the binding sites. The unwound parts of M4 (Pro 312 to Glu 309) and M6 (Gly 801 and Asp 800) form rows of exposed main-chain carbonyl oxygen atoms pointing toward the cytoplasm, creating a hydrophilic pathway to the Ca²⁺-binding sites. M4 unwinds between Ile 307 and Gly 310, and M6 unwinds around Asp 800 and Gly 801.

### Ca²⁺ entry and exit pathways

Ca²⁺ entry is proposed to occur through a cavity surrounded by M2, M4, and M6, which is water accessible with seven water molecules visible. The outlet of Ca²⁺ is likely located in the area surrounded by M3–M5. Both pathways feature rows of exposed oxygen atoms that displace water molecules bound to Ca²⁺, which are bound much more tightly than to monovalent cations.

### Decavanadate inhibition mechanism

In the absence of Ca²⁺, decavanadate (V₁₀O₂₈⁶⁻) binds in a groove between domains N and P, acting like a ball in jaws preventing the jaws from closing. It binds near Asp 351 and prevents domain closure. Arg 489, Lys 492 (domain N), and Arg 678 participate in decavanadate binding. This explains its inhibitory effect on ATPase activity by locking the enzyme in an open conformation.

### Loop L67 connects transmembrane domain to phosphorylation domain

The lumenal loop connecting helices M6 and M7 (L67) runs along the bottom of domain P and transmits signals between the phosphorylation site and the Ca²⁺-binding sites, which are separated by ~50 Å. Extensive hydrogen bonds exist between this loop and various helices. L67 is directly connected to M6, and changes induced in M6 by Ca²⁺ binding are transmitted to domain P through L67 and the P1 or P2 helix. The cytoplasmic half of M6 may undergo rotation or winding/unwinding during Ca²⁺ transport.

### Modulatory ATP binding mode in the E2 state

Crystal structures of the calcium-free E2 state of SERCA stabilized by thapsigargin and the ATP analog AMPPCP reveal ATP bound in a modulatory mode uncoupled from the Asp351 phosphorylation site. The Glu439 side chain interacts with AMPPCP via an Mg²⁺ ion. This E2-ATP state represents an initial stage of ATP-modulated deprotonation of E2, preceding Ca²⁺ ion binding. The structure shows a compact arrangement between N- and P-domains mediated by modulatory ATP, leading to exposure of buried M3-M4 and M5-M6 segments on the cytoplasmic side. A putative phosphate release tunnel appears upon dislocation of the TGES motif from the phosphorylation site.

### E309Q mutant reveals Ca²⁺ binding at both sites without cooperativity

The SERCA1a E309Q mutant, in which Glu309 (a key residue in Ca²⁺ site II) is replaced by glutamine, was found to bind two Ca²⁺ ions with micromolar affinity but without cooperativity, contrary to previous studies suggesting it binds only one Ca²⁺ ion. The mutant adopts a catalytically incompetent Ca2E1 conformation with defective A-domain positioning and loss of the M1 helix kink, providing insight into the signal transmission mechanism between Ca²⁺ binding sites and the catalytic site.

### E2 state helix rearrangements: six transmembrane helices undergo large-scale movements

In the calcium-free E2 state, six of the ten transmembrane helices (M1–M6) exhibit large-scale rearrangements. M1 and M2 move upward toward the cytoplasm (+z direction) while M3 and M4 shift downwards, with displacements up to 5 Å. M1 shows a unique lateral movement within the membrane plane. M3 and M5 are strongly curved in opposite directions. These rearrangements are essential for releasing Ca²⁺ into the lumen and creating a cytoplasmic entry pathway for new Ca²⁺ ions.

### P domain coordinates transmembrane helix movements via flexible joints

The P domain functions as a coordinator of transmembrane helix movements. M3–M5 helices are directly linked to the P domain by hydrogen bonds: M5 integrates into the Rossmann fold, M3 connects through the P1 helix at the bottom of the P domain, and M6 connects through loop L67 and M5. The pivoting point is around Gly 770 on M5. Flexible joints between the P domain and helices allow adjustments that prevent steric clashes.

### SLN-bound SERCA reveals E1 intermediate with exposed Ca2+ sites

The crystal structure of rabbit SERCA1a in complex with sarcolipin (SLN) at 3.1 A resolution reveals a previously undescribed E1 intermediate state (E1-SLN). The SLN helix binds in a groove between transmembrane helices M2, M6, and M9 of SERCA, trapping the Ca2+-ATPase in this E1 state with exposure of the Ca2+ binding sites through an open cytoplasmic pathway stabilized by Mg2+.

### Orthovanadate marks a trigonal bipyramidal dephosphorylation intermediate

Vanadate binds at the catalytic site as planar VO₃⁻ in a trigonal bipyramidal phosphate transition-state-like conformation, coordinated between Asp351 (the residue phosphorylated by ATP) and a water molecule, flanked by Mg²⁺ and three water molecules. This validates the associative transfer mechanism for dephosphorylation. Anomalous difference Fourier maps at wavelengths of 0.98 and 2.21 Å confirmed the vanadium position and excluded the possibility of pyrovanadate (PDB 5A3Q, 5A3R).

### Chloride-binding site near the catalytic site

A previously undescribed chloride-binding site was identified in the P domain near conserved residues Asn628 and Arg678, confirmed by anomalous difference Fourier analysis (6.0 σ peak). The site is connected via the protein backbone to the conserved ⁶²⁵TGD motif and may stabilize the E2·P dephosphorylation transition state. Re-examination of earlier E2·AlF₄⁻ and E2·MgFₓ structures showed similar electron density previously assigned as water.

### TNP-nucleotides reveal dual binding modes in E2P-like conformations

TNP-AMPPCP binding was compared in the E2·VO₃⁻ (E2·P transition state analog) and E2·BeF₃⁻ (E2P ground state analog) structures. The TNP moiety occupies the adenine-binding pocket in both states, but the remainder of the nucleotide is rotated by ~180° between the two structures. This dual binding mode reflects the higher nucleotide affinity in the transition-state conformation (E2·VO₃⁻) compared to the ground state (E2·BeF₃⁻), with KD values 2-10 fold lower in the transition state.

### A bulky substitution at O-8 fits between transmembrane helices M3–M5

The crystal structure of thapsigargin derivative 2 (8-O-N-tert-butoxycarbonyl-12-aminododecanoyl-8-O-debutanoylthapsigargin) bound to SERCA (PDB 2by4) showed that the methylene groups of the long 12-Boc-aminododecanoyl chain are located between transmembrane helices M3, M4 and M5. The flexible chain adopts a conformation adjusted between the transmembrane sections. The Boc-group sits in a cavity between M1–M2 and M3–M4, exposed to solvent, explaining why this bulky substitution is tolerated. Local adjustments include movement of Leu253 and Ile315 side chains to accommodate the extension.

### CAD204520 binds at the membrane interface between M1–M4 helices

The selective SERCA inhibitor CAD204520 was co-crystallized with SERCA and the structure determined at 3.4 Å resolution. CAD204520 binds to a groove at the membrane interface between transmembrane helices M1, M2, M3, and M4, forming two key polar interactions: 2.9 Å to Asp59 on M1 and 2.7 Å to Asn101 on M2, plus several hydrophobic interactions with residues including Phe256, Pro308, Pro312, Ile307, Leu61, and Val62. The binding mode of CAD204520 is distinct from thapsigargin but similar to cyclopiazonic acid (CPA), enabling synergistic effects when combined with thapsigargin. CAD204520 inhibits NOTCH1 signaling in T-ALL via SERCA inhibition without cardiac toxicity in vivo.

### Solvent Contrast Modulation Reveals the Lipid Bilayer Around SERCA

The X-ray solvent contrast modulation technique (doi/10.1038##nature22357) resolved the entire first layer of phospholipids surrounding Ca²⁺-ATPase in four different states. Using iohexol as a contrast medium to modulate solvent electron density, the electron density maps at 4.5 Å resolution revealed 30 phospholipid molecules per protein in the E2(TG) state. Fewer than half of the first-layer phospholipids are hydrogen-bonded to protein residues, and phospholipids follow the movements of associated residues during the reaction cycle, causing local distortions and changes in bilayer thickness.

### Trp Residues Function as Membrane Floats to Govern Protein Tilting

During the reaction cycle, the entire SERCA molecule tilts by approximately 18° to accommodate large perpendicular movements of transmembrane helices (e.g., M2 moves ~10 Å during E1·2Ca²⁺ → E1·ATP·2Ca²⁺). A belt of Trp residues (especially Trp928 and Trp967 at the distal M10 end) remains horizontal in the cytoplasmic leaflet and acts as a membrane float, minimizing the energetic cost of helix movements into and out of different environments. Trp residues sense the water-lipid boundary, analogous to membrane floats.

### Arg Residues Use Phospholipids as Molecular Anchors

Two classes of basic residues interact with phospholipids: (1) Arg/Lys residues that snorkel from within the bilayer and fix particular phospholipids by forming salt bridges (clustering within the M1–M3 area); and (2) Arg residues (e.g., Arg324 on M4) that approach phospholipid head groups from the cytoplasm and change interacting partners during the reaction cycle. Arg324 makes a salt bridge with PL18 in E1·AlF₄⁻·ADP·2Ca²⁺ and with PL19/PL21 in E2·AlF₄⁻(TG). Mutation of Arg324 to Glu inhibits the E1P → E2P transition while accelerating E2 → E1. These Arg residues exploit phospholipids as anchors for conformational switching, a role distinct from the membrane-float function of Trp.

### Bilayer Distortions and Detergent Islands in SERCA Crystals

The lipid bilayers in SERCA crystals are far from flat due to dispositions of adjacent Ca²⁺-ATPase molecules and the presence of detergent (C₁₂E₈). The distance of the density maximum (phosphate position) from the bilayer centre varies by ~9 Å within a single leaflet. Islands with particularly small zp values (~11 Å) lacking head group density were identified as putative detergent islands. The detergent C₁₂E₈ forms thin bilayer regions that explain the rather thin bilayer observed in earlier SERCA crystals.

### Low-Resolution X-ray Data Reveals Lipid Bilayer Electron Density in SERCA Crystals

Using carefully collected low-resolution X-ray diffraction data (60-8 Å for E2-dBTG, 30-8 Å for E2P) from crystals of SERCA with large lipid-detergent content, electron density features corresponding to the hydrophobic/hydrophilic interfaces of membrane bilayers were visualized. The position of lipid phosphate groups delineates the bilayer boundaries surrounding SERCA, providing direct structural information on the protein's lipid environment in E2 and E2P states. These features were validated by MD simulations of SERCA in crystal-mimicking detergent-containing bilayers, showing excellent agreement with the observed electron density.

### Hydrophobic Matching and Local Bilayer Deformation

SERCA's hydrophobic thickness (21-24 Å, based on Trp and basic residue distribution at membrane interfaces) is significantly narrower than the 28-32 Å typical for many membrane proteins. MD simulations in bilayers of varying hydrophobic thickness [(di-C14:1)PC (23 Å), POPC (29 Å), SGPC (32 Å)] revealed that SERCA adapts to different membrane environments through local bilayer deformations (±4 Å deviations in leaflet interface position) and small protein rearrangements (side-chain rotations, helix tilt changes). In (di-C14:1)PC thin membranes, defective ATPase function was observed, while POPC (29 Å) provided the most optimal environment, balancing hydrophobic coverage against the need for conformational flexibility during the transport cycle.

### Mutual Adaptation Enables Conformational Transitions

The apparent hydrophobic mismatch between SERCA's narrow hydrophobic span and the thicker POPC bilayer is functionally important. The flexible, adaptive protein-lipid interface allows the 5 Å up-down translocations of transmembrane helices during E1-to-E2 conformational changes without significant energy barriers from hydrophobic/hydrophilic exposure. In thin (di-C14:1)PC membranes, the reduced hydrophobic coverage imposes a 'straitjacket' effect, severely reducing the rate of E2P dephosphorylation and limiting Ca2+ transport to one instead of two ions per ATPase cycle. This strategy of mutual adaptation is likely of general nature for many membrane proteins.

### \"E2 → E1 Transition Triggered by Deprotonation of Gating Residue Glu309\"

Crystal structures of SERCA1a Glu309Gln (2.5 Å) and Glu309Ala (3.4 Å) mutants under E2 conditions (with thapsigargin) revealed unexpected large structural rearrangements while maintaining the E2 state. The Glu309Gln mutant shows an open headpiece, nearly straight M5 helix, and an A-domain rotated ~125° (even beyond the E1·Mg²⁺ position), yet the Ca²⁺-coordinating residues remain in E2 positions. Combined with quantum chemical calculations, this reveals the E2→E1 transition mechanism: (i) Deprotonation of Glu309 opens the M4 hinge by electrostatic repulsion, opening the headpiece; (ii) it pulls Asn796 toward Val304, destroying one of two hydrogen bonds between Glu771 (M5) and Asn796 (M6), unlocking the latch; (iii) anticlockwise rotation of the unwound M6 part brings Thr799 and Asp800 into the Ca²⁺-binding cavity; (iv) M4C inclines toward M3, detaching from M5/M6; (v) M5 straightens, bringing M4 upward to the E1 position. Key insight: deprotonation of Glu309 is the first chemical event in E2→E1.

### SERCA1a structure solved by vanadium SAD phasing at 3.13 A

The SERCA1a·VO₃⁻ complex was used as a test case for vanadium SAD (V-SAD) phasing. Data collected on beamline I23 at Diamond Light Source at lambda=2.2604 A exploited the vanadium K-edge anomalous signal (f'' ~4 e-). Despite a ratio of only one vanadium per 994 residues, both V atoms in the asymmetric unit (two monomers) were located by SHELXD with strong anomalous peaks (33.8 and 30.2 sigma). A high redundancy of 77 was required to obtain interpretable electron-density maps from CRANK2, which also located sulfur sites essential for phasing. The final structure (PDB 6yso) had an all-atom r.m.s.d. of 0.6 A compared with the previously reported SERCA·VO₃⁻ structure determined by molecular replacement, validating the V-SAD approach.

### E1·Mg2+ intermediate state fills a gap in the reaction cycle

The crystal structure of SERCA1a in the E1·Mg2+ state (native, with bound sarcolipin) at 3.0 Å resolution fills a gap in the structural elucidation of the P-type ATPase reaction cycle. In this state, Mg2+ occupies Ca2+-binding site II, coordinated by the main-chain carbonyl of Ala305. The high-affinity Ca2+-binding sites are only half-formed, preparing to accept new cytosolic Ca2+. The A domain is rotated ~30° around a horizontal axis compared to E1·2Ca2+. Sarcolipin stabilizes this E1·Mg2+ state by binding in a groove between M2, M6, and M9, interfering with transition to E1·2Ca2+.

### Sarcolipin binding mechanism revealed by E1·Mg2+ structure

Sarcolipin (SLN), a 31-residue regulatory membrane protein, binds SERCA1a in a groove surrounded by transmembrane helices M2, M6, and M9. The groove is particularly narrow (~9 Å) near the cytoplasmic surface around Thr5 of SLN. This groove becomes too narrow (<6 Å) in E1·2Ca2+ and too wide (>20 Å) in E2, explaining why E1·Mg2+ is the preferred state for SLN binding. SLN stabilizes the E1·Mg2+ state and decreases the apparent Ca2+ affinity of SERCA by lowering the free energy difference between Ca2+-bound and Ca2+-unbound states.
