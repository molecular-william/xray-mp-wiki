# Human CD81 Tetraspanin

## Overview

CD81 (also known as TAPA-1 or tspan28) is a human tetraspanin protein belonging to the TM4SF family. It is a four-pass transmembrane protein that plays critical roles in B-cell biology, forming a multi-protein complex with CD19, CD21, and CD225 to regulate B cell receptor function. CD81 is also a well-known host factor for Hepatitis C virus entry. This structure represents the first full-length tetraspanin structure, revealing a unique transmembrane fold and a cholesterol-binding pocket.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Unique waffle cone architecture

The CD81 structure resembles a waffle cone with the EC2 domain covering an intramembrane cavity bounded by four transmembrane helices. The transmembrane helices fold as two largely separated pairs of antiparallel helices (TM1/TM2 and TM3/TM4), with the pairs only converging near the cytoplasmic side of the membrane through contacts between TM2 and TM3. This fold contrasts with early modeling predictions of a tightly associated four-helix bundle and is evolutionarily conserved among tetraspanins as confirmed by evolutionary coupling analysis.

### Intramembrane cholesterol-binding pocket

The central cavity bounded by the four transmembrane helices and the bottom face of EC2 encloses approximately 3300 A^3. A cholesterol molecule is bound within this hydrophobic pocket, with specific contacts including hydrophobic residues (F21 of TM1, I64/V68/V71/M72/V75 of TM2, F94/L98/L101 of TM3, V212/M216 of TM4) and polar hydrogen bonds between cholesterol hydroxyl and N18/TM1, E219/TM4. These 13 amino acid contact residues are nearly 100% conserved across 37 available tetraspanin homologs.

### Cholesterol binding modulates CD81 function

Radioactive binding assays show CD81 specifically binds cholesterol with ~15-fold enrichment over control. The E219A and E219Q mutations reduce cholesterol binding by ~50%, confirming E219 as a critical polar contact. Molecular dynamics simulations reveal that cholesterol-bound CD81 maintains a closed conformation, while cholesterol-free CD81 transitions to an open conformation where EC2 disengages from the transmembrane domain. Mutations that compromise cholesterol binding (E219A, E219Q) enhance CD19 surface delivery, suggesting cholesterol stabilizes a closed state that disfavors partner protein binding.

### Conformational dynamics of EC2

Molecular dynamics simulations identify an "open" conformation of CD81 where EC2 separates from the transmembrane domain. This transition involves breaking a salt bridge between D196 on EC2 and K201 on TM4, leading to extension of TM4. The open conformation is observed when cholesterol is absent from the binding pocket but not when cholesterol is bound. This suggests cholesterol binding stabilizes the closed conformation and that tetraspanins may regulate partner protein binding through cholesterol-dependent conformational changes.
