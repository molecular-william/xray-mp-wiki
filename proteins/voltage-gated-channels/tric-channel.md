# Prokaryotic TRIC Trimeric Intracellular Cation Channels

## Overview

TRIC (trimeric intracellular cation) channels are monovalent cation channels localized at the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) and endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where they balance membrane potentials generated by Ca2+ release and uptake. The prokaryotic TRIC channels from Rhodobacter sphaeroides (RsTRIC) and Sulfolobus solfataricus (SsTRIC) were structurally characterized, revealing a trimeric architecture with each subunit containing seven transmembrane (TM) helices organized in two inverted repeats (N-repeat: TM1-TM3, C-repeat: TM4-TM6 plus TM7). Each subunit contains a monomeric ion-conducting pore, and trimer formation contributes to protein stability. The TM2 and TM5 helices are kinked at conserved [Glycine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/glycine/) clusters that are essential for channel activity. Lateral fenestrations at subunit interfaces accommodate lipid molecules.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Trimeric architecture and subunit fold

Each TRIC subunit contains seven TM helices with two inverted repeats
(N-repeat: TM1-TM3; C-repeat: TM4-TM6) related by pseudo-twofold symmetry.
The TM2 and TM5 helices are kinked at conserved [Glycine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/glycine/) clusters (Gly45/Gly46
in TM2, Gly130/Gly132/Gly133/Gly134 in TM5 of SsTRIC). The trimer is
stabilized by symmetrical inter-subunit interactions mediated by TM2 and TM5
helices on both periplasmic and cytoplasmic sides.

### Monomeric ion-conducting pore

Each TRIC subunit contains an independent ion-conducting pore, demonstrated
by a monomeric mutant (L52W/I134W/L138W/L139W/I153F in RsTRIC) that retains
channel activity in patch-clamp analysis. The monomeric pore is solvent-accessible
on the periplasmic side and closed on the cytoplasmic side in the resting state.
Two in-line phenylalanine residues (Phe16/Phe104 in SsTRIC) constrict the pore
on the periplasmic side.

### Glycine clusters are essential for channel activity

The conserved [Glycine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/glycine/) clusters in TM2 (N-kink) and TM5 (C-kink) are critical
for channel function. Both N-kink (G45A/G47A) and C-kink (G132A/G133A)
mutants of RsTRIC showed no measurable channel activity in single-channel
recordings. The kink motions at these [Glycine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/glycine/) clusters are proposed to drive
channel opening through expansion of the transmembrane domain.

### Lipid-filled lateral fenestrations

Three lateral fenestrations at each subunit interface provide access from
the membrane to the center of the trimer. These highly hydrophobic
fenestrations (~20 A long) accommodate acyl chains of lipid molecules.
MD simulations confirmed stable lipid occupancy in the fenestrations.
Notably, similar lateral fenestrations exist in voltage-sensing Na+ channels
and mechanosensitive K2P channels, suggesting a conserved lipid modulation
mechanism.

### Comparison with eukaryotic TRIC channels

Despite low sequence identity (~10-15%), prokaryotic and eukaryotic TRIC
channels share conserved overall architecture (trimer, 7-TM subunit, monomeric
pore). Differences include: (1) eukaryotic TRIC channels lack the lateral
fenestrations and instead bind [PIP2](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/phosphatidylinositol-4-5-bisphosphate/) near the cytoplasmic side via a KEVXRXXK
motif; (2) eukaryotic TM2/TM5 helices are more kinked at the cytoplasmic
side, rendering the cytoplasmic pore solvent-accessible. These differences
may represent early conformational changes during channel activation.
