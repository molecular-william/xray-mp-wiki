# GtrB Polyisoprenyl-Phosphate Glycosyltransferase from Synechocystis

## Overview

GtrB is a polyisoprenyl-phosphate glycosyltransferase (PI-GT) from the cyanobacterium Synechocystis sp. PCC6803 that catalyzes the transfer of [Glucose](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/additives/glucose/) from [Udp [Glucose](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/additives/glucose/)](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/udp-glucose/) to undecaprenyl phosphate (UndP), generating UndP-glucose. This reaction represents the first step of glycosylation in bacterial cell wall O-antigen serotype conversion. The 3.0 Angstrom resolution crystal structure reveals that GtrB forms a tetramer, with each protomer contributing two transmembrane helices to a membrane-spanning bundle. The soluble glycosyltransferase (GT) domain is of the GT-A fold and is connected to the transmembrane region by a juxtamembrane region composed of two amphipathic helices (JM1 and JM2) that position the active site approximately 15 Angstroms from the membrane surface.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Tetrameric architecture of PI-GT family

GtrB forms a tetramer in which each protomer contributes two TM helices to a central membrane-spanning bundle. The soluble GT domains form a stable assembly in the cytosol, with ~900 Angstroms^2 of buried hydrophobic surface between each pair of GT domains, including eight inter-subunit hydrogen bonds and two salt bridges at each interface. A short C-terminal beta-hairpin bridges the interaction between adjacent subunits.

### Active site architecture and catalytic mechanism

The active site is located in the GT-A fold domain approximately 15 Angstroms from the membrane surface. A conserved DXD motif (D94xD96D) coordinates a divalent metal ion (Mg2+ or Mn2+) essential for catalysis. Residues R122 and R200, universally conserved among prokaryotic and eukaryotic PI-GTs, coordinate the phosphate of the lipid acceptor. D157 acts as a putative catalytic acid. GtrB is an inverting glycosyltransferase that does not require a catalytic base because the acceptor phosphate oxygen is already deprotonated under physiological conditions.

### Substrate translocation pathway hypothesis

The GT domain is positioned 15 Angstroms from the membrane, raising the question of how the hydrophobic UndP substrate reaches the active site. Conserved hydrophobic and basic residues in the juxtamembrane region (R122, R125, K132, A136, Y140, R200, L212, A216, R290) may form a shielded pathway for translocation of the polyisoprenyl-phosphate from the membrane to the active site. Mutations at any of these sites led to complete loss of enzymatic activity, supporting their functional importance.

### Relevance to human DPM1 and CDG type 1e

GtrB shares high conservation of key functional residues with human DPM1, the catalytic subunit of dolichol phosphate mannose synthase (DPMS). Disease-causing mutations in human DPM1 (G152V, S248P, R92S) map to corresponding regions in GtrB. Functional assays in a zebrafish dpm1 knockdown model confirmed that mutations at corresponding positions (R122/R200 in GtrB; R147/R234 in DPM1) severely impair function, providing a structural basis for understanding congenital disorders of glycosylation type 1e.
