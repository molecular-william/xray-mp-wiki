# Human CXCR4 Chemokine Receptor

## Overview

CXCR4 is a G protein-coupled chemokine receptor activated exclusively by the chemokine CXCL12 (SDF-1) and couples primarily through Gi proteins. It is a critical regulator of cell migration in immune surveillance, inflammation, and development. CXCR4 has been associated with more than 23 types of cancers, where it promotes metastasis, angiogenesis, and tumor growth. It also serves as a co-receptor for T-tropic HIV-1 viral entry.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Five crystal structures of CXCR4

Five independent crystal structures of CXCR4 were determined bound to the small-molecule antagonist [It1T](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/it1t/) and the cyclic peptide [Cvx15](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/cvx15/) at 2.5 to 3.2 Angstrom resolution.

### Consistent homodimer interface

All structures reveal a consistent homodimer with an interface including helices V and VI that may be involved in regulating signaling.

### Distinctive ligand-binding pocket

The ligand-binding sites are larger, more open, and located closer to the extracellular surface compared to other GPCR structures, with [It1T](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/it1t/) occupying a pocket defined by helices I, II, III, and VII.

### Absence of canonical helix VIII

The CXCR4 structure lacks the canonical helix VIII found in most class A GPCRs; the C-terminal region adopts an extended conformation.

### Disulfide bond architecture

Two disulfide bonds (C28-C274 and C109-C186) constrain the N-terminus and ECL2, critical for ligand binding and conserved in chemokine receptors.

### T240P uncoupling mutation

The T240(6.36)P mutation in construct CXCR4-3 disrupts helix VI structure and abolishes signaling, representing a novel structure-based uncoupling mutation.

### Ligand-induced conformational changes

[Cvx15](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/cvx15/) cyclic peptide binding induces a tilt in helix V, bringing intracellular regions of opposing receptors into close contact, suggesting a mechanism for cooperative binding and allosteric modulation.

### Novel dimerization interface

CXCR4 dimerization involves mainly hydrophobic interactions at helices V and VI involving Leu194, Val197, Val198, Phe201, Met205, and Leu210, distinct from previous GPCR dimer models that suggested contacts through helix I or helices IV and V.

### CXCR4-vMIP-II complex structure at 3.1 Angstrom

The structure of CXCR4 bound to the viral CC chemokine antagonist [vMip Ii](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/vmip-ii/) was determined at 3.1 Angstrom resolution (PDB 4RWS). The complex reveals a 1:1 stoichiometry and a contiguous 1330 Angstrom² binding interface, establishing the three-part chemokine recognition site model (CRS1, CRS1.5, CRS2).

### Chemokine-induced conformational changes

To accommodate chemokine binding, the extracellular half of helix I shifts outward by ~2.4 Angstrom forming an extra alpha-helical turn. The CXCR4 N-terminus adopts an orientation almost perpendicular to the membrane to form a beta-sheet interaction with the chemokine at CRS1.5. ECL2 forms a beta-hairpin more closed onto the binding pocket compared to small-molecule-bound structures.

### Disulfide trapping strategy for complex stabilization

An engineered disulfide bridge between CXCR4(D187C) and [vMip Ii](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/vmip-ii/)(W5C) was used to stabilize the complex for crystallization. This irreversible trapping approach enabled structure determination of a membrane protein complex that was insufficiently stable in detergent for conventional crystallization.

### CC vs CXC chemokine recognition specificity

The structure provides a structural explanation for why CC chemokines bind exclusively to CC receptors and CXC chemokines to CXC receptors. CC chemokines have a straight CC motif that dimerizes via beta-sheet interactions coinciding with CRS1.5, making dimer binding sterically impossible. CXC chemokines have a bent CC motif that directs their N-terminus towards helices V/VI, and their beta1-strand dimerization interface does not overlap with receptor contacts, allowing dimer binding.
