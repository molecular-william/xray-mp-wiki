# ClyA Cytotoxin from Escherichia coli

## Overview

ClyA (also known as HlyE and SheA) is a pore-forming cytotoxin from Escherichia coli that assembles into a large dodecameric transmembrane pore. The crystal structure was determined at 3.3 A resolution, revealing a 12-helix bundle that forms an alpha-helical pore traversing the membrane. The ClyA protomer undergoes a dramatic conformational change from the soluble form to the membrane-inserted protomeric state, where a three-helix bundle transforms into an extended helical rod. This spring-loaded mechanism of pore formation is reminiscent of the conformational changes observed in viral fusion proteins such as influenza hemagglutinin. The pore is approximately 70 A in diameter at the entrance and narrows to about 30 A at the exit.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Spring-loaded pore formation mechanism

ClyA undergoes a dramatic conformational change to form the transmembrane pore. In the soluble form, the protein is a four-helix bundle. Upon membrane insertion, it converts to a protomeric form where three helices (alphaB, alphaC, alphaG) form an extended helical rod. This spring-loaded mechanism is analogous to the conformational changes observed in influenza hemagglutinin (HA), where loop regions between conserved helices refold into extended helices to drive membrane fusion.

### Dodecameric pore assembly

The ClyA pore is a dodecamer (12-mer) composed of 12 protomers arranged with D6 symmetry. The pore has been observed in different oligomeric states by EM, including tridecameric (13-mer) and hexameric forms, suggesting multiple oligomeric states may coexist in solution.

### Structural similarity to Bacillus cereus HBL-B

The helical bundle architecture of the ClyA monomer shows close similarity to the B-component of the B. cereus HBL enterotoxin (HBL-B). The head regions share a common architecture, and the helical bundle platform that undergoes minimal conformational change during pore formation is structurally related.
