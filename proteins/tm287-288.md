# TM287/288 (ABC Exporter from Thermotoga maritima)

## Overview

TM287/288 is a heterodimeric ATP-binding cassette (ABC) exporter from the thermophilic bacterium Thermotoga maritima. It shares 36% sequence identity with LmrCD, a well-characterized heterodimeric ABC exporter from Lactococcus lactis. TM287/288 transports Hoechst 33342 and daunomycin. The crystal structure at 2.9 A resolution reveals an inward-facing conformation with the two nucleotide-binding domains (NBDs) only partially separated, remaining in contact through a conserved D-loop interface. AMP-PNP is bound to the degenerate catalytic site (NBD1/TM287), while the consensus site (NBD2/TM288) features a distorted catalytic dyad. This structure provides unprecedented insights into the mechanism of heterodimeric ABC exporters and the asymmetric nucleotide binding sites characteristic of this class of transporters.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### First heterodimeric ABC exporter structure with asymmetric nucleotide sites

TM287/288 is the first heterodimeric ABC exporter crystallized in its inward-facing state with nonequal ATP binding sites. The degenerate site (NBD1/TM287) binds AMP-PNP, while the consensus site (NBD2/TM288) features a distorted catalytic dyad with Glu517 pointing away from the expected ATP position. The noncanonical D-loop sequence of NBD2 (SNVD instead of SALD) and the noncanonical Asn521 complete the degenerate site architecture.

### Partially separated NBDs with conserved motif interface

Unlike previous inward-facing ABC structures where NBDs are completely separated, TM287/288 NBDs remain in contact through a hydrogen-bond network mediated by the D-loop of NBD2. The D-loop of NBD2 interacts with the Walker A motif and switch Gln526 of NBD1 through a hydrogen bonding network. The D-loop of NBD1 forms two hydrogen bonds with Walker A Thr390 of NBD2. The interaction is weaker at the consensus site, causing a 7-degree tilt between the NBDs.

### Distorted catalytic dyad at consensus site reduces nucleotide affinity

The Walker B motif and D-loop of NBD2 are separated by only two amino acid residues. Glu517 (Walker B glutamate) points away from the gamma-phosphate position of potentially bound ATP. Asp516 (Walker B aspartate) and Gln414 (Q-loop glutamine) are shifted away from expected nucleotide binding position. His548 (switch histidine) forms a hydrogen bond with Asp516. These distortions decrease nucleotide affinity at the consensus site relative to the degenerate site.

### Transport of Hoechst 33342 and daunomycin

TM287/288 transports the DNA-intercalating dye Hoechst 33342 and the anticancer drug daunomycin. Expression in L. lactis increased daunomycin resistance by a factor of 1.6. ATPase activity is stimulated by Hoechst 33342 in membrane vesicles. The apparent Km for ATP is 114 +/- 6 uM (detergent-purified) and 80 +/- 8 uM (reconstituted).

### Nucleotides drive NBD closure and outward-facing transition

Cross-linking experiments with TM287(N105C)-TM288(T486C) double mutant show that ATP and AMP-PNP drive NBD closure and promote the outward-facing conformation. The distance between thiol groups is 21 A in the inward-facing crystal structure and approaches 4 A during NBD closure. Cross-linking occurred only in the presence of nucleotides and oxidizing agent copper phenanthroline.

### Inward-facing cavity opens to cytoplasm

The transmembrane cavity opens to the cytoplasm and is inaccessible from the inner leaflet of the lipid bilayer. The cavity shape is asymmetric due to clustering of bulky side chains in TM6 of TM287. Three xenon binding sites line the cavity surface close to the cytoplasmic entry, indicating hydrophobic patches mainly at the cavity surface of TM287.

### Eukaryotic homologs and clinical relevance

TM287/288 shares 36% sequence identity with LmrCD from L. lactis. The degenerate site architecture is conserved in eukaryotic homologs including CFTR, TAP1/2, and MRP1. The structure provides a framework for understanding multidrug resistance in cancer cells and cystic fibrosis.
