# 

## Overview

The c-subunit of the F-type Na+-dependent ATP synthase from Ilyobacter tartaricus forms the membrane-embedded rotor ring (c-ring) consisting of 11 subunits. Each c-subunit contains two transmembrane alpha helices connected by a short cytoplasmic loop. The c11-ring contains 11 Na+ binding sites located between adjacent subunits. The structure was refined at 2.35 A resolution, revealing a complete ion-coordination structure for each Na+ site: Gln32, Glu65, Ser66, Val63, and a buried structural water molecule coordinated by Thr67. Thr67 is the key residue distinguishing Na+- dependent from H+-dependent ATP synthases.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Na+ coordination structure

Each Na+ binding site in the c11-ring is coordinated by five ligands: the side-chain oxygen of Gln32, the backbone carbonyl of Val63, the side-chain oxygen of Glu65, the hydroxyl oxygen of Ser66 from the adjacent subunit, and a buried structural water molecule. The water is further coordinated by Thr67 and Ala64. This fifth ligand is essential for Na+ selectivity, as free-energy calculations show the site cannot correctly discriminate Na+ from K+ or Li+ without the water molecule.

### Thr67 as the Na+/H+ selectivity determinant

Thr67 is the only residue in the Na+-dependent c-subunit that distinguishes it from H+-dependent ATP synthases. In H+-ATP synthases, this position is occupied by an alanine. MD simulations show that without the coordinating water, Thr67 exchanges between rotamers A and B, destabilizing the binding site. With the water, Thr67 maintains a stable conformation that positions its hydroxyl group to coordinate the water molecule, sustaining the coordination shell structure.
