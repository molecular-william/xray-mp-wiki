# SERCA1a (Sarcoplasmic Reticulum Ca²⁺-ATPase 1a)

## Overview

SERCA1a is the Ca²⁺-ATPase of fast-twitch skeletal muscle sarcoplasmic reticulum. It is a P-type ATPase that pumps calcium ions from the cytoplasm back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum using ATP hydrolysis, thereby relaxing muscle cells. The enzyme consists of a single 994-residue polypeptide chain with ten transmembrane helices and three cytoplasmic domains (A, N, and P). This was the first high-resolution structure of a P-type ATPase, revealing the mechanism of Ca²⁺ binding and large domain motions during the transport cycle.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Ten transmembrane helices with Ca²⁺-binding sites surrounded by M4-M6 and M8

The transmembrane region comprises ten α-helices (M1–M10). The two Ca²⁺-binding sites are located side by side, 5.7 Å apart, and surrounded by four transmembrane helices (M4, M5, M6, and M8). Helices M4 and M6 are unwound for efficient coordination geometry. M5 is located at the center of the molecule and extends from the lumenal surface to the center of cytosolic domain P.

### Two high-affinity Ca²⁺-binding sites with distinct coordination geometries

Site I is located between M5 and M6 helices with contributions from M8. It is coordinated by Asn 768, Glu 771 (M5), Thr 799, Asp 800 (M6), and Glu 908 (M8) side-chain oxygen atoms. Site II is formed almost on helix M4 by main-chain carbonyl oxygen atoms of Val 304, Ala 305, and Ile 307 (M4) and side-chain oxygen atoms of Asn 796, Asp 800 (M6), and Glu 309 (M4). The PEGL motif in the unwound region of M4 is a key motif. Asp 800 is coordinated to both Ca²⁺ atoms, making its correct positioning critical.

### Three cytoplasmic domains (A, N, P) with distinct functions

The cytoplasmic headpiece consists of three well-separated domains. Domain P (central) contains the phosphorylation site Asp 351 and has a Rossman fold with a seven-stranded parallel β-sheet, resembling L-2-haloacid dehalogenase (HAD). Domain N (largest) binds the adenosine moiety of nucleotides (e.g., TNP-AMP) in a positively charged pocket. Domain A (smallest, ~16 K) comprises ~150 residues forming a distorted jelly roll structure and contains the conserved TGES motif (Thr 181) at its edge.

### Large domain motions during the transport cycle

Comparison with a low-resolution electron density map of the enzyme in the absence of Ca²⁺ (tubular crystals with decavanadate) reveals large domain movements. Domain A undergoes the largest movement, nearly a 90° rotation. Domain N comes close to domain P in the phosphorylated states. The phosphorylation site (Asp 351) is more than 25 Å away from the bound nucleotide, indicating that domain closure must occur during ATP hydrolysis. The hinge region between domains N and P involves Gly 354 and the DPPR motif.

### Helix unwinding enables Ca²⁺ coordination and transport pathway

The unwinding of transmembrane helices serves two purposes: first, to realize efficient Ca²⁺ coordination geometry; second, to provide rows of oxygen atoms for guiding Ca²⁺ to the binding sites. The unwound parts of M4 (Pro 312 to Glu 309) and M6 (Gly 801 and Asp 800) form rows of exposed main-chain carbonyl oxygen atoms pointing toward the cytoplasm, creating a hydrophilic pathway to the Ca²⁺-binding sites. M4 unwinds between Ile 307 and Gly 310, and M6 unwinds around Asp 800 and Gly 801.

### Ca²⁺ entry and exit pathways

Ca²⁺ entry is proposed to occur through a cavity surrounded by M2, M4, and M6, which is water accessible with seven water molecules visible. The outlet of Ca²⁺ is likely located in the area surrounded by M3–M5. Both pathways feature rows of exposed oxygen atoms that displace water molecules bound to Ca²⁺, which are bound much more tightly than to monovalent cations.

### Decavanadate inhibition mechanism

In the absence of Ca²⁺, decavanadate (V₁₀O₂₈⁶⁻) binds in a groove between domains N and P, acting like a ball in jaws preventing the jaws from closing. It binds near Asp 351 and prevents domain closure. Arg 489, Lys 492 (domain N), and Arg 678 participate in decavanadate binding. This explains its inhibitory effect on ATPase activity by locking the enzyme in an open conformation.

### Loop L67 connects transmembrane domain to phosphorylation domain

The lumenal loop connecting helices M6 and M7 (L67) runs along the bottom of domain P and transmits signals between the phosphorylation site and the Ca²⁺-binding sites, which are separated by ~50 Å. Extensive hydrogen bonds exist between this loop and various helices. L67 is directly connected to M6, and changes induced in M6 by Ca²⁺ binding are transmitted to domain P through L67 and the P1 or P2 helix. The cytoplasmic half of M6 may undergo rotation or winding/unwinding during Ca²⁺ transport.

### Modulatory ATP binding mode in the E2 state

Crystal structures of the calcium-free E2 state of SERCA stabilized by thapsigargin and the ATP analog AMPPCP reveal ATP bound in a modulatory mode uncoupled from the Asp351 phosphorylation site. The Glu439 side chain interacts with AMPPCP via an Mg²⁺ ion. This E2-ATP state represents an initial stage of ATP-modulated deprotonation of E2, preceding Ca²⁺ ion binding. The structure shows a compact arrangement between N- and P-domains mediated by modulatory ATP, leading to exposure of buried M3-M4 and M5-M6 segments on the cytoplasmic side. A putative phosphate release tunnel appears upon dislocation of the TGES motif from the phosphorylation site.

### E309Q mutant reveals Ca²⁺ binding at both sites without cooperativity

The SERCA1a E309Q mutant, in which Glu309 (a key residue in Ca²⁺ site II) is replaced by glutamine, was found to bind two Ca²⁺ ions with micromolar affinity but without cooperativity, contrary to previous studies suggesting it binds only one Ca²⁺ ion. The mutant adopts a catalytically incompetent Ca2E1 conformation with defective A-domain positioning and loss of the M1 helix kink, providing insight into the signal transmission mechanism between Ca²⁺ binding sites and the catalytic site. The Glu309 mutation severely retards both ATP-dependent phosphorylation and E2P dephosphorylation.

### E2 state helix rearrangements: six transmembrane helices undergo large-scale movements

In the calcium-free E2 state, six of the ten transmembrane helices (M1–M6) exhibit large-scale rearrangements. M1 and M2 move upward toward the cytoplasm (+z direction) while M3 and M4 shift downwards, with displacements up to 5 Å (close to one turn of an α-helix). M1 shows a unique lateral movement within the membrane plane. M3 and M5 are strongly curved in opposite directions. These rearrangements are essential for releasing Ca²⁺ into the lumen and creating a cytoplasmic entry pathway for new Ca²⁺ ions.

### P domain coordinates transmembrane helix movements via flexible joints

The P domain functions as a coordinator of transmembrane helix movements. M3–M5 helices are directly linked to the P domain by hydrogen bonds: M5 integrates into the Rossmann fold, M3 connects through the P1 helix at the bottom of the P domain, and M6 connects through loop L67 and M5. The pivoting point is around Gly 770 on M5. Flexible joints between the P domain and helices allow adjustments that prevent steric clashes. The connections are mediated mostly by van der Waals interactions, with the P domain conducting movements of M1 and M2 indirectly.

### Conformation changes at Ca²⁺-binding sites during dissociation

During Ca²⁺ dissociation, the residues on M6 in site I rotate nearly 90° clockwise: Asn 796 moves away toward site II, Thr 799 orients away from the molecule center, and Asp 800 replaces it. In site II, Glu 309 points away from the binding site, M4 shifts downwards, and Asp 800 moves away. The change in inclination of M5 above Gly 770 decreases the number of oxygen atoms contributing to site I, effectively lowering Ca²⁺ affinity in the E2 state.

### Water-accessible channel and closed lumenal gate in the E2 state

In the E2(TG) structure, a water-accessible channel with an 8 × 10 Å opening exists, lined by negatively charged residues and leading to the carboxyl group of Glu 309. This channel is proposed to be the entry pathway for the first Ca²⁺ ion during binding. On the lumenal side, the loop connecting M3 and M4 comes closer to the L78 loop, sealing the access pathway. This deviates from classical E1–E2 models that assume lumenally opened binding sites in the E2 state.

### ATP-induced domain closure and Ca2+ occlusion in the E1 state (PDB 1VFP)

The E1-AMPPCP structure (PDB 1VFP, 2.9 A) reveals how ATP binding triggers large-scale conformational changes that occlude bound Ca2+ ions. AMPPCP directly bridges the N- and P-domains, causing the three cytoplasmic domains (A, N, P) to form a compact headpiece from their widely separated positions in the nucleotide-free E1·2Ca2+ state. The N-domain inclines ~90° towards the P-domain, facilitated by AMPPCP whose adenine ring binds to Phe 487 in the N-domain while the gamma-phosphate binds near Asp 351 in the P-domain. This causes the A-domain to tilt ~30° around an axis parallel to the membrane plane, which in turn pulls the M1 helix towards the cytoplasmic side. The M1 helix movement occupies the space previously empty on the cytoplasmic side of Glu 309 (the gating residue for Ca2+ site II), forming van der Waals contacts with Glu 309 and a hydrogen bond with Asn 796. This doubly locks Glu 309 in a conformation that occludes Ca2+ site II, preventing Ca2+ exchange with the cytoplasm. The P-domain also bends upon binding the gamma-phosphate and Mg2+, gaining better structural match with the catalytic domain of phosphoserine phosphatase (HAD superfamily). This structure represents the state just before phosphoryl transfer from ATP to Asp 351.

### E2P-BeF3- and E2-P*-AlF4- structures from supplementary data (doi/10.1038/nature06418)

Supplementary crystallographic data for the main paper (10.1038/nature06418) reports additional structures of SERCA1a in the E2P-BeF3- state (space group P212121, resolution 2.65 A) and the E2-P*-AlF4- state (space group P21, resolution 3.0 A). These structures provide independent confirmation of the phosphate-analog-trapped states and complement the primary E2P (PDB 3B9B, C2) and E2-Pi (PDB 3B9R, C2) structures. The E2P-BeF3- structure was collected at ESRF ID23-2 with wavelength 0.873 A, while the E2-P*-AlF4- structure was collected at ESRF ID29 with wavelength 1.066 A. Thapsigargin was not present in the E2-P*-AlF4- structure, allowing comparison of AlF4- complexes with and without inhibitor (Supplementary Fig. S5). The E2P-BeF3- and E2-P*-AlF4- structures show essentially identical domain arrangements, confirming the structural similarity between the phosphorylated and phosphate-analog-trapped states.

### Homology modeling of H+,K+-ATPase using SERCA1a E2P as template

The SERCA1a E2P structure (Supplementary Fig. S6) was used as a template for homology modeling of the omeprazole binding site on the gastric H+,K+-ATPase. The E2P structure reveals an exposed pocket containing Pro789 (SERCA1a numbering) which corresponds to Cys813 in H+,K+-ATPase. This residue is exposed to the extracellular side in the E2P state and crosslinks to omeprazole. The SERCA1a model provides insight into the inhibitor binding mechanism of related P-type ATPases.

### SLN-bound SERCA reveals E1 intermediate with exposed Ca2+ sites

The crystal structure of rabbit SERCA1a in complex with sarcolipin (SLN) at 3.1 A resolution reveals a previously undescribed E1 intermediate state (E1-SLN). The SLN helix binds in a groove between transmembrane helices M2, M6, and M9 of SERCA, trapping the Ca2+-ATPase in this E1 state with exposure of the Ca2+ binding sites through an open cytoplasmic pathway stabilized by Mg2+. The M1 sliding door is open, with side chains of Asp 800 and Glu 309 exposed to the cytoplasmic surface. A deep, funnel-shaped negatively charged path leads to the Ca2+ sites, limiting energetic barriers for loading the ion-binding sites located halfway through the membrane bilayer. SLN binding is compatible with the [Hn]E2 state but would be incompatible with the [Ca2]E1P conformation where the groove is constricted by Leu 96, Trp 107, and Gln 108.

### Mg2+ ions coordinate at Ca2+ site II in E1-SLN state

Two Mg2+ ions are associated with Ca2+ site II in the E1-SLN structure, one of which is in close vicinity to exposed main-chain carbonyls of Val 304, Ala 305, and Ile 307. Initial Ca2+ binding at site I is likely to engage Asp 800, thus distorting the Mg2+ sites and preparing site II for Ca2+ binding as a basis for cooperativity. Mg2+ ions bound at low-affinity sites (saturated at ~75 mM in the crystallization drop) stabilize an open structure of site II with exposed carbonyl groups, representing a genuine E1 intermediate that allows cytoplasmic ion exchange.
