# GkApcT Amino Acid Transporter (CAT Homologue)

## Overview

GkApcT is a proton-coupled amino acid transporter from Geobacillus kaustophilus belonging to the SLC7/CAT family of cationic amino acid transporters within the APC (amino acid-polyamine-organocation) superfamily. It serves as a close homologue of the mammalian CAT transporters (SLC7A1-4 and SLC7A14) and provides the first structural model for understanding cationic amino acid transport in mammals. GkApcT adopts the conserved "5 + 5 inverted topology" fold characteristic of APC superfamily transporters and functions as a proton-coupled, sodium-independent amino acid symporter. Crystal structures with bound L-Ala (2.86 A) and L-Arg (3.13 A, M321S variant) reveal the molecular basis for amino acid selectivity through steric constraints rather than charge complementarity, and provide insights into the alternating access transport mechanism and the kinetic differences between CAT-2A and CAT-2B isoforms.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Proton-coupled transport mechanism in the APC superfamily

GkApcT is the first proton-coupled amino acid transporter from the APC superfamily to be structurally and biochemically characterized. Transport assays demonstrate that GkApcT is proton-coupled (not sodium-dependent), with uptake driven by an inwardly directed proton gradient and abolished by the proton ionophore CCCP. Mutational analysis identified Glu115 (TM3) as a key proton-coupling residue, with the Glu115Gln variant retaining counterflow but losing electrogenic uptake. Lys191 (TM5) occupies a position equivalent to the Na2 site in LeuT and is proposed to stabilize the unwound region of TM1 during transport, undergoing protonation/deprotonation during the transport cycle without being the primary coupling site. The data reveal how the "5 + 5 inverted topology" fold has adapted to couple amino acid uptake to the proton electrochemical gradient.

### Amino acid selectivity via steric constraints

CAT family transporters are highly specialized for cationic amino acids (L-Arg, L-Lys, L-ornithine) unlike their LAT homologues. The GkApcT structure reveals that amino acid selectivity in this subfamily is determined by steric constraints within the binding site rather than charge complementarity. Met321 (TM8) acts as a steric gatekeeper — its large side chain restricts access to larger amino acids. The Met321Ser variant showed dramatically increased affinity for L-Arg (IC50 reduced from 6 mM to 11.4 uM), and the 3.13 A crystal structure of this variant with bound L-Arg (PDB 6F34) confirmed that the M321S mutation opens up the ligand binding site to accommodate the larger arginine side chain. This provides a molecular mechanism for how the SLC7 family has evolved to select specific subsets of amino acids.

### Structural basis for CAT-2A/CAT-2B isoform differences

The two isoforms of the CAT-2 transporter (CAT-2A, low affinity in liver/muscle; CAT-2B, high affinity in macrophages/T-cells) differ by two amino acids on an intracellular loop between TM8 and TM9. The GkApcT structure reveals that Arg334 (equivalent to the site of the CAT-2A/B difference) forms a salt bridge to Glu15 in the lateral helix, stabilizing TM8 and through interactions TM6B and TM3. The Arg334Glu variant (mimicking CAT-2B) showed increased affinity (IC50 reduced from 2 uM to 0.4 uM), providing a structural explanation for the kinetic differences between the two isoforms. The structure suggests that the affinity change arises from altered packing of TM6B, TM8, and TM3 affecting the amino acid binding site.

### Alternating access mechanism with water-coordinated substrate binding

GkApcT operates via an alternating access mechanism where TM1 and TM6 act as toggle switches. The inward occluded structure reveals a bound L-Ala ligand trapped by a thick extracellular gate (involving Phe231) and thin intracellular gate (TM6B packing against TM3 and TM8). A notable feature is the use of a water molecule to coordinate the alpha-carboxylate group of the substrate, occupying a position similar to the Na1 ion in LeuT. This water-mediated coordination suggests that movement of TM1 during transport disrupts the hydrogen bond network, facilitating substrate release. The structure also reveals that a cholesterol binding site is formed at the interface between GkApcT and the single-TM chaperone MgtS, which may stabilize the heterodimeric complex — a model relevant to the mammalian HAT (heteromeric amino acid transporter) complexes involving SLC3 and SLC7 subunits.
