# Crystallization Methods

Membrane protein crystallization is the process of growing well-ordered protein crystals suitable for X-ray diffraction. Unlike soluble proteins, membrane proteins require detergents or lipids to maintain stability in aqueous solution, making crystallization significantly more challenging.

## Overview

Successful crystallization requires balancing protein solubility with crystal nucleation. The protein-lipid-detergent mixture must form an ordered lattice while maintaining structural integrity. Key variables include precipitant concentration, temperature, pH, and the presence of ligands or stabilizing agents.

## Methods

- [lipidic-cubic-phase](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/crystallization/lipidic-cubic-phase/) — Lipidic Cubic Phase (LCP) Crystallization. Embeds membrane proteins in a lipidic mesophase mimicking native bilayer environment. Particularly successful for GPCRs and transporters.
- [microbatch](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/crystallization/microbatch/) — Microbatch Crystallization. Oil-overlay method that minimizes evaporation and is suitable for small volumes.
- [monoolein](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/crystallization/monoolein/) — Monoolein as the primary lipid used in LCP crystallization. Forms the cubic mesophase with protein-lipid ratios typically 1:1 to 1:3 (w/w).
- [vapor-diffusion](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/crystallization/vapor-diffusion/) — Vapor Diffusion Crystallization. Conventional sitting-drop or hanging-drop method where precipitant concentration equilibrates through vapor phase. Most widely used crystallization technique.
