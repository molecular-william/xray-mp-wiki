# Structure Determination

Structure determination encompasses all methods used to determine the three-dimensional atomic structure of membrane proteins. The choice of method depends on protein properties, available sample, and required resolution.

## Overview

Membrane protein structure determination has been revolutionized over the past two decades. X-ray crystallography remains the dominant method for high-resolution structures, while cryo-EM has emerged as a powerful alternative, particularly for large complexes and proteins resistant to crystallization. Serial femtosecond crystallography using X-ray free-electron lasers enables structure determination of microcrystals.

## Methods

- [cryoem](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/structure-determination/cryoem/) — Cryo-EM (Cryogenic Electron Microscopy). Single-particle cryo-EM determines structures by imaging frozen-hydrated particles. Resolution has improved dramatically, now reaching near-atomic resolution for many targets.
- [molecular-replacement](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/structure-determination/molecular-replacement/) — Molecular Replacement. X-ray phasing method using a known homologous structure as a search model. Most common phasing method when a related structure exists.
- [serial-femtosecond-crystallography](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/structure-determination/serial-femtosecond-crystallography/) — Serial Femtosecond Crystallography (SFX). X-ray free-electron laser method that collects diffraction from thousands of microcrystals. Enables structures from crystals too small for conventional X-ray sources.
- [xray-crystallography](/xray-mp-wiki/methods/structure-determination/xray-crystallography/) — X-ray Crystallography. The gold standard for high-resolution membrane protein structures. Requires well-ordered crystals and involves crystal growth, data collection, phasing, and model building.
