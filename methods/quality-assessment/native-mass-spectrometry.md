# Native Mass Spectrometry

## Overview

Native mass spectrometry (native MS) is a technique for analyzing intact macromolecular complexes under non-denaturing conditions. It preserves non-covalent interactions, allowing the study of protein-lipid, protein-protein, and protein-ligand interactions in their native-like state. Native MS was used to determine the oligomerization state of [MmpL3 from Mycobacterium smegmatis](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/mmpL3/) (monomeric), characterize its lipid-binding specificity (TMM, PE, PG, PI, [DAG](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/dag/), CDL), and measure dissociation constants (Kd = 3.7 uM for TMM, 19.5 uM for PE).

## Principle

Native MS maintains proteins in their folded state by using volatile buffers (e.g., [Ammonium Acetate](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/ammonium-acetate/)) and gentle ionization conditions. Detergent-solubilized membrane proteins can be analyzed to determine mass, stoichiometry of bound ligands, and binding affinities through titration experiments.
