# Hydrogen-Deuterium Exchange Mass Spectrometry (HDX-MS)

## Overview

Hydrogen-deuterium exchange mass spectrometry (HDX-MS) is a biophysical
technique for probing protein dynamics, conformational changes, and
protein-protein or protein-ligand interactions. Proteins are incubated in
D2O buffer, where backbone amide hydrogens exchange with deuterium at rates
that depend on hydrogen bonding and solvent accessibility. After variable
labeling times, the exchange is quenched (low pH, low temperature), proteins
are digested with pepsin, and the deuterium incorporation into individual
peptides is measured by mass spectrometry. Regions that are protected from
exchange (slow deuteration) are typically buried in the protein core or
involved in hydrogen bonding, interfaces, or stable secondary structure.

## Principle

The rate of hydrogen-deuterium exchange for a backbone amide hydrogen depends on its local environment. Exposed, unstructured regions exchange rapidly (seconds), while buried or hydrogen-bonded regions exchange more slowly (minutes to hours). By measuring deuterium uptake over time, information about protein dynamics and conformational changes can be obtained. When comparing two states (e.g., apo vs. ligand-bound, or WT vs. mutant), differences in deuteration reveal regions that change in structure or dynamics. Experiments are typically performed in triplicate with error bars showing standard deviation. Results are commonly presented as kinetic curves (deuterium uptake % vs. time) or Woods plots (differential HDX % across the protein sequence).
