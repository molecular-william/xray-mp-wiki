# Listeriolysin O


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/A0A0H3GD84">UniProt: A0A0H3GD84</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Listeria MONOCYTOGENES</span>

## Overview

Listeriolysin O (LLO) is a cholesterol-dependent cytolysin (CDC) and essential virulence factor of the Gram-positive pathogen *Listeria monocytogenes*. LLO enables the bacteria to escape the phagosome by forming pores in the phagosomal membrane. It is unique among CDCs for its pH- and temperature-dependent pore-forming activity, which restricts its activity to the acidic phagosomal compartment while sparing the neutral-pH host plasma membrane. LLO contains four domains (D1-D4), a distinctive N-terminal PPII helix, and a tryptophan-rich undecapeptide. The crystal structure reveals the molecular basis of oligomerization and pore formation, including seven ion-coordination clusters that regulate conformational switching.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### pH and temperature regulation via ion-coordination clusters

The pore-forming activity of LLO depends on pH and temperature, which is unique among cholesterol-dependent cytolysins. The crystal structure of LLO revealed seven interaction clusters at the interface of the membrane-inserting helix bundles (HB1/HB2) with surrounding domains D1, D2, and the central beta-sheet. These clusters coordinate a total of 7 Na+ ions and 18 water molecules. The initially predicted acidic triad (D208/E247/Y206 from the central beta-sheet with K316/D320 from HB2) is part of a larger network. Additional clusters involve residues Y406, N140, N402, T223, Y348, S250, K252, Y303, Y348, E246, K305, K344, S213, E214, S215, Y78, D81, and S404. These clusters stabilize the pre-pore conformation and act as a pH sensor, with the acidic residues D208, E247, and D320 identified as the central pH-sensing triad. The Na+ ions and water molecules mediate tight interactions between the helix bundles and surrounding domains, locking the toxin in a pre-pore state until pH and temperature conditions trigger pore formation.

### Oligomerization interface and pore formation mechanism

The crystal structure reveals linear interactions between LLO protomers in the crystal lattice, providing a model for the initial oligomerization step on the membrane. Interface mutations (K175E, S176W, E262K) that abolish or reduce haemolytic activity prevent ring-shaped oligomer formation on erythrocyte ghost membranes, while mutations with retained or enhanced activity (A40W, S44D, S44E, N230W, D394W, E262W) still form rings. The non-haemolytic mutants form irregular rows and granular protein structures rather than complete rings, indicating they can bind the membrane but cannot achieve the curvature required for pore formation. The wild-type LLO forms complete rings of variable size on ghost membranes, with incomplete rings and arcs also observed. Pore formation requires precise interface interactions during toxin oligomerization, with surface charge complementarity being essential for both haemolytic activity and Ca2+ channel formation. The Ca2+ channels formed by LLO at sublytic concentrations are narrower than the large haemolytic pores and likely involve fewer monomers.

### Regulatory role of the N-terminal PPII helix

The N-terminal PPII helix of LLO plays a regulatory role in pore formation. Located laterally above domain D1, the PPII helix contains three putative phosphorylation sites (S44, S48, T51). In the crystal structure, the PPII helix is positioned between LLO molecules in linear arrays, where it would interfere with the formation of optimally curved oligomers required for membrane insertion. This positioning effectively downregulates LLO activity by reducing ring formation. Deletion of the PPII helix (LLO Delta PPII) or mutations that support its displacement from the D1 surface (A40W, S44D, S44E) increase both haemolytic activity and Ca2+ uptake, consistent with a model where PPII helix displacement aids formation of optimally curved oligomers. The PPII helix is stabilized by interactions with helix alpha1, which in turn interacts with loops connecting helices alpha9 and alpha10. These interactions are largely conserved among *Listeria* species but poorly conserved among other CDCs, explaining the unique pH-dependent activity of LLO.
