# US28 Viral Chemokine Receptor

## Overview

US28 is a G-protein-coupled receptor encoded by human cytomegalovirus (CMV) that
functions as a promiscuous chemokine 'sink' to facilitate evasion of host immune
responses. US28 binds with high affinity to multiple CC-type chemokines and CX3CL1
(fractalkine), exhibiting unique ligand cross-reactivity that far exceeds that of
any human chemokine receptor. The receptor signals constitutively via Gq and
beta-arrestin pathways, and natural chemokine ligands (CX3CL1, CCL5/RANTES, CCL3/MIP1a)
produce distinct signaling outcomes ranging from G-protein-biased agonism (CCL3) to
balanced Gq and migration (CCL5, CX3CL1). Deep sequencing of CX3CL1 N-terminal
libraries revealed that US28 can engage thousands of distinct chemokine sequences,
with signaling remarkably robust to mutational disruption of individual
receptor-ligand contacts. Structural comparisons between apo-US28, wild-type
CX3CL1-bound US28, and an engineered G-protein-biased variant (CX3CL1.35) bound
to US28 revealed that different chemokines induce distinct conformational changes
in the receptor extracellular loops and transmembrane helices, suggesting that
steric bulk rather than specific bonding chemistries drives activation.

**Expression:**

- **Expression system**: HEK293S GnTI- cells (BacMam baculovirus transduction)
- **Construct**: US28 truncated by 10 aa at N-terminus and 44 aa at C-terminus, fused to [Nanobody](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/protein-tags/nanobody/) 7 (Nb7) via a linker containing two thrombin recognition sites and a six-residue Gly-Ser linker. Decorated with HA signal peptide, N-terminal FLAG epitope tag, C-terminal 3C protease site, protein C and 8xHis tags.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Chemokine promiscuity and signaling bias

US28 engages thousands of distinct chemokine N-terminal sequences with broad sequence
degeneracy. Deep sequencing of CX3CL1 N-terminal libraries selected on nanobody-stabilized
US28 revealed hydrophobic amino acids enrich at position 1 (over wild-type Q), and Arg
enriches at position 2 (over wild-type H). Despite extreme sequence diversity, nearly all
selected variants induce some level of cell migration, and several elicit stronger G-protein
signaling than wild-type CX3CL1. Notably, signaling outcomes are relatively insensitive to
individual sequence substitutions within a given N-terminal motif.

### Structural basis for ligand cross-reactivity

The crystal structure of the G-protein-biased CX3CL1.35 variant (LLPHANY N-terminus) bound
to US28 reveals an entirely unique chemokine N-terminal peptide conformation compared to
wild-type CX3CL1, projecting into the major receptor pocket toward TM5 and TM6. Unlike
wild-type CX3CL1 which hugs TM2, CX3CL1.35 contacts Asn189(5.39), Leu192(5.42),
Tyr244(6.51), Leu248(6.55), and Asp251(6.58) - residues surrounding the Trp241(6.48)
rotamer switch implicated in GPCR activation. The chemokine body is rotated by 16.8
degrees relative to wild-type, and extensive direct contacts to TM2 and ECL2 are lost.

### Steric bulk as the primary determinant of signaling

US28 signaling is remarkably robust to mutational disruption of receptor-ligand contacts
throughout the binding pocket. CX3CL1.35 family members with even drastic sequence changes
at positions contacting the receptor maintain similar signaling outputs. This suggests that
steric bulk of the chemokine N-terminus, which distorts the walls of the US28 binding
pocket, is more important than specific bonding chemistries for activation. The
capsule-shaped US28 pocket affords numerous adventitious bonding interactions with
alternative N-terminal peptide conformations.

### Chemokine-dependent extracellular conformational changes

Comparison of apo-US28 (5wb1), CX3CL1-bound (4xt1), and CX3CL1.35-bound (5wb2) structures
reveals distinct conformational changes. In apo-US28, ECL1 and ECL2 collapse inward,
constricting access to the binding pocket. CX3CL1 displaces ECL1 and ECL2 away from the
receptor core with minimal overall distortion. CX3CL1.35 distorts TM1, TM6, and TM7 away
from the receptor core, resulting in an expanded binding pocket. These differences suggest
chemokines function as bitopic ligands via both Site 1.5 (ECL) and Site 2 (N-terminus into
TM bundle) interactions.

### Viral evolution of chemokine promiscuity

US28 evolved from a human chemokine GPCR through viral hijacking to serve as a highly
cross-reactive 'chemokine sink' (decoy receptor) that subverts host immunity. Unlike most
receptor-ligand interactions characterized by high specificity, US28 accommodates and
functionally discriminates amongst highly degenerate chemokine sequences. Natural
chemokine sequences represent a subset of local minima in a broad signaling fitness
landscape. This mechanism explains how CMV evolved a GPCR to be highly promiscuous for
viral survival.
