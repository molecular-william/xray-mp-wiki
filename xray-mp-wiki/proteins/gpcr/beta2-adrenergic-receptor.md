# Human Beta2-Adrenergic Receptor (beta2 AR)


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/P00720">UniProt: P00720</a> <a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/P07550">UniProt: P07550</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Homo sapiens</span>

## Overview

The human beta2-adrenergic receptor (beta2 AR) is a class A G protein-coupled receptor that plays a central role in sympathetic nervous system signaling, mediating effects on heart rate, bronchodilation, and metabolic processes. Multiple crystal structures have been determined using different stabilization strategies. The landmark first high-resolution structure (PDB: 2RH1, 2.4 A, Cherezov et al., Science 2007) used a T4 lysozyme fusion (T4L) replacing the flexible third intracellular loop (ICL3), crystallized in cholesterol-doped lipidic cubic phase (LCP) with the partial inverse agonist carazolol. This structure was the first of any human GPCR bound to a diffusible ligand and only the second GPCR structure ever determined after rhodopsin. A concurrent structure (PDB: 2R4R/2R4S, 3.4 A, Rosenbaum et al., Nature 2007) used a C-terminal truncated construct (beta2 AR365) in complex with a Fab5 antibody fragment. Subsequent T4L fusion structures with inverse agonists ICI 118,551, compound 2, and [ALPRENOLOL](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/alprenolol) were determined at 2.8-3.1 A resolution. The landmark active-state structure (PDB: 3SNY, 3.2 A) captured the first high-resolution view of an agonist-bound GPCR in complex with its Gs heterotrimeric G protein. The structure of beta2 AR bound to the allosteric modulator Cmpd-15PA (PDB: 5X7D, 2.7 A) revealed the mechanism of intracellular allosteric antagonism. The structure of beta2 AR bound to the membrane-facing allosteric modulator AS408 (PDB: 6OBA, 3.1 A) revealed a novel allosteric binding pocket at the TM3-TM5 lipid-protein interface, adjacent to the conserved P211-I121-F282 activation switch.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Cholesterol binding site and consensus motif in class A GPCRs

The 2.8 A structure of beta2AR(E122W)-T4L (PDB 3D4S) revealed two cholesterol molecules bound per receptor in a specific binding site between helices I, II, III, and IV, not in the crystal packing interface. Thermal stability analysis using isothermal denaturation showed that CHS (cholesteryl hemisuccinate) significantly enhances receptor stability, with a 5-fold increase in half-life at 35 C. A cholesterol consensus motif (CCM) was defined: [4.39-4.43(R,K)]-[4.50(W,Y)]-[4.46(I,V,L)]-[2.41(F,Y)], predicting cholesterol binding for 44% of human class A receptors (strict-CCM: 21%). The aromatic Trp158(4.50) is almost universally conserved (94%) among class A GPCRs and contributes the most significant interaction with cholesterol.

### First high-resolution structure of a diffusible-ligand GPCR

The 2.4 A crystal structure of beta2AR-T4L (PDB: 2RH1, Cherezov et al., Science 2007) represented the first high-resolution structure of a human GPCR bound to a diffusible ligand, and only the second GPCR structure ever determined after rhodopsin. The T4 lysozyme fusion strategy (replacing the flexible ICL3) became a widely adopted approach for GPCR crystallography. The success of lipidic cubic phase crystallization established LCP as the primary method for GPCR structure determination. The structure revealed cholesterol-mediated receptor dimerization, the architecture of the extracellular loops accessible for diffusible ligand binding, and the structural divergence from rhodopsin that explained GPCR ligand diversity.

### T4L fusion strategy and LCP crystallization methodology

The beta2AR-T4L structure (Cherezov et al., Science 2007) pioneered two key methodologies that revolutionized GPCR structural biology: (1) replacement of the flexible third intracellular loop (ICL3) with T4 lysozyme (T4L) to reduce conformational heterogeneity and provide crystal contacts, and (2) crystallization in cholesterol-doped lipidic cubic phase (LCP), which provided a native-like membrane environment. The T4L fusion strategy was subsequently used to determine structures of A2A, D3, H1, M2, M3, S1P1, CXCR4, and many other GPCRs. The LCP method enabled crystallization of receptors that resisted detergent-based approaches.

### Carazolol binding and the toggle switch mechanism

The beta2AR-T4L carazolol complex revealed how an inverse agonist suppresses basal GPCR activity. Carazolol binds deep in the orthosteric pocket, positioning its carbazole moiety adjacent to Phe289(6.51), Phe290(6.52), and Trp286(6.48) - the conserved tryptophan "toggle switch." Unlike retinal in rhodopsin, carazolol does not directly interact with Trp286 but constrains it through the surrounding aromatic network, mimicking the effect of the beta-ionone ring of retinal. This explains how diffusible ligand binding can stabilize the inactive conformation despite lacking covalent attachment.

### Conserved hydroxy-amine binding motif

All three compounds (ICI 118,551, compound 2, and [ALPRENOLOL](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/alprenolol)) contain a hydroxy-amine
motif that establishes a conserved hydrogen bond network with the receptor. This motif
is a common feature among the 'classical' scaffold of beta2 AR ligands with inverse
agonist, antagonist, or full/partial agonist activities.

### Ligand-specific aromatic interactions define pharmacology

While the hydroxy-amine motif is conserved, distinct interactions between the head groups
of the ligands and the receptor are observed. The aromatic moieties of all compounds are
anchored by strong hydrophobic interactions in the binding cleft. Specific hydrogen bonds
are established by substituent moieties in compound 2, timolol, and [CARAZOLOL](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/carazolol).

### Minor conformational impact of inverse agonists and antagonists

Minimal structural differences between the three complexes indicate that the ligands
studied exert only a minor local impact on the structure of the receptor. The result
that beta2 AR bound to pharmacologically distinct ligands have virtually identical
backbone conformations suggests that conformational changes capable of modifying
signaling properties are very small.

### Ligand-specific extracellular surface (ECS) conformations

NMR spectroscopy of [13C]methyl-beta2 AR revealed three distinct conformations of the
extracellular surface: one for the unliganded receptor or neutral antagonist ([ALPRENOLOL](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/alprenolol)),
one for the inverse agonist (carazolol), and one for the agonist (formoterol). The
Lys 305-Asp 192 salt bridge, linking extracellular loops 2 and 3, serves as a key
reporter of ECS conformation. Inverse agonists stabilize the salt bridge, while
agonists induce conformational fluctuations that liberate Lys 305 from the salt
bridge. These findings demonstrate conformational coupling between the orthosteric
ligand-binding pocket in the transmembrane core and the extracellular surface,
showing that drugs targeting this diverse surface could function as allosteric
modulators with high subtype selectivity.

### Active-state GPCR structure with Gs protein

The T4L-beta2 AR-Gs-[NB35](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/antibodies/nb35) complex structure (PDB: 3SNY, 3.2 A) represents the first
high-resolution view of an active-state GPCR coupled to its G protein. Key structural
changes include a 14 A outward movement of TM6 at the cytoplasmic end and a 7-residue
extension of the cytoplasmic end of TM5. The Gs alpha-5 helix docks into a cavity
formed between TM5 and TM6, with 6 A displacement towards the receptor. The largest
conformational change in Gs is a 127-degree rotation of the alpha-helical domain
relative to the Ras domain, attributed to the empty nucleotide-binding pocket.

### GPCR-G protein interface interactions

The principal interactions between the beta2 AR and Gs involve the amino- and
carboxy-terminal alpha-helices of Galpha s. The carboxy-terminal alpha-5 helix of
Galpha s docks into a cavity formed by the opening of TM5 and TM6. A key interaction
involves Phe 139 of the beta2 AR at the beginning of the ICL2 helix, which sits in
a hydrophobic pocket on Galpha s formed by His 41, Val 217, Phe 376, Cys 379,
Arg 380, and Ile 383. The F139A mutation severely impairs Gs coupling.

### MNG-3 detergent stabilization

MNG-3 detergent uniquely stabilizes the beta2 AR even when diluted below its critical
micelle concentration, unlike [DDM](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/detergents/ddm) which rapidly loses binding activity. MNG-3
maintained the structural integrity of the T4L-beta2 AR-Gs complex during its
incorporation into lipidic cubic phase, improving both crystal size and quality.

### Intracellular allosteric modulation mechanism

The structure of beta2 AR-T4L bound to [CARAZOLOL](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/carazolol) and Cmpd-15PA (PDB: 5X7D, 2.7 A)
revealed an intracellular allosteric binding pocket formed by the cytoplasmic ends
of TM1, TM2, TM6, TM7, ICL1, and helix 8. Cmpd-15PA stabilizes TM6 in the inactive
conformation through direct polar interactions with Thr274^6.36 and non-polar
interactions with Ala271^6.33 and Leu275^6.37. The compound disrupts a salt bridge
between Arg63^ICL1 and Asp331^8.49, enabling a new salt bridge between Lys267^6.29
and Asp331^8.49. This allosteric pocket is distinct from the orthosteric site and
overlaps with the G-protein-binding interface, explaining how Cmpd-15 inhibits
both G protein and beta-arrestin coupling. Molecular dynamics simulations show
that Cmpd-15 binding stabilizes a more inward conformation of TM6.

### beta2AR-GsCT intermediate complex structure

The beta2AR-T4L-GsCT-CC structure (PDB: 6E67, 3.1 A) captures an intermediate state in GPCR-G protein complex formation. The C-terminal 14 amino acids of Galpha s (GsCT) bind to the cytoplasmic core of the active beta2 AR in an alternate orientation compared to the fully-coupled nucleotide-free complex (3SNY). E392^Galpha s and R389^Galpha s (part of the Gs-specific bar code) interact with R131^3.50 of the conserved DRY motif and Y141^ICL2, whereas in the fully-coupled state H387^Galpha s and Y391^Galpha s contact the receptor. The structure of Gs-GDP (PDB: 6EG8) reveals that prior to receptor engagement, the alpha5 helix is bent with H387^Galpha s and Y391^Galpha s interacting with the Ras domain while E392^Galpha s and R389^Galpha s are surface-exposed, consistent with a stepwise engagement mechanism.

### Nb80 nanobody-stabilized active state of beta2 AR

The structure of beta2 AR-T4L bound to the high-affinity agonist BI-167107 and stabilized by the G protein-mimetic nanobody Nb80 (PDB: 3NY7, 3.5 A, Rasmussen et al., Nature 2011) represents the first active-state structure of a diffusible-ligand GPCR. Nb80 binds to the cytoplasmic end of beta2 AR with its CDR3 loop projecting into the core of the receptor, occupying a position similar to the C-terminal peptide of transducin in opsin. The structure reveals an 11.4 A outward movement of TM6 at Glu268(6.30), inward movements of TM5 and TM7, and a broken ionic lock between Arg131(3.50) and the conserved D130(3.49) region. The intracellular loop 2 (ICL2) adopts a two-turn alpha helix similar to that observed in the turkey beta1 AR structure. Changes in the agonist-binding pocket are subtle: an inward bulge of TM5 centered around Ser207(5.46) with a 2.1 A C-alpha shift. The high-affinity agonist BI-167107 (Kd = 84 pM) forms extensive polar interactions with Ser203(5.42), Ser207(5.46), Asn293(6.55), and Tyr308(7.35). Nb80 increases agonist affinity by 95-fold (Ki from 107 nM to 1.13 nM), faithfully mimicking the effect of Gs protein. The structure was crystallized in lipidic cubic phase (LCP) using monoolein with 10% cholesterol, and data were collected from 23 microcrystals at APS 23-ID using a 10-um microbeam.

### FAUC50 covalent agonist tethering and active-state stabilization

The 3.5 A crystal structure of FAUC50-beta2 AR(H93C)-T4L (PDB: 3P0G, Rosenbaum et al., Nature 2011) represents the first structure of a GPCR with a covalently tethered agonist. FAUC50 is a disulfide-functionalized derivative of the beta2 AR agonist that forms a covalent disulfide bond with Cys93(2.61) introduced by H93C mutation. This tethering strategy enabled capture of an active-like conformation without requiring stabilization by a G protein or nanobody, although the receptor was still fused to T4L in ICL3. The structure revealed that FAUC50 occupies the orthosteric binding pocket in a mode similar to other beta2 AR agonists, with its ethanolamine moiety forming the conserved hydrogen bond network with Ser203(5.42), Ser207(5.46), and Asn293(6.55). The covalently tethered agonist approach provides a general method for stabilizing active GPCR conformations for crystallographic studies. The structure was determined at 3.5 A resolution in space group P21212 using LCP crystallization with monoolein.

### Constrained catecholamines achieve beta2AR selectivity via allosteric effects on pocket dynamics

The 3.2 A crystal structure of beta2AR bound to constrained epinephrine (c-Epi, PDB 7XKA) revealed that c-Epi binds in a nearly identical position as native epinephrine. The bridging carbons of c-Epi cause an upward displacement of F193(45.52) by approximately 0.8 A. While the orthosteric pocket residues are identical between beta1AR and beta2AR, constrained catecholamines (c-Epi, c-NorEpi, c-ISO) display over 100-fold selectivity for beta2AR. The selectivity arises from: (1) faster association rates on beta2AR due to conformational restriction matching the bioactive conformation, (2) slower association on beta1AR due to incompatible binding pathways through the extracellular vestibule, and (3) reduced stability of c-Epi in the beta1AR orthosteric pocket with loss of canonical hydrogen bonds to Ser(5.42) and Ser(5.46). MD simulations showed these H-bonds occur 76-87% of the time in beta2AR but only 20-21% in beta1AR. Four residues surrounding F45.52 (W199/ECL2, V209/ECL2, K347/6.58, F359/7.35 in beta1AR vs Y174/ECL2, F194/ECL2, H296/6.58, Y308/7.35 in beta2AR) were shown to allosterically modulate pocket shape and stability. Mutating these four residues in beta1AR to beta2AR-like residues restored c-Epi affinity.

### AS408 allosteric modulator binds to a membrane-facing pocket at TM3-TM5

The 3.1 A crystal structure of beta2 AR bound to the allosteric modulator AS408 (PDB 6OBA) revealed a novel binding pocket at the membrane-facing surface of transmembrane segments 3 and 5, distinct from the orthosteric and previously described allosteric sites. AS408 disrupts a water-mediated polar network involving E122(3.41) and the backbone carbonyls of V206(5.45) and S207(5.46). The AS408 binding site is adjacent to a previously identified molecular switch for beta2 AR activation formed by I121(3.40), P211(5.50) and F282(6.44). The structure reveals how AS408 stabilizes the inactive conformation of this switch, thereby acting as a negative allosteric modulator (NAM) for agonists and positive allosteric modulator (PAM) for inverse agonists. The binding pose is stable in 12 us MD simulations, with the protonated AS408 forming persistent ionic interactions with E122(3.41) (>99% frequency). AS408 is a NAM for G-protein activation and arrestin recruitment, with the greatest effect in suppressing arrestin recruitment by norepinephrine. E122 mutations (E122Q, E122L, E122R) diminish AS408 activity, with E122R showing dramatically increased basal activity and uncoupling of the extracellular orthosteric pocket from the intracellular G-protein interface.

### N-terminal T4L fusion enables GPCR crystallization without intracellular modification

The T4L-beta2AR-deltaICL3 structure (PDB 4GBR, 4.0 A, Zou et al. PLoS ONE 2012) demonstrated that fusing T4 lysozyme to the N-terminus of beta2AR provides sufficient rigid hydrophilic surface for crystal packing without requiring thermostabilizing mutations, stabilizing antibodies, or T4L insertion into ICL3. Each T4L makes four distinct packing interactions in the crystal lattice. The structure is virtually identical to beta2AR-T4L (PDB 2RH1, RMSD 0.32 A), confirming that the N-terminal fusion does not distort the receptor. This approach is particularly valuable for crystallizing GPCR signaling complexes where intracellular modifications would interfere with G protein or arrestin binding.
