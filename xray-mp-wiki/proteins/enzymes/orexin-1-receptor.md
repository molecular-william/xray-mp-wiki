# Human Orexin 1 Receptor

## Overview

The human orexin 1 receptor (OX1R, HCRTR1) is a class A [G Protein-Coupled Receptor](/xray-mp-wiki/concepts/signaling-receptors/gpcr-g-protein-coupling/) that binds the neuropeptide orexin A and orexin B. It regulates sleep-wake cycles, feeding behavior, and reward pathways, and is a therapeutic target for insomnia and narcolepsy. The first crystal structures of OX1R were determined at 2.75 A (suvorexant complex, PDB 4ZJ8) and 2.83 A (SB-674042 complex, PDB 4ZJC) resolution (Yin et al., Nature Structural & Molecular Biology, 2016), revealing the structural basis for antagonist subtype selectivity between OX1R and OX2R. Key differences at positions 2.61 (S103 in OX1R vs T111 in OX2R) and 3.33 (A127 in OX1R vs T135 in OX2R) determine ligand selectivity. A large set of co-crystal structures spanning 14 ligand-receptor complexes with diverse chemotypes revealed that lipophilic hotspots drive GPCR ligand binding and that water-mediated hydrogen bond networks provide a basis for the observed diversity in orexin receptor-ligand binding modes. A structure-based design approach exploiting the single residue difference at position 3.33 (Ala127 in OX1R vs Thr135 in OX2R) yielded the selective antagonist JH112 (compound 2), which was co-crystallized with OX1R at 2.8 A resolution (Hellmann et al., PNAS, 2020). JH112 carries an (S,S)-sec-butyl substituent that points toward Ala127 in OX1R without steric clash, while creating repulsive interactions with Thr135 in OX2R, achieving 75-fold selectivity.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Subtype selectivity determined by residues at positions 2.61 and 3.33

The OX1R structure with SB-674042 (PDB 4ZJC) revealed that residues at positions 2.61
and 3.33 are critical for subtype selectivity. In OX1R, position 2.61 is occupied by
S103 (vs T111 in OX2R) and position 3.33 by A127 (vs T135 in OX2R). The smaller
residues in OX1R create a more open binding pocket that accommodates the bulky
SB-674042 ligand but creates steric clash with [EMPA](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/empa).

### Lipophilic hotspots drive GPCR ligand binding

Despite variability in binding modes across diverse chemotypes, all orexin receptor
ligands target conserved lipophilic hotspot regions: I (A/T3.33 and F5.42), II
(S/T2.61 and W23.50), III (S/T2.61, Y6.48 and Y7.43), and IV (F5.42 and Y6.48).
GRID Molecular Interaction Field analysis confirmed that hydrophobic hotspots and
displacement of high-energy water molecules drive ligand binding affinity.

### Water-mediated hydrogen bond networks enable binding diversity

The large set of orexin receptor-ligand structures reveals the degree of variability
of water-mediated H-bond networks possible in GPCR-ligand interactions. Direct polar
interactions between ligands and receptors are limited and not conserved, while
water-mediated interactions vary significantly. Predicting specific binding modes
is extremely difficult when matching polar pharmacophore features across chemical
series, as hydrophobic interactions dominate binding.

### Selectivity via trapped high-energy water molecules

The A127T3.33 residue in OX1R (vs T135 in OX2R) accounts for the ~1000-fold OX2
selectivity of [EMPA](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/empa). WaterFLAP calculations showed that A127 in OX1R creates a
slightly larger site that traps a high-energy water molecule in lipophilic hotspot region II when [EMPA](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/empa) is bound, explaining the selectivity without needing direct
ligand-receptor hydrogen bonds.

### Structure-based design of OX1R-selective antagonist JH112

Hellmann et al. (PNAS 2020) exploited the single amino acid difference at position 3.33 (Ala127 in OX1R vs Thr135 in OX2R) to develop the highly selective OX1R antagonist JH112 (compound 2). Starting from the suvorexant scaffold, the methyl group was relocated from the propylene to the ethylene bridge of the homopiperazine core, and optimized to an (S,S)-sec-butyl substituent. Molecular docking predicted the sec-butyl group would point toward Ala127 in OX1R (distance 4.0 A) without steric clash while clashing with Thr135 in OX2R (distance 2.9 A). The crystal structure of OX1R-PGS-JH112 (PDB 6X35, 2.8 A) confirmed the predicted binding geometry. JH112 exhibits subnanomolar OX1R affinity (Ki = 0.83 nM), 75-fold selectivity over OX2R, insurmountable antagonism for Gq activation and beta-arrestin-2 recruitment, blood-brain barrier penetration (brain-to-plasma ratio 0.3), and improved metabolic stability (t1/2 = 25.3 min in rat liver microsomes) compared to suvorexant (t1/2 = 7.3 min). This demonstrates the power of structure-based drug design for developing subtype-selective GPCR ligands with reduced off-target effects.
