# ApUbiA (UbiA from Aeropyrum pernix) — Intramembrane Prenyltransferase

<div class="expr-badges"><span class="expr-badge expr-e-coli">E. coli</span></div>


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/Q9YBM8">UniProt: Q9YBM8</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Aeropyrum pernix</span>

## Overview

ApUbiA is an intramembrane prenyltransferase from the hyperthermophilic archaeon Aeropyrum pernix, a member of the UbiA superfamily that catalyzes the condensation of isoprenylpyrophosphate (IPP) with p-hydroxybenzoate (PHB) to generate prenylated aromatic compounds. This is a key step in the biosynthesis of ubiquinones, menaquinones, chlorophylls, hemes, and vitamin E — lipid-soluble molecules that serve as electron and proton carriers for cellular respiration and photosynthesis and as antioxidants. The crystal structures of ApUbiA in its apo and substrate-bound states at 3.3 and 3.6 angstrom resolution, respectively, reveal nine transmembrane helices arranged counterclockwise in a U shape surrounding a large central cavity containing the active site. An unusual lateral portal opens the active site to the lipid bilayer, facilitating substrate binding and product release directly into the membrane. The structures rationalize disease-related mutations in eukaryotic homologs COQ2 and UBIAD1, linked to cardiovascular degeneration, Parkinson's disease, and mitochondrial dysfunction.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Nine-transmembrane helix architecture with large central cavity

ApUbiA contains nine transmembrane helices (TMs) arranged counterclockwise in a U shape around a large central cavity. The cavity is surrounded by TM1, TM2, and TM9 at the front and TM5 and TM6 from the back. C-terminal extensions of TM2, TM4, and TM6 kink to create short extramembrane helices and loops (HL2-3, HL4-5, HL6-7) that cap the central cavity. HL2-3 and HL6-7 each contain an Asp-rich motif (D54XXXD58 and D182XXXD186), while HL4-5 harbors a conserved Y115XXXK119 motif. All these conserved residues protrude into the central cavity for substrate binding and catalysis.

### Lateral portal enables catalysis within the membrane

One side of the central cavity has a lateral portal — an opening largely buried in the membrane delineated by kinked TM1 and TM9 (each with a proline in the middle). The portal allows long isoprenyl chains of IPP substrates to extend directly into the lipid bilayer. This enables UbiA to bind long-chain isoprenyl substrates (CoQ6 to CoQ10 in different species) and release prenylated products directly into the membrane — a key strategy for intramembrane enzymes.

### Substrate recognition and catalytic mechanism

The central cavity contains a basic pocket for PHB (with Arg43 contacting the carboxyl group) and a pyrophosphate-binding site coordinated by two Mg2+ ions bridging Asp54/Asp58 (Mg1) and Asp182/Asp186 (Mg2). GSPP binding induces local conformational changes: the Lys119–Asp54 interaction in the apo state is unlocked, and Lys119 recognizes the pyrophosphate while Asp54 interacts with Arg67. The loop containing Arg63 and Arg67 becomes ordered upon GSPP binding. Residues near the C1 atom of GSPP (Asn50, Asp175, Tyr178) are candidates for stabilizing the carbocation intermediate during the condensation reaction.

### Disease-relevant mutations in eukaryotic homologs

Mutations in eukaryotic COQ2 and UBIAD1 are linked to cardiovascular degeneration, Parkinson's disease, and mitochondrial dysfunction. Several disease mutations correspond to conserved residues in ApUbiA. Mutating these residues in E. coli UbiA (EcUbiA) lowers enzymatic activity or substrate binding affinity, confirming the functional conservation across the superfamily and providing a framework for understanding disease mechanisms.
