# TM287/288 Heterodimeric ABC Exporter from Thermotoga maritima

<div class="expr-badges"><span class="expr-badge expr-e-coli">E. coli</span></div>


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/Q9WYC3">UniProt: Q9WYC3</a> <a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/Q9WYC4">UniProt: Q9WYC4</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Thermotoga maritima (strain ATCC 43589 / MSB8 / DSM 3109 / JCM 10099)</span>

## Overview

TM287/288 is a heterodimeric ATP-binding cassette (ABC) exporter from
the thermophilic bacterium Thermotoga maritima. It consists of two
transmembrane domains (TMDs) and two asymmetric nucleotide-binding
domains (NBDs), with one catalytically impaired degenerate site and
one consensus site where ATP is hydrolyzed. The crystal structure of
the apo state at 2.53 A resolution reveals that the NBDs remain in
molecular contact even in the absence of nucleotides, providing
structural insights into allosteric cross-talk between the asymmetric
ATP-binding sites via D-loop-mediated communication.

Three outward-facing (OF) crystal structures were subsequently
determined at 3.2-4.2 A resolution in complex with single-domain
antibody fragments (sybody Sb_TM#35, nanobodies Nb_TM#1 and Nb_TM#2).
The OF structures show a fully closed, symmetric NBD dimer with two
ATP molecules and widely opened extracellular gate. The extracellular
gate is sealed by conserved aspartates (D41 of TM287, D65 of TM288)
that form hydrogen bonds with the opposite wing in the IF state.
Sybody Sb_TM#35 binds to an extracellular wing, traps the OF state,
and inhibits ATPase activity. The conserved aspartates constitute an
energy barrier for the IF-OF transition and are allosterically coupled
to NBD dissociation after ATP hydrolysis.

The isolated nucleotide-binding domains (NBDs) of TM287/288 — NBD287
(residues 330-577) and NBD288 (residues 353-592) — were crystallized
separately and their structures determined at 1.8-2.35 A resolution
(PDB 4Q7K, 4Q7L, 4Q7M). Key structural elements of the degenerate
ATP binding site are disordered in isolation and become structured
only in the full-length transporter context. The comparison reveals
interdomain contacts critical for proper assembly and functioning of
the heterodimeric ABC exporter.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### NBDs remain in contact without nucleotides

Unlike many other ABC exporters (e.g., [MSBA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/msba/)) where NBDs separate
in the absence of nucleotides, the NBDs of TM287/288 remain in
molecular contact in the apo state. This was confirmed by both
crystal structures and DEER spectroscopy measurements. The NBD
arrangement allows allosteric communication between the two ATP
binding sites during the entire transport cycle.

### Asymmetric D-loop function

The D-loops at the NBD interface function asymmetrically. The
D-loop at the degenerate site (NBD2, Asp523) ties the NBDs together
even in the absence of nucleotides. Mutation D523A increases ATPase
activity ~2-fold and decreases apparent ATP affinity (20.3 to
71.8 uM). The D-loop at the consensus site (NBD1, Asp501) is
highly flexible in the apo state and contacts NBD2 via its aspartate
side chain only when the degenerate site is occupied. Mutation
D501A causes a ~16-fold decrease in maximal ATPase activity,
demonstrating its critical role in ATP hydrolysis.

### Allosteric communication mechanism

Nucleotide binding at the degenerate site induces structural
rearrangements transmitted to the consensus site via the two
D-loops. The Walker B motif (Asp494) senses nucleotide-magnesium
complex at the degenerate site and transmits this information
via long-range interactions through the D-loop to the Walker A
motif of the consensus site. Hydrogen bonding networks between
NBDs are rearranged from 7 bonds (AMP-PNP-bound) to 4 bonds
(apo state). The consensus site D-loop aspartate is critical for
ATP hydrolysis in TM287/288 and substrate transport in the
homolog LmrCD.

### Inward-facing state dominance

DEER measurements show the conformational equilibrium of
TM287/288 is strongly shifted toward the inward-facing state
even in the presence of AMP-PNG. In contrast to the homodimeric
ABC exporter [MSBA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/msba/), [AMP-PNP (Adenylyl Imidodiphosphate)](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/amp-pnp/) does not lead to NBD closure or
transition to the outward-facing state in TM287/288, suggesting
major differences between homodimeric and heterodimeric ABC
exporters in response to nucleotide analogs.

### Outward-facing crystal structures reveal closed symmetric NBD dimer

Three OF structures of TM287/288 were solved in complex with single-domain
antibody fragments (sybody Sb_TM#35, nanobodies Nb_TM#1 and Nb_TM#2).
The closed NBD dimer sandwiches two ATPγS-Mg molecules between Walker A
and ABC signature motifs at both degenerate and consensus sites in a highly
symmetric manner. Distortions found at the catalytic dyad of the consensus
site in the IF state (E517 and H548 of TM288) relax during NBD closure to
adopt a hydrolysis-competent arrangement. Two possible Pi exit tunnels are
present at the consensus site. The TMDs are widely opened towards the
outside with an RMSD of 1.73 A to [Sav1866 Multidrug ABC Transporter](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/abc-transporters/sav1866/).

### Sybody Sb_TM#35 acts as a molecular clamp at the extracellular gate

The sybody Sb_TM#35 binds to the extracellular wing of TM287/288,
inserting four aromatic residues (Y33, W52, Y59, W113) between TM1 and
TM2 of TM287 and TM5' and TM6' of TM288. It traps the transporter in the
OF state, shifting the IF-OF equilibrium towards OF (IC50 = 66.1 nM for
ATPase inhibition). DEER spectroscopy confirmed the sybody increases mean
interspin distances at extracellular, intracellular, and NBD positions,
consistent with stabilizing the fully opened extracellular gate.

### Conserved aspartates seal the extracellular gate

D41 of TM287 and D65 of TM288 in TM1 establish hydrogen bonds with backbone
amides of the opposite wing in the IF state. These aspartates are conserved
in bacterial ABC exporters. Alanine substitutions (2xDtoA mutant) decrease
ATPase activity ~10-fold and shift the conformational equilibrium towards the
OF state, as shown by DEER. The 2xDtoA mutant also impairs drug transport
in the homologous ABC exporter EfrEF from Enterococcus faecalis, as shown by
[Ethidium - Fluorescent Intercalating Dye](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/ethidium/) accumulation assays in Lactococcus lactis. The DtoA and sybody
effects are synergistic, with Sb_TM#35 affinity increased ~8-fold for the
2xDtoA mutant (KD = 14 nM vs 110 nM for WT).

### Extracellular gate closure is coupled to NBD dissociation

Using state-specific binders (Sb_TM#35 and Nb_TM#1) as conformational probes
in SPR, the OF-to-IF transition was monitored over hours. The lifetime of the
OF state correlates with ATP hydrolysis kinetics: t1/2 of 45-49 min (EtoQ)
and 287-268 min (EtoA). Extracellular gate closure is required to dissociate
the NBD dimer after ATP hydrolysis and reset the transporter to the IF state.
This establishes a direct allosteric coupling between the extracellular gate
and the ATPase cycle, where the sealed gate provides mechanical force for
NBD separation.

### NBDs are partially unstructured and flexible in isolation

The isolated NBDs of TM287/288 are unable to hydrolyze ATP and do not homodimerize or heterodimerize. NBD287 (PDB 4Q7K) lacks electron density for sheet 1 of the RecA-like domain, and NBD288 (PDB 4Q7L) has a disordered D-loop. The helical domain rotates 12° (NBD287) or 5° (NBD288) relative to the full-length context, demonstrating remarkable subdomain plasticity required for accommodating coupling helices of TMDs and supporting conformational changes during the catalytic cycle.

### Degenerate site forms only in the full-length transporter

Structural elements constituting the degenerate ATP binding site of TM287/288 (Sheet 1 of NBD287 and D-loop of NBD288) are disordered in the isolated NBDs and become structured only through interdomain contacts with the opposite NBD and coupling helices of ICL1 and ICL4 of the TMDs. This explains why NBD287 does not bind nucleotides in isolation, and why a productive NBD dimer can only form in the full-length protein.

### Hydrogen bonding patterns differ fundamentally in isolated vs context-embedded NBDs

Comparison of isolated NBDs with their counterparts in full-length apo TM287/288 reveals completely different hydrogen bonding patterns for conserved key residues (Walker B, switch loop, Q-loop, D-loop). In the full-length context, these residues interact across domains—the Walker B Asp495 of NBD1 interacts with Q-loop Gln414 and the D-loop of NBD2, whereas in isolation Asp495 contacts the switch loop Gln526. This illustrates that interdomain contacts critically influence the conformation of catalytic residues.
