# Pea Light-Harvesting Complex II (LHC-II)


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/Q29411">UniProt: Q29411</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Sus scrofa</span>

## Overview

The pea light-harvesting complex II (LHC-II) is the major light-harvesting pigment-protein complex of photosystem II in plants. It forms a trimeric assembly that collects and transmits solar energy for photosynthesis in chloroplast thylakoid membranes. Each LHC-II monomer binds 14 chlorophyll molecules (8 chlorophyll a and 6 chlorophyll b), 4 carotenoids (2 [LUTEIN](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/lutein), 1 [Neoxanthin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/neoxanthin), and 1 [Violaxanthin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/violaxanthin)), and 2 lipids. The complex is central to photoprotection through non-photochemical quenching and plays a key role in the organization of thylakoid membrane stacks (grana).

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Chlorophyll and carotenoid arrangement

The 2.5 A structure reveals the mutual arrangement of 42 chlorophylls (a and b) and 12 carotenoids (2 [LUTEIN](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/lutein), 1 [Neoxanthin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/neoxanthin), 1 [Violaxanthin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/violaxanthin) per monomer) in the LHC-II trimer. The Qy dipole moments of the 42 chlorophylls sample all directions in space almost evenly to maximize light-harvesting efficiency. [LUTEIN](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/lutein) molecules 1 and 2 are coplanar with chlorophylls 2 and 5 respectively at interplane distances of 3.5 and 3.6 A, acting as triplet energy sinks for photoprotection.

### Non-photochemical quenching mechanism

The structure reveals a mechanism for the slow, xanthophyll-dependent component of non-photochemical quenching (NPQ). [Violaxanthin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/violaxanthin) in its binding site can be replaced by zeaxanthin via the xanthophyll cycle. Zeaxanthin, with a more extended pi system of 11 conjugated double bonds compared to [Violaxanthin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/violaxanthin)'s 9, accepts energy from nearby chlorophylls and dissipates it as heat, converting the LHC-II trimer into an energy sink.

### Membrane appression and grana formation

The stromal surface of LHC-II is essentially flat and negatively charged, apart from the first 15 residues which contain four positive charges. The nonspecific interaction of the positively charged N-terminal peptides with the negatively charged surface of trimers in apposed thylakoid membranes likely plays a major role in the cohesion of chloroplast grana, analogous to a velcro-like mechanism.
