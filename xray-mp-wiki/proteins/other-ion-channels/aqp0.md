# Aquaporin 0 (AQP0) from Bos taurus

<div class="expr-badges"><span class="expr-badge expr-native-tissue">Native tissue</span></div>


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/P06624">UniProt: P06624</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Bos taurus</span>

## Overview

Aquaporin 0 (AQP0) is the major integral membrane protein of the vertebrate lens fiber cell, accounting for over 60% of the fiber cell plasma membrane protein complement. The x-ray structure of bovine AQP0 (bAQP0) was determined at 2.2 A resolution, revealing a water-selective channel with eight discrete water molecules within the channel lumen. The selectivity for water is based on the identity and location of signature amino acid residues that are hallmarks of the water-selective arm of the [Aquaporin](/xray-mp-wiki/concepts/transport-mechanisms/aquaporin/) family. The channel lumen is narrowed by two quasi-2-fold related tyrosine side chains (Tyr-23 and Tyr-149) that account for reduced water conductance relative to other aquaporins. The structure showed no evidence of channel gating at high pH (pH 10), contrary to the postulated pH-gating mechanism.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Channel architecture and water selectivity

The AQP0 channel lumen is narrowed at two constrictions formed by quasi-2-fold related tyrosine side chains: Tyr-23 (extracellular) and Tyr-149 (cytoplasmic). Together with Val-56, Gly-64, His-66, and Phe-75, Tyr-149 forms the narrowest region of the channel accepting a sphere with a maximum diameter of 1.5 Å. The channel contains signature NPA (Asn-Pro-Ala) motifs and the aromatic/arginine (ar/R) constriction typical of water-specific aquaporins. Eight discrete water molecules were identified within the channel, confirming it is functionally open to the passage of water.

### Reduced water conductance relative to other aquaporins

AQP0 conducts water 15- to 45-fold less than AQP1. The reduced conductance is attributed to the narrow constrictions formed by Tyr-23 and Tyr-149, which are not present in other aquaporins. The lens fiber cell compensates for having an incredibly large number of AQP0 channels (approximately 60% of membrane protein by weight) by having each channel conduct water very poorly, maintaining homogeneous transparency throughout the lens while ensuring a uniform response to osmotic challenge.

### No evidence for pH-dependent channel gating

Contrary to previous studies that suggested a closed conformation at low pH and gating at high pH, the x-ray structure at pH 10 showed no structural changes to the channel or the molecule as a whole. The channel was found to be functionally open at both pH 6.0 and pH 10, with no evidence of channel gating by association of the extracellular domains of AQP0.

### Cell-cell adhesion function

AQP0 has a proposed dual role as both a water channel and a cell-cell adhesion molecule in the lens. The extracellular surface of bAQP0 has a net positive charge of +4 per monomer (+16 per tetramer), which may mediate interaction with negatively charged surfaces. AQP0 localizes to junctional plaques and interdigitations of the lens, suggesting a role in mechanical adhesion of adjacent fiber cells, resisting shear forces during optical accommodation and minimizing light scatter by keeping intercellular spaces small.

### Congenital cataracts from AQP0 mutations

Two independent mutations in AQP0 cause congenital cataracts: Glu134Gly and Thr138Arg. Both residues are highly conserved in essentially all aquaporins and are located in the middle of the M5 helix. Glu134 hydrogen bonds to successive water molecules and orients the line of four backbone carbonyls (Gly-180, Ala-181, Gly-182, Met-183) that provide hydrogen bond acceptors for adjacent channel waters. Glu134Gly removes this orienting function, while Thr138Arg removes the anion-pi interaction with Glu-134 and introduces a bulky side chain, both distorting the functional carbonyl line in the channel.
