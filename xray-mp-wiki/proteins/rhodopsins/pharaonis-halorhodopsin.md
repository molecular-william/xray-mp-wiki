# pharaonis Halorhodopsin (phR)


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/Q3ITX1">UniProt: Q3ITX1</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Natronomonas pharaonis DSM 2160</span>

## Overview

pharaonis [Halorhodopsin (HR) from Halobacterium salinarum](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/rhodopsins/halorhodopsin/) (phR) is a light-driven chloride ion pump from Natronomonas pharaonis. It contains [Retinal](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/retinal/) as a chromophore and uses light energy to import chloride ions into the cell against the membrane potential. phR forms a trimeric complex with the [Carotenoid](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/carotenoid/) [Bacterioruberin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/bacterioruberin/), which binds to crevices between adjacent protein subunits. The 2.0 A crystal structure reveals seven transmembrane helices (A to G) characteristic of archaeal rhodopsins, plus an amphipathic alpha-helix (A') at the N-terminus that forms a hydrophobic cap covering the extracellular surface. A chloride ion is bound near the [Retinal](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/retinal/) Schiff base, fixed by Thr126 and Ser130 in helix C. The anion uptake pathway features a hydrogen-bonding network of four ionisable residues (Arg123, Glu234, Arg176, His100) and seven water molecules, while the cytoplasmic half is filled mostly by hydrophobic residues forming an energetic barrier against anion transport.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### N-terminal amphipathic helix A' and hydrophobic cap

phR possesses a unique N-terminal amphipathic alpha-helix (A') not found in other archaeal rhodopsins. This helix, together with the long BC loop, forms a large hydrophobic cap covering most of the extracellular surface. The cap prevents rapid ion exchange between the active centre and the extracellular medium, explaining the slow decay of the O intermediate in phR compared to shR.

### Chloride binding site and anion uptake pathway

The chloride ion in the primary binding site is coordinated by the [Retinal](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/retinal/) Schiff base (3.2 A), Thr126 OH (3.2 A), Ser130 OH (3.2 A), and a water molecule (Wat502, 3.3 A). The anion uptake pathway consists of four ionisable residues (Arg123, Glu234, Arg176, His100) and seven water molecules forming a long hydrogen-bonding network. Glu234, which is known to deprotonate during the photocycle, likely functions as a gate in the uptake pathway.

### Structural comparison with shR explains different chloride affinities

The primary binding site structure differs between phR and shR. Thr126 OH in phR directs toward the chloride ion, while Thr111 OH in shR is hydrogen-bonded to a palmitate molecule. This subtle difference explains the higher chloride affinity of phR (Kd ~1 mM) compared to shR (Kd ~10 mM). The extracellular hydrophobic cap in phR replaces the secondary chloride binding site found in shR.

### Bacterioruberin binds in inter-subunit crevices

The [Carotenoid](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/carotenoid/) [Bacterioruberin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/bacterioruberin/) binds to crevices between adjacent subunits in the phR trimer, at the interface between helices AB and DE of neighbouring protomers. This binding mode is similar to that seen in [Archaerhodopsin-2](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/rhodopsins/archaerhodopsin-2/), despite low sequence identity (28%). [Bacterioruberin](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/bacterioruberin/) likely protects the protein against harmful sunlight intensities and/or regulates pumping activity by sensing membrane potential.

### Proton/HCl antiporter mechanism

The cytoplasmic half of phR is mostly hydrophobic with no ionisable residues, creating a large energetic barrier against anion transport. A long cavity from Lys256 CO to Thr71 OH suggests a water-mediated proton transfer pathway. The structure supports a model where phR functions as a proton/HCl antiporter rather than a simple chloride pump, with the net output equivalent to active Cl- transport.
