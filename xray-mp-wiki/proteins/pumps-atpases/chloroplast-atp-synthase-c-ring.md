# Chloroplast ATP Synthase c-Ring from Pisum sativum

<div class="expr-badges"><span class="expr-badge expr-native-tissue">Native tissue</span></div>


<a class="badge badge-uniprot" href="https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/P08212">UniProt: P08212</a>

<span class="expr-badge">Pisum sativum</span>

## Overview

The chloroplast [ATP](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/atp/) synthase c-ring from Pisum sativum (green pea) is a
homo-tetradecameric ring composed of 14 identical c-subunits that forms
the rotor of the FoF1-[ATP](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/atp/) synthase. The ring structure was determined at
3.4 A resolution from a native preparation, revealing a concave barrel
shape with a pronounced waist in the middle. The ring is 60.5 A long with
outer diameters of 60.5 A (stroma side) and 61.5 A (lumen side), and an
inner diameter of 35 A. The narrowest diameter (27 A) is at Ile22 and
Gly23. Each monomer is an 81-residue hairpin-shaped polypeptide with two
transmembrane helices. The essential proton-binding site glutamate (Glu61)
is conserved and its coordination resembles that of cyanobacterial c-rings.

## Biological / Functional Insights

### Elastic network analysis of c-ring dynamics

Elastic network analysis (GNM/ANM) of the c-ring revealed five dominant
modes of motion in three classes: (I) ellipsoidal contraction/expansion,
(II) opposite twisting of luminal vs stromal surfaces, and (III) kinking
of hairpin monomers in the middle resulting in bending/stretching.

### Type I motion - Ellipsoidal deformation

Type I motion (ellipsoidal) is proposed to affect c-ring interaction
with subunits a and bb' in Fo, facilitating proton translocation and
torque generation by strengthening/weakening electrostatic interactions
between Glu61 and the essential arginine on subunit a.

### Type II motion - Twisting deformation

Type II motion (twisting) is proposed to serve as an elastic buffer
between Fo and F1, contributing to smooth torque transmission during
[ATP](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/atp/) synthesis/hydrolysis.

### Conserved hinge at proton-binding site

The essential acidic residue Glu61 at the proton-binding site is part
of the hinge region identified in the GNM analysis, consistent with its
functional importance in proton translocation.

### Conservation across species

Similar dominant modes were found in c-rings from other organisms (8-15
monomers) and in V-ATPase rings, suggesting conserved mechanical
principles in rotary ATPase biology.
