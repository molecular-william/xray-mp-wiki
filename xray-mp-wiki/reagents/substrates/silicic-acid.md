# Silicic Acid

## Overview

Silicic acid (H4SiO4) is the bioavailable form of silicon in biological systems. It is a polar but neutral molecule at physiological pH, with a pKa of 9.25 and solubility of approximately 2 mM in soil solutions. In plants, silicic acid is actively transported across root cell membranes by NIP (Nodulin26-like Intrinsic Protein) family transporters, particularly OsNIP2;1 in rice. Once inside plant cells, silicic acid spontaneously polymerizes to form amorphous silica bodies that provide structural and mechanical stability. Silicic acid transport is also responsible for arsenic accumulation in rice grains, as OsNIP2;1 co-transports arsenous acid.


## Properties

- **Chemical name**: orthosilicic acid
- **Molecular weight**: 96.11 g/mol
- **Class**: inorganic compound (metalloid oxyacid)

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Substrate for silicon uptake transporters

Silicic acid is the physiological substrate transported by NIP-family aquaporins. OsNIP2;1 from rice mediates efficient silicic acid uptake in root cells, with the five-residue selectivity filter (TGSGR) providing hydrogen bond donors and acceptors for the four hydroxyl groups of silicic acid. The silicic acid molecule (diameter 6 +/- 0.5 A) translocates through the channel forming transient hydrogen bonds with SF residues, especially Thr65 and Ser207.


### Silicon biofortification

Understanding silicic acid transport mechanisms enables rational manipulation of silicon accumulation in crops for biofortification. Silicon provides resistance to abiotic and biotic stresses, improves light interception, and minimizes transpiration losses in plants.
