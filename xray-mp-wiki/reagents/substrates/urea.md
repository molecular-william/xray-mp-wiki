# Urea

## Overview

Urea (CH4N2O) is a small polar molecule with a strong dipole moment (4.6 D) that serves as the physiological substrate for urea transporters (UTs). It is a key nitrogen metabolism compound in many organisms and is used as an osmolyte in the mammalian kidney. Urea cannot readily diffuse across lipid bilayers due to its high polarity, requiring specialized transport proteins.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to Urea transporter (UT) family

Urea carbonyl oxygen hydrogen-bonds to backbone amide nitrogens and side-chain hydroxyls of threonines. Urea amide nitrogens form hydrogen bonds with oxygen ladder residues. Amide-nitrogen to phenylalanine amide-pi stacking interactions also contribute to binding.

- **Pocket volume**: Constricted selectivity filter pore (~16 angstrom long)
- **Key residues**: Oxygen ladder residues from pore helices (Pa, Pb) and transmembrane helices T3a, T3b, T5a, T5b; Thr130, Thr294; Phe residues flanking the filter
