# 4-Hydroxytamoxifen

## Overview

4-Hydroxytamoxifen (4-OH-Tam) is a primary metabolite of tamoxifen formed by 4-hydroxylation. It is approximately 30- to 100-fold more potent in vivo as an anti-estrogen than tamoxifen itself, although the metabolic pathway producing it contributes minimally to overall tamoxifen metabolism. 4-Hydroxytamoxifen was crystallized in complex with the NavMs sodium channel (PDB 6SXG), revealing binding at both inner and outer sites near the intracellular gate. It inhibits sodium currents with an IC50 of 297 nM against NavMs and 2.1 uM against human Na_v1.2. The D220E and E227D mutations in NavMs disrupt the inner binding site and increase the IC50 to 3.6 uM and 2.8 uM, respectively.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to NavMs voltage-gated sodium channel

4-Hydroxytamoxifen binds to both inner and outer sites near the intracellular gate of the NavMs sodium channel. The inner site (PDB 6SXG) involves hydrogen bond interactions with M222 (intracellular gate residue), K226, and E227. The hydroxyl group of 4-OH-Tam forms additional hydrogen bonds with the protein. Mutations D220E and E227D disrupt optimal hydrogen bond coordination distances, increasing the free energy of binding by 1.4 kcal/mol and 2.4 kcal/mol, respectively. The binding pocket shares geometric features with the estrogen receptor, including hydrogen bonds with aspartic acid (D220 in NavMs, D351 in ER) and glutamate (E227 in NavMs, E353 in ER).
