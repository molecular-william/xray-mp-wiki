# MitTx Toxin

## Overview

MitTx (MITxin) is a heterodimeric toxin from the venom of the Texas coral snake (Micrurus tener). It consists of an alpha subunit with a Kunitz-type domain and a beta subunit that is a phospholipase A2 (PLA2) homolog. MitTx acts as a potent and selective agonist of acid-sensing ion channel 1a (ASIC1a), binding to the wrist, palm, and thumb domains and stabilizing the channel in a physiologically relevant, open-channel, sodium-selective state. The toxin-channel complex buries 1,350 A^2 of solvent-accessible surface area and the binding site overlaps with psalmotoxin, explaining mutual exclusivity of their actions.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Structural probe for open channel state

MitTx binds to and stabilizes ASIC1a in an open-channel conformation, enabling the first high-resolution structure of an open ASIC. The toxin-channel complex was crystallized at pH 5.5 with three different conditions (Na+, amiloride, Cs+) yielding structures at 2.1, 2.3, and 2.6 A resolution.


### Functional agonist for ASIC1a

At pH 7.4, MitTx application induces a slow inward current in cells expressing the delta13 construct, reaching a steady-state amplitude of approximately 15% of the peak amplitude from low pH activation. MitTx also slows channel closure rate and increases steady-state current upon step to pH 5.5.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to ASIC1a

Phe 14 of alpha subunit inserts as a hook into the subunit interface. Lys 16 of alpha subunit inserts as a barb into the wrist-TM1 interface. Beta subunit engages palm and thumb domains. Heterodimer buries 1,350 A^2 of solvent-accessible surface area (800 A^2 from alpha, 550 A^2 from beta).
