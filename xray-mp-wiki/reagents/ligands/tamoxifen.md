# Tamoxifen

## Overview

Tamoxifen is a triphenylethylene-based selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM) developed as a treatment for breast cancer over 40 years ago, currently prescribed to more than 200 million patients worldwide. Beyond its estrogen receptor (ER) activity, tamoxifen was shown to bind to a previously unidentified drug binding site within the [NAVMS](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/navms/) voltage-gated sodium channel. Tamoxifen also binds to the human Emopamil-Binding Protein ([EBP](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/human-ebp/)), a sterol isomerase in the [Cholesterol](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/cholesterol/) biosynthesis pathway, contributing to drug resistance.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### EBP structural studies

Tamoxifen was co-crystallized with human [EBP](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/human-ebp/) (Emopamil-Binding Protein) and the structure determined at 3.5 A resolution. The compound binds in the membrane cavity of [EBP](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/human-ebp/), with its positively-charged amine group recognized by Asn193 and Glu122, and stabilized by pi-cation interaction with Trp196. Mutagenesis studies show that [EBP](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/human-ebp/) binding lowers the availability of intracellular tamoxifen, causing drug resistance in cancer cells.


### NavMs structural studies

Tamoxifen binds to both inner and outer sites near the intracellular gate of the [NAVMS](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/navms/) sodium channel. The inner site involves hydrogen bond interactions with M222 (intracellular gate residue), K226, and E227. Tamoxifen inhibits sodium currents with an IC50 of 830 nM against [NAVMS](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/navms/), acting by stabilizing the non-conducting inactivated state.
