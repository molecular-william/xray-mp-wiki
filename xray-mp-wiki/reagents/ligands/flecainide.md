# Flecainide

## Overview

Flecainide is a class IC antiarrhythmic drug (AAD) used for treatment of atrial
arrhythmias. It blocks voltage-gated sodium channels by binding to a receptor site in the
central cavity of the pore, on the intracellular side of the selectivity filter. A crystal
structure of the bacterial sodium channel NavAb in complex with flecainide revealed a
single well-defined binding pose, with the positively charged tertiary amino group
projecting into the selectivity filter and the fluoromethylated aromatic rings projecting
toward the wall of the central cavity near the fenestrations. Flecainide has a larger
molecular volume (331.9 Å³) and polar surface area (59.6 Å²) compared with lidocaine.
The fenestrations in sodium channels control flecainide's access to its binding site during
resting-state block; widening the fenestrations (F203A mutation) increases potency 7.7-fold,
while narrowing them (F203W) decreases potency 6.5-fold.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

- Effective for maintaining sinus rhythm in atrial fibrillation
- Tight binding to sodium channels (class IC kinetics, slow dissociation)

### Disadvantages

- Proarrhythmic in patients with structural heart disease (CAST trial)
- Slower off-rate compared to class IB drugs like lidocaine
