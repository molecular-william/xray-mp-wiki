# Misoprostol

## Overview

Misoprostol is a synthetic prostaglandin E1 analog used clinically as a uterotonic drug to prevent and treat postpartum hemorrhage, a leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide. It is on the World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines due to its affordability, stability at room temperature, oral bioavailability, and clinical efficacy. Misoprostol is administered as a methyl ester prodrug (misoprostol-ME) that is rapidly converted by cellular esterases to the biologically active free acid form (misoprostol-FA). Misoprostol-FA binds and activates the EP3 receptor, inducing myometrial contraction. The crystal structure of misoprostol-FA bound to the EP3 receptor was determined at 2.5 A resolution, revealing the atomic details of its mechanism of action.


## Properties


## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### EP3 receptor agonist for labor induction

Misoprostol-FA is a potent agonist of the EP3 receptor, activating Gi
protein signaling. The 2.5 A crystal structure of EP3 bound to
misoprostol-FA (Audet et al., 2018) reveals that misoprostol binds in a
completely enclosed binding pocket. The ligand engages the receptor through
a salt bridge between its carboxylate and Arg333(7.40), hydrogen bonding
with Tyr114(2.65), Thr206(EL2), Ser336(7.43), and Gln339(7.46), and
hydrophobic contacts. A structured water molecule coordinates the E-ring
hydroxyl. The branched carbon 16 (C16 methyl group) contacts Gly141(3.36),
constraining the omega-chain orientation and contributing to EP3
selectivity over other EP receptors.


### Structure-activity relationship

Misoprostol differs from natural PGE1/PGE2 by the presence of a methyl
ester (prodrug form) or free acid (active form) and a methyl branch at
carbon 16 of the omega-chain. This C16 methylation at the omega-chain is a
key selectivity determinant for EP3 over EP1 and EP2. Residue 6.51 is Met
in EP1 and Phe in EP2, creating a steric clash with the branched
omega-chain, while EP3 and EP4 have Leu at this position, accommodating the
branch.


### Limitations and side effects

Despite its clinical utility, misoprostol lacks receptor and tissue
selectivity, activating other EP receptors in the gastrointestinal tract,
cardiovascular system, and central nervous system. Side effects include
diarrhea, uterine hyperstimulation, fetal heart abnormalities, and uterine
rupture. The enclosed binding pocket may contribute to the long residence
time of misoprostol on EP3, potentially explaining some of the
pharmacokinetic properties associated with adverse effects.
