# D-Alanine

## Overview

D-Alanine is a D-enantiomer of the amino acid alanine, used as a ligand to study
enantiomer selectivity in amino acid transporters. Unlike [L-Alanine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/substrates/l-alanine/), which is the
dominant form in proteins, D-alanine plays important roles in bacterial cell wall
remodeling and serves as a nitrogen source in archaea such as Methanococcus
maripaludis. In structural biology, D-alanine is used as a substrate ligand for
crystallizing amino acid transporters to understand the molecular basis of
D-enantiomer recognition.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Crystallization Ligand for AgcS Transporter

50 mM D-alanine was included in the crystallization of AgcS (alanine/glycine:cation
symporter) from Methanococcus maripaludis complexed with Fab antibody fragments.
The D-alanine-bound structure revealed that the small hydrophobic binding pocket
of AgcS accommodates both L- and D-alanine equally well, with stereoselectivity
determined by the polarity of residues at the binding site surface.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to AgcS alanine/glycine:cation symporter

Coordinated by main-chain carbonyls of Ala-74, Thr-75, Ser-274, and side-chain of Glu-276 (amino group); hydrogen bonds with Gly-77, Thr-78, Gln-170 (carboxyl group)

- **Key residues**: Ile-165, Phe-273, Thr-366, Trp-370 (hydrophobic pocket)
