# Memantine

## Overview

Memantine is an aminoadamantane derivative clinically used in the treatment of Alzheimer's disease. It acts as a pore blocker of pentameric ligand-gated ion channels (pLGICs) and N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptors. Memantine is structurally similar to rimantadine and binds at the extracellular entryway of the channel pore in ELIC, trapping the channel in a closed conformation.


## Properties


## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Pore blocker of pentameric ligand-gated ion channels

Memantine blocks ion conduction at pLGICs by binding in the channel pore. In ELIC, memantine binds between the 13' and 16' residues of the M2 pore-lining helix, at the extracellular entryway of the channel pore. The ELIC F16'S mutation increases memantine sensitivity approximately 14-fold (IC50 from 118 microM to 8.3 microM).


### Alzheimer's disease therapeutic

Memantine is clinically used to improve cognition in Alzheimer's disease, likely through moderate-affinity uncompetitive blockade of NMDA-type glutamate receptors.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

- Clinically approved for Alzheimer's disease with well-characterized pharmacology
- Well-characterised binding at extracellular pore entryway in closed channel

### Disadvantages

- Moderate potency; requires F16'S mutation in ELIC for high sensitivity
- May induce short-lived conformational state not captured by crystallography
