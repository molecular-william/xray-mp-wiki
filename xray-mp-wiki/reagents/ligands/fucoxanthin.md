# Fucoxanthin

## Overview

Fucoxanthin (Fx) is a carotenoid pigment found in brown algae and diatoms that gives them their characteristic brown color. It contains an unusual allenic bond and a conjugated carbonyl group, giving it large solvent-dependent spectral shifts. In fucoxanthin-chlorophyll a/c-binding proteins (FCPs), Fx serves as both a major light-harvesting pigment absorbing blue-green light (the "green gap" where Chl a and b absorb weakly) and a photoprotective quencher that dissipates excess energy as heat. FCP binds seven Fxs per monomer, far more than the 3-4 carotenoids in LHCII, and each Fx is surrounded by one or more Chls with close pi-pi contacts (4.0 A or less), enabling energy transfer with >90% efficiency and as fast as 75 fs.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Blue-green light harvesting in diatom FCPs

Fx absorbs light in the blue-green region (450-550 nm) that penetrates deeper into water but is not effectively used by chlorophyll a/b-containing organisms. Its conjugated carbonyl group causes a large redshift in polar environments, enabling tuning of absorption by the protein scaffold. Each of the seven Fxs in FCP has a distinct binding environment with different hydrophilicities at its two end groups, producing varied spectral properties.


### Energy dissipation and photoprotection

The close association of every Fx with one or more Chls enables efficient energy dissipation. Under excess light, Fx can accept excitation energy from Chls and dissipate it as heat, protecting the photosynthetic apparatus from photodamage. Fx301 and Fx302 may undergo conformational changes triggered by protonation of acidic residues, leading to quenching of excitation energy from nearby Chls.
