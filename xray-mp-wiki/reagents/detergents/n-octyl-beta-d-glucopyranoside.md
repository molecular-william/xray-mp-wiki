# n-Octyl-beta-D-glucopyranoside (OG)

## Overview

n-Octyl-beta-D-glucopyranoside (octylglucoside, OG) is a mild nonionic detergent with an 8-carbon alkyl chain and a single glucose head group. It is a short-chain alkyl glucoside commonly used in membrane protein crystallography for crystal washing and detergent exchange. In the 1.55 Å structure of bacteriorhodopsin, OG was used at 0.1% (w/v) to remove adhering cubic lipid phase from crystals prior to data collection.

## Properties

- **Chemical name**: n-Octyl-beta-D-glucopyranoside
- **Molecular weight**: 296.37 g/mol
- **Class**: detergent-nonionic

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Crystal washing for bacteriorhodopsin

Used at 0.1% (w/v) in 3 M sodium phosphate pH 5.6 to soak bacteriorhodopsin crystals grown in cubic lipid phase, removing adhering monoolein lipid phase before X-ray data collection. This treatment enabled collection of diffraction data to 1.55 Å resolution from crystals grown in the lipidic cubic phase.


### Membrane protein crystallization

OG serves as a crystallization detergent for various membrane proteins. Its small head group and short alkyl chain allow it to access tight binding pockets on membrane proteins and form well-ordered crystal lattices.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

- Short C8 alkyl chain and single glucose head group allow OG to access tight hydrophobic pockets on membrane proteins
- Well-characterized detergent with predictable phase behavior in crystallization screens
- Readily removed by dialysis due to high CMC (20-28 mM), facilitating detergent exchange

### Disadvantages

- High CMC requires higher detergent concentrations, increasing cost for large-scale preparations
- Short alkyl chain provides less hydrophobic stabilization, potentially destabilizing sensitive membrane proteins
- May partially denature sensitive membrane proteins due to relatively short C8 chain providing less hydrophobic stabilization
