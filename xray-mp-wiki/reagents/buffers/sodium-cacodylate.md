# Sodium Cacodylate

## Overview

Sodium cacodylate (dimethylarsinic acid sodium salt) is an organic buffer
commonly used in protein crystallography, particularly for membrane protein
crystallization. It has a useful pH range of 5.0-7.4 (pKa 6.27) and is
frequently employed in crystallization screens as a buffer for PEG-based
conditions. The arsenic-containing buffer provides stable pH in the slightly
acidic to neutral range and is compatible with many precipitants including
PEGs and salts.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Crystallization buffer for membrane proteins

Sodium cacodylate at pH 6.5 is used extensively in membrane protein crystallization,
particularly for aquaporins. The 2.5 A structure of E. coli AqpZ was obtained
from crystals grown in 100 mM sodium cacodylate pH 6.5 with 28% PEG MME 2000
and 200 mM MgCl2. It was also used for crystallization of AqpZ selectivity
filter mutants at similar concentrations (25-30% PEG 2000 MME, 100 mM sodium
cacodylate pH 6.5, 50-100 mM MgCl2).

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

- Stable pH in the 5.0-7.4 range, useful for many protein crystallization conditions
- Compatible with PEG precipitants and divalent cations
- Good buffering capacity at pH 6.5, which is optimal for many membrane protein crystallization screens

### Disadvantages

- Contains arsenic (toxic; requires careful handling)
- Not compatible with reducing agents (thiols react with cacodylate)
- Not suitable for biological assays due to toxicity
