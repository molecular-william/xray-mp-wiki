# Barium Chloride

## Overview

Barium chloride (BaCl2) is an inorganic salt that dissociates into Ba2+ ions in aqueous solution. Ba2+ is a classic permeant blocker of potassium (K+) channels, binding with high affinity to the selectivity filter and interrupting K+ flow. Barium block experiments are a powerful tool to investigate the selectivity of ion-binding sites along the pore of potassium channels, exploiting the so-called "external lock-in effect" whereby external K+ binding impedes Ba2+ forward translocation.


## Properties

- **Chemical name**: barium chloride
- **Class**: inorganic salt

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Potassium channel block studies

BaCl2 is used at concentrations of 0-10 mM to study barium block of the [KCSA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/kcsa/) potassium channel. Wild-type [KCSA](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/kcsa/) crystals were soaked in solutions containing varying BaCl2 concentrations (1-10 mM) with 5 mM KCl, revealing Ba2+ binding to the innermost selectivity filter site S4 in the open-gate conformation. Higher BaCl2 concentrations (up to 10 mM) were needed for external block, consistent with Ba2+ being an open-state blocker entering from the intracellular side.


### MthK gating ring activation and structural studies

BaCl2 at 100 mM is used as a ligand to activate the [MTHK](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/mthk/) RCK domain gating ring. Ba2+ selectively binds to the C1 activation site (determined by residues D184 and E210), overlapping with the Ca2+ activation site. Electrophysiological recordings show Ba2+ can activate reconstituted [MTHK](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/mthk/) channels with EC50 values of 54.8-81.1 mM depending on pH. The D184N mutation abolishes Ba2+ activation, confirming C1 as the primary activation site. Ba2+-bound [MTHK](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/mthk/) gating ring was crystallized at 3.1 A resolution (PDB 4EI2), revealing an intermediate-activated conformation distinct from the fully activated Ca2+-bound form.
