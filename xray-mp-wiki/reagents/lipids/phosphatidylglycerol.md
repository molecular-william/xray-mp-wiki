# Phosphatidylglycerol

## Overview

Phosphatidylglycerol (PG) is a glycerophospholipid consisting of a diglyceride backbone with a [Glycerol](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/additives/glycerol/) head group. PG is a major phospholipid component of bacterial membranes and is found predominantly in the inner leaflet of the inner membrane and the inner leaflet of the outer membrane in Gram-negative bacteria. PG is one of the primary lipid species bound by MCE (mammalian cell entry) family proteins including [E. coli MlaD MCE Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/structural-adhesion/mlaD/), [E. coli PqiB Syringe-like MCE Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/structural-adhesion/pqiB/), and [E. coli YebT Tube-like MCE Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/structural-adhesion/yebT/), suggesting its role as a substrate for periplasmic lipid transport. PG species (including PG 30:1) are also detected in [MSCS](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/voltage-gated-channels/mscs/) samples by mass spectrometry, though PE is the predominant phospholipid bound to the channel. In plant thylakoid membranes, PG is intimately involved in light-harvesting complex II ([LHC-II](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/photosynthesis/pea-light-harvesting-complex-ii/)) trimer formation. IM-MS analysis of the ammonium transporter AmtB revealed unexpectedly high selectivity for PG, with 8 PG molecules resolved in the 2.3 A crystal structure (PDB 4NH2) and a specific PG-binding site formed by conformational changes in residues 70-81.
