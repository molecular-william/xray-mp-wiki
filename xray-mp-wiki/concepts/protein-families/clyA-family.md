# ClyA Family (Alpha-Helical Pore-Forming Toxins)

## Overview
The ClyA (or HlyE/SheA) family is a group of alpha-helical pore-forming toxins characterized by an elongated bundle of long amphipathic alpha helices (~90 A) with a head domain folded across one end. The family is defined by pfam05791 and includes ClyA/HlyE/SheA from Escherichia coli, the HblB and NheA/NheC components of the Bacillus cereus hemolytic and non-hemolytic toxin complexes, and Cry6Aa from Bacillus thuringiensis. The core fold consists of five major helices (alphaA-alphaE), with the head domain containing a beta tongue region that carries a putative transmembrane sequence. ClyA/HlyE (PDB 1QOY) was the first family member to be structurally characterized, revealing the elongated alpha-helical bundle architecture. These toxins form oligomeric pores (8-mer to 13-mer) in target cell membranes, with the beta tongue region inserting into the membrane to line the pore. Despite sharing very low sequence identity (10-12%), family members maintain a conserved fold, and structures have been solved by molecular replacement using existing family members as search models.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- ClyA (E. coli Cytotoxin) — The founding member of the family. ClyA (PDB 1QOY) is an alpha-helical pore-forming toxin that forms 8-mer or 13-mer pores in target cell membranes.
- NheA (B. cereus Non-Hemolytic Enterotoxin Component) — NheA shares the conserved alpha-helical bundle fold and was used as a molecular replacement model (Z-score 21%) for solving the Cry6Aa structure.
