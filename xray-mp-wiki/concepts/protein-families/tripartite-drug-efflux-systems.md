# Tripartite Drug Efflux Systems

## Overview
Tripartite drug efflux systems are macromolecular assemblies in Gram-negative bacteria that span both the inner and outer membranes, enabling the direct extrusion of antibiotics, toxic compounds, and other substrates from the cell into the external medium. These systems consist of three essential components: an inner membrane transporter (typically belonging to the RND, MFS, or ABC transporter families), a periplasmic adaptor protein (membrane fusion protein, MFP), and an outer membrane channel (outer membrane factor, OMF). The best-characterized example is the AcrAB-TolC system from Escherichia coli, where AcrB (RND-type inner membrane transporter), AcrA (periplasmic adaptor), and TolC (outer membrane channel) assemble into a continuous conduit of ~130 A in length. Substrates are captured by the inner membrane transporter and actively pumped through the adaptor protein into the TolC channel, bypassing the periplasm entirely. These systems confer multidrug resistance and are major contributors to antibiotic resistance in Gram-negative pathogens such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa, E. coli, and Acinetobacter baumannii.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- AcrB-AcrA-TolC (E. coli) — The prototypical tripartite RND efflux pump. AcrB captures antibiotics from the inner membrane or periplasm, AcrA bridges the periplasmic gap, and TolC provides the outer membrane channel.
- MexAB-OprM (P. aeruginosa) — A homologous tripartite system from P. aeruginosa that exports beta-lactams, quinolones, and other clinically important antibiotics.
