# Complex III Assembly

## Overview
Complex III (cytochrome bc1 complex, or ubiquinol-cytochrome c reductase) assembly is the process by which the 11 subunits of respiratory complex III in the mitochondrial inner membrane are synthesized, cofactor-loaded, and assembled into a functional homodimeric complex. Complex III is a central component of the electron transport chain, where it catalyzes electron transfer from ubiquinol to cytochrome c coupled to proton translocation via the Q-cycle mechanism.


## Mechanism/Details
Complex III assembly occurs through a modular, stepwise pathway involving dedicated assembly factors. The catalytic core subunits (cytochrome b, cytochrome c1, and the Rieske iron-sulfur protein) are assembled first, with the mitochondrial DNA-encoded cytochrome b serving as the assembly scaffold. The Rieske iron-sulfur protein requires the BCS1 AAA-ATPase for chaperone-mediated membrane insertion. The remaining eight supernumerary subunits assemble around this catalytic core. The process involves multiple quality control checkpoints, including incorporation of heme bH, heme bL, and the [2Fe-2S] cluster cofactors. Defects in complex III assembly cause mitochondrial disorders including GRACILE syndrome and Bjornstad syndrome.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- BCS1 — AAA-ATPase chaperone essential for Rieske iron-sulfur protein insertion into complex III during assembly; mutations cause GRACILE syndrome and complex III deficiency
- Cytochrome b (MT-CYB) — mtDNA-encoded catalytic core subunit that serves as the scaffold for complex III assembly
