# Lipoprotein

## Overview
Lipoproteins are a class of proteins that are post-translationally
modified by the covalent attachment of lipid moieties, typically at a
conserved N-terminal cysteine residue (the lipobox motif). In bacteria, the
most common acylation involves the attachment of diacylglycerol via a
thioether linkage to the N-terminal cysteine (prolipoprotein diacylglyceryl
transferase, Lgt, step), followed by signal peptide cleavage (LspA, signal
peptidase II) and, in Gram-negative bacteria, further N-acylation (Lnt
step). These lipid modifications anchor the lipoprotein to the membrane,
either the inner or outer membrane depending on the presence of outer
membrane retention signals. Bacterial lipoproteins serve diverse functions
including structural roles (e.g., Braun's lipoprotein Lpp, which connects
the outer membrane to the peptidoglycan), substrate-binding components of
ABC transporters, and components of the bacterial cell envelope
biogenesis machinery. The Lol (Localization of lipoproteins) pathway
components such as LoLA, LoLB, LoLC, and LoLE mediate the sorting of
lipoproteins to their correct membrane destination.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Braun Lipoprotein (Lpp) — The most abundant protein in E. coli, Lpp covalently links the outer membrane to the peptidoglycan layer, providing structural integrity to the cell envelope.
- LoLA (Outer Membrane Lipoprotein Chaperone) — A periplasmic chaperone that binds to outer membrane-directed lipoproteins and delivers them to the LoLB-LoLC-E complex for insertion into the outer membrane.
