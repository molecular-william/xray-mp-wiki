# PIB-4-Type ATPase Family

## Overview
PIB-4-ATPases are a subclass of heavy-metal transporting P-type (P1B) ATPases with the broadest cargo scope, capable of transporting zinc, cobalt, cadmium, and other transition metals. They are among the simplest and shortest proteins within the entire P-type ATPase superfamily. They are found in plants, archaea, and prokaryotes, and have been assigned roles as virulence factors in pathogens (e.g., MtCtpD in Mycobacterium tuberculosis). Unlike other P1B subclasses, PIB-4-ATPases typically lack classical heavy-metal-binding domains (HMBDs) and possess eight transmembrane helices (MA, MB, M1-M6). The first high-resolution structures of a PIB-4-ATPase (sCoaT from Sulfitobacter sp. NAS14-1) revealed the architecture, a unique internal counterion mechanism via a conserved histidine (H657), and potassium-independent turnover. 

## Mechanism/Details
The PIB-4 subclass is defined by conserved sequence motifs distinct from other P1B subclasses. They contain an SPC motif in M4 (starting from S325 in sCoaT) and an HEGxT motif in M6 (starting from H657 in sCoaT) that form the high-affinity metal-binding site. The release pathway is lined by M2, M4, M5, and M6, with E120 (M2) serving as a transient metal ligand during ion release. A conserved histidine (H657 in sCoaT) in M6 acts as a built-in counterion, shifting to a sandwiched position between the SPC motif residues after ion release to prevent back-transfer. The A-/P-domain interaction in PIB-4-ATPases is maintained by a direct salt bridge between an arginine and aspartate (R273/D601 in sCoaT) rather than through a K+ ion, making their turnover potassium-independent. PIB-4-ATPases serve as virulence factors in pathogens and are absent in humans, making them attractive targets for novel antibiotics. Inhibitor screening has identified compounds that block both PIB-2 and PIB-4 ATPase activity with antimycobacterial effects.
