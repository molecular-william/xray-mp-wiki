# Cry Toxin Family

## Overview
The Cry (Crystal) toxin family comprises insecticidal pore-forming toxins produced by the bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) during sporulation. Cry toxins are widely used as biological pesticides in agriculture and for control of disease vectors. The canonical Cry toxins (e.g., Cry1Aa, Cry3Aa, Cry4Aa) adopt a three-domain architecture: Domain I (N-terminal alpha-helical bundle) is involved in pore formation, Domain II (beta-prism) mediates receptor recognition, and Domain III (beta-sandwich) contributes to receptor binding and stability. A second structural class within the Cry family was discovered with the Cry6Aa structure (PDB 5KUC), which adopts the alpha-helical pore-forming toxin fold (pfam05791) distinct from the three-domain architecture. Cry toxins act through a multi-step mechanism: solubilization in the insect midgut, proteolytic activation by gut proteases, binding to specific receptors (e.g., cadherin, alkaline phosphatase, aminopeptidase N), oligomerization, and membrane insertion to form lytic pores that cause osmotic cell death. The Cry toxin family is divided into multiple classes (Cry1-Cry78) based on sequence homology.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Cry6Aa (B. thuringiensis Pore-Forming Toxin) — Cry6Aa (PDB 5KUC, 5KUD) represents a second structural class of Cry toxins. It adopts the alpha-helical bundle fold (pfam05791) shared with ClyA/HlyE and NheA, rather than the three-domain architecture.
- Cry1Aa — The prototypical three-domain Cry toxin. Domain I (residues 1-290) forms a seven-helix bundle, Domain II (291-500) is a beta-prism, and Domain III (501-609) is a beta-sandwich.
