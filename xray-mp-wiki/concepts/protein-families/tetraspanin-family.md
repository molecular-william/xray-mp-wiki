# Tetraspanin Family

## Overview
Tetraspanins are a large family of four-pass transmembrane proteins (TM4SF)
found in all multicellular eukaryotes. In humans, 33 tetraspanin members have
been identified. Tetraspanins are characterized by a conserved architecture
of four transmembrane helices (TM1-TM4), a small extracellular loop (EC1), a
larger extracellular loop (EC2) containing a conserved CCG motif, and short
intracellular N- and C-termini. They function as molecular organizers in the
plasma membrane, forming tetraspanin-enriched microdomains (TEMs) that
recruit and organize partner proteins including integrins, immunoglobulins,
and signaling receptors.


## Mechanism/Details
Tetraspanins adopt a unique transmembrane fold where the four TM helices form
two largely separated antiparallel pairs (TM1/TM2 and TM3/TM4) that converge
near the cytoplasmic membrane interface through contacts between TM2 and TM3.
This creates an intramembrane cavity bounded by the four TM helices and the
bottom face of EC2. Many tetraspanins (e.g., CD81, CD53) bind cholesterol
within this intramembrane pocket, which stabilizes a closed conformation of
the protein. Cholesterol binding and release modulates the ability of
tetraspanins to engage partner proteins. The EC2 domain varies in size and
complexity across family members — some (e.g., CD81) contain additional C
and D helices, while others (e.g., CD53) lack these structural elements.

Key features of the tetraspanin family:
- Four transmembrane helices (TM1-TM4) with conserved topology
- Small extracellular loop (EC1) and large extracellular loop (EC2)
- Conserved CCG motif in EC2 (two or more cysteine residues forming disulfide bridges)
- Intramembrane cholesterol-binding pocket
- Short intracellular N- and C-termini
- Palmitoylation sites in juxtamembrane regions
- Form tetraspanin-enriched microdomains (TEMs)
- Antiparallel dimerization in crystals may reflect biological organization
- Partner proteins include integrins, Ig-superfamily members, signaling receptors
- CD81, CD53, and other members show high sequence conservation in TM regions
