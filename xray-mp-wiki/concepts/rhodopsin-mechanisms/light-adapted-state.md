# Light-Adapted State of Microbial Rhodopsins

## Overview
The light-adapted (LA) state is the ground state of microbial rhodopsins established after prolonged illumination. In this state, the retinal chromophore exists exclusively as all-trans retinal, in contrast to the dark-adapted state which contains a mixture of all-trans and 13-cis isomers. The LA state is characterized by a highly ordered pentagonal H-bond network around the Schiff base that locks the chromophore into the all-trans configuration and raises the activation energy for thermal isomerization. Light absorption is required to convert between all-trans and 13-cis isomers in the LA state, as the thermal barrier (21.5 kcal/mol) is too high for spontaneous isomerization at room temperature.

## Mechanism/Details
In the light-adapted state, the Schiff base nitrogen atom shifts by approximately 0.5 A compared to the dark-adapted state, which destabilizes the pentagonal H-bond network involving Wat402, Thr99, and Asp95. This shift causes Wat402 to become disordered (only partial electron density observed) and allows Asp95 to adopt two conformations with different rotameric states. The destabilized water network increases the activation energy for retinal isomerization to 21.5 kcal/mol, compared to 17.1 kcal/mol in the dark-adapted state. The energy difference between cis and trans retinal is approximately ten times larger in the LA state (Delta G = 10.9 kcal/mol favoring all-trans) than in the DA state (Delta G = -1.9 kcal/mol favoring 13-cis), making the 13-cis isomer essentially undetectable. This thermodynamic locking ensures that photon absorption is required to initiate the photocycle.
