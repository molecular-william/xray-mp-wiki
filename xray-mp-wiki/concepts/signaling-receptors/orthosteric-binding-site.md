# Orthosteric Binding Site

## Overview
The orthosteric binding site is the primary, endogenous ligand-binding site
on a receptor. For G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), the orthosteric site
is the pocket where the native ligand (e.g., epinephrine for the beta-2
adrenergic receptor, retinal for rhodopsin) binds to activate or inhibit
receptor signaling. It is distinct from allosteric sites, which are
topographically separate and modulate receptor function through a different
mechanism. The orthosteric pocket is typically the most conserved and
functionally critical region of the receptor's ligand-binding domain.


## Mechanism/Details
Structural characterization of orthosteric binding sites has been critical
for rational drug design. Co-crystal structures of GPCRs with orthosteric
ligands reveal the specific interactions (hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic
contacts, pi-pi stacking) that determine binding affinity and selectivity.
Comparison of agonist-bound and antagonist-bound receptor structures has
revealed that the orthosteric site undergoes conformational changes upon
activation, including a characteristic movement of TM6.

For transporters, occupation of the orthosteric site by substrate triggers
the conformational switches required for alternating access transport.
