# Polar Network in GPCR Activation

## Overview
The polar network is an extensive system of hydrogen bonds and water-mediated interactions that connects the orthosteric ligand-binding pocket to the G-protein-coupling interface in class A G-protein-coupled receptors. This network must rearrange upon receptor activation and consists of multiple low-energy molecular switches that break and reform in a concerted manner during the transition from inactive to active conformation. The polar network includes water molecules, conserved residues, and backbone carbonyls spanning from the transmembrane core to the cytoplasmic surface.


## Mechanism/Details
The high-resolution (2.1 A) active muOR structure reveals more ordered water molecules within the polar network than previously observed in other active-state GPCR structures. The inactive structure of the highly homologous delta-OR at 1.8 A resolution provides a comparison, highlighting many hydrogen bonds that stabilize both inactive and active states. The sodium-binding site (coordinated by D2.50, N3.35, S3.39, and W6.48 through a water molecule) is part of this network and collapses upon activation, precluding sodium binding. At the cytoplasmic surface, the network ends at the NPxxY motif in TM7, where N7.49 and Y7.53 participate in a hydrogen-bond network involving Y5.58 in TM5 and backbone carbonyls of L3.43 in TM3 and V6.40 in TM6. The muOR and beta2AR have more extensive polar networks stabilizing the inactive state compared to rhodopsin, which relies more on the covalent inverse agonist [11-cis-Retinal](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/11-cis-retinal/) for stability.
