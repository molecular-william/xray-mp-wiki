# Extracellular Domain-Mediated Allostery in GPCRs

## Overview
Extracellular domain-mediated allostery refers to the mechanism by which large extracellular domains of GPCRs, such as cysteine-rich domains (CRDs), regulate signalling by the heptahelical transmembrane domain (TMD). In Smoothened (SMO), the CRD is stacked atop the TMD via an intervening wedge-like linker domain, and the interface between these domains stabilizes the inactive state. Ligand binding to the CRD or TMD induces conformational changes that are propagated across the domain interface via extracellular loop 3 (ECL3).

## Mechanism/Details
In the human SMO structure, the CRD is positioned above the TMD and connected via a linker domain that forms a wedge-like structure. The interface between the CRD, linker domain, and TMD buries 745 Å² of surface area and primarily involves hydrophobic interactions. A disulfide bridge (Cys78-Cys213) links the CRD base and the start of a connector segment that links the CRD and linker domain. Cholesterol bound at the CRD site sits at this three-domain interface, stabilizing the inactive conformation. Mutations that destabilize CRD-linker domain contacts (Pro120Ser), alter the CRD surface near the interface (Ile160Asn/Glu162Thr), or disrupt a disulfide bond within the linker domain (Cys197Ser/Cys217Ser) all increase constitutive SMO activity, mimicking complete CRD deletion. Binding of the antagonist vismodegib to the TMD induces a shift of ECL3 and a pivoting of the CRD on the extracellular end of the TMD bundle, leading to rearrangement of the sterol-binding site and cholesterol displacement from the interface. SAXS experiments show that the CRD ligand 20(S)-OHC induces an increase in maximum particle size from 120 Å to 129 Å, suggesting domain rearrangements upon activation. This provides a structural mechanism for how extracellular signals (via the CRD) can be transduced across the membrane (via the TMD) through a series of conformational changes at the domain interface.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Human Smoothened (SMO) — Extracellular Domain Regulation — CRD-linker domain-TMD interface stabilizes SMO inactive state. Mutations disrupting this interface cause constitutive activity. Vismodegib binding to TMD propagates conformational change to CRD via ECL3.
