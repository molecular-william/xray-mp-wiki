# Phototaxis Signaling in Microbial Rhodopsins

## Overview
Phototaxis signaling in microbial rhodopsins is a process where light-activated photoreceptors transmit conformational changes to membrane-bound transducer proteins, which in turn modulate cytoplasmic signaling cascades controlling cell motility. Sensory rhodopsin II (SRII) is the prototypical phototaxis receptor, transmitting signals to the HtrII transducer. The BR_A215T mutant demonstrated that a single residue substitution can convert a light-driven proton pump into a functional phototaxis sensor.

## Mechanism/Details
Light absorption by the retinal chromophore triggers all-trans to 13-cis photoisomerization, which creates a steric conflict between the introduced threonine (Thr204 in SRII, Thr215 in BR_A215T) and the retinal C14 group. This steric clash propagates through the protein via the threonine-tyrosine hydrogen-bonded pair to the transducer-interacting surface. In SRII, signal transmission from the receptor to the HtrII transducer modulates a cytoplasmic histidine kinase that controls the flagellar motor switch, enabling the cell to avoid blue light. The BR_A215T mutant achieves this signaling function without the Arg72 reorientation found in SRII, indicating that the steric trigger is the essential signaling event while Arg72 is primarily for spectral tuning.
