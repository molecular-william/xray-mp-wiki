# SARS-CoV (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus)

## Overview
SARS-CoV is the betacoronavirus responsible for the 2002-2004 SARS outbreak.
It emerged from horseshoe bats (Rhinolophus spp.) and was transmitted to
humans via intermediate hosts (likely civet cats). SARS-CoV enters host cells
through the angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) receptor, mediated by its
spike (S) glycoprotein. The virus caused approximately 8,000 infections with
a ~10% case fatality rate before being contained through public health
measures.


## Mechanism/Details
The spike protein of SARS-CoV was the first coronavirus spike to be
structurally characterized. Key structures include the RBD bound to ACE2
(PDB 2AJF), the post-fusion core of S2 (PDB 1WYY), and the full-length
S trimer in the pre-fusion conformation. The S2 subunit contains the
heptad repeat regions HR1 and HR2 that form a six-helix bundle in the
post-fusion state.

SARS-CoV accessory proteins such as ORF3a, ORF6, ORF7a, and ORF8 play
roles in immune evasion and pathogenesis. The envelope (E) protein forms
an ion channel (viroporin) that contributes to inflammasome activation
and pathogenesis. Structural studies of SARS-CoV proteins have provided
a foundation for understanding coronavirus biology that proved critical
during the COVID-19 pandemic.
