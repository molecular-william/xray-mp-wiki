# Hydrophobic Tunnel for Sterol Access in Smoothened

## Overview

The hydrophobic tunnel is a conduit between transmembrane helices TM5 and TM6 of Smoothened (SMO) that connects the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane to the deep 7TM sterol-binding pocket. First observed in the active mouse SMO structure (PDB 6O3C), this tunnel allows membrane sterols (primarily cholesterol) to reach the 7TM sterol site without the energetically prohibitive step of membrane desolvation, providing a direct structural link between the cholesterol transporter-like activity of PTCH1 and SMO regulation.

## Mechanism/Details

In the active conformation of SMO, a channel opens between TM5 and TM6 that leads from the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane into the deep 7TM sterol-binding pocket. The tunnel is lined by hydrophobic residues, providing a compatible environment for sterol transit through the membrane interior. The position of the cholesterol hydroxyl group in active SMO suggests that access from the inner leaflet would require a flip in sterol orientation en route to the 7TM site. The tunnel entrance in active mSMO is almost identical to a tunnel observed in cyclopamine-bound Xenopus SMO (PDB 6D32). Mutations designed to occlude the tunnel entrance — particularly A463F — rendered SMO unresponsive to stimulation by Sonic hedgehog (ShhNp), while G420F partially reduced the response. The structurally observed hydrophobic tunnel provides a potential mechanism for the specific action of inner-leaflet sterols on the 7TM sterol-binding site. This mode of agonist access from the membrane into the 7TM bundle has precedent in other lipid-activated GPCRs.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work

- Mouse Smoothened (SMO) — Hydrophobic tunnel between TM5 and TM6 connecting inner leaflet to 7TM sterol site. Identical tunnel observed in Xenopus SMO (PDB 6D32). A463F mutation at tunnel entrance blocks ShhNp activation.
