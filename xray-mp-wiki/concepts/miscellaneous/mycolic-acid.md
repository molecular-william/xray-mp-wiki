# Mycolic Acid

## Overview
Mycolic acids are long-chain (C60-C90) alpha-alkyl, beta-hydroxy fatty acids that are major components of the cell envelope of *Mycobacterium* species and related actinobacteria. These waxy lipids form a dense, hydrophobic permeability barrier that contributes to intrinsic antibiotic resistance and virulence in *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. Mycolic acids are attached to the cell wall arabinogalactan-peptidoglycan polymer and form the inner leaflet of the outer membrane-like mycomembrane.


## Mechanism/Details
Mycolic acids are synthesized by the type II fatty acid synthase system (FAS-II) and exist in several functional forms: alpha-mycolates (the most abundant), methoxy-mycolates, and keto-mycolates. The MmpL3 transporter has been identified as the essential flippase that translocates mycolic acids across the inner membrane for transport to the cell wall. The extraordinary length and hydrophobicity of mycolic acids create a highly impermeable barrier that reduces efficacy of many antibiotics, making mycolic acid biosynthesis and transport pathways critical targets for antitubercular drug development.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- MmpL3 — Essential mycolic acid flippase in M. tuberculosis; target of multiple anti-TB drug candidates including SQ109
- InhA — Enoyl-ACP reductase in the FAS-II mycolic acid biosynthesis pathway; target of the frontline TB drug isoniazid
