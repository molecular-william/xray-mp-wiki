# Alpha-Dystroglycanopathy

## Overview
Alpha-dystroglycanopathy is a class of congenital muscular dystrophies caused by defects in the O-glycosylation of alpha-dystroglycan, a key component of the dystrophin-glycoprotein complex (DGC) that links the extracellular matrix to the actin cytoskeleton. These disorders are characterized by reduced glycosylation of alpha-dystroglycan, leading to impaired binding to extracellular matrix proteins such as laminin, agrin, and neurexin.


## Mechanism/Details
Alpha-dystroglycan is heavily O-mannosylated, with a specific O-mannosyl glycan structure (GalNAc-beta3-GlcNAc-beta4-Man) required for binding to extracellular matrix proteins containing laminin G (LG) domains. Mutations in genes encoding enzymes involved in the synthesis of this glycan — including POMT1, POMT2, POMGNT1, FKTN, FKRP, LARGE, and ISPD — disrupt the functional glycosylation of alpha-dystroglycan. The severity of alpha-dystroglycanopathies ranges from the severe Walker-Warburg syndrome (WWS) and muscle-eye-brain disease (MEB) to milder forms such as limb-girdle muscular dystrophy type 2I (LGMD2I). The common molecular feature is reduced ligand-binding activity of alpha-dystroglycan due to defective glycosylation.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Alpha-Dystroglycan — Core protein whose abnormal glycosylation defines the disease class; functionally links the extracellular matrix to the cytoskeleton via the DGC
- POMT1/POMT2 — Protein O-mannosyltransferase complex that initiates O-mannosyl glycosylation; mutations cause Walker-Warburg syndrome
