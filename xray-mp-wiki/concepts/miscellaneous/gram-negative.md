# Gram-Negative Bacteria

## Overview
Gram-negative bacteria are a major class of bacteria characterized by a cell envelope consisting of two distinct lipid bilayers: an inner (cytoplasmic) membrane and an outer membrane, with a peptidoglycan layer residing in the intervening periplasmic space. The outer membrane is asymmetric, with phospholipids in the inner leaflet and lipopolysaccharides (LPS) in the outer leaflet, and contains pore-forming proteins called porins that allow passage of small hydrophilic nutrients. The presence of the outer membrane makes Gram-negative bacteria intrinsically resistant to many antibiotics that target intracellular processes, as the outer membrane acts as an effective permeability barrier. Well-studied Gram-negative species include Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Salmonella enterica, and Neisseria meningitidis. In structural biology of membrane proteins, the Gram-negative envelope presents unique challenges for protein expression, purification, and reconstitution, and is the source of many extensively characterized outer membrane proteins (OMPs) and inner membrane transporters.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- OmpF Porin — A trimeric outer membrane porin from E. coli that forms non-specific diffusion channels for small hydrophilic molecules (<600 Da).
