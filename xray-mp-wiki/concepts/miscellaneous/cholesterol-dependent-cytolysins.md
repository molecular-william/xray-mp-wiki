# Cholesterol-Dependent Cytolysins

## Overview
Cholesterol-dependent cytolysins (CDCs) are a family of bacterial pore-forming toxins secreted in water-soluble monomeric form that bind to cholesterol-rich membranes and oligomerize into large ring-shaped pores. CDCs comprise the largest family of cytolytic bacterial proteins, with 40-70% sequence identity among family members. They share a conserved tertiary structure with four domains (D1-D4) and a common mechanism of action: monomer binding to [Cholesterol](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/cholesterol/), oligomerization into rings of up to 50 monomers (approximately 300 A diameter), and a dramatic conformational change where two helix bundles in each monomer convert into amphiphilic transmembrane beta-hairpins that insert into the membrane to form a beta-barrel pore. Key family members include [Listeriolysin O](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/toxins/listeriolysin-o/) (LLO) from *Listeria monocytogenes*, streptolysin O (SLO) from *Streptococcus pyogenes*, and perfringolysin O (PFO) from *Clostridium perfringens*.


## Mechanism/Details
CDC monomers contain four structurally distinct domains: D1-D3 form an intertwined core with a central beta-sheet and two membrane-inserting helix bundles (HB1/HB2), while D4 is a separate beta-sandwich folding unit involved in [Cholesterol](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/cholesterol/) recognition and membrane binding. The undecapeptide motif near the C-terminus of D4 is conserved among CDCs and participates in intermolecular interactions during oligomerization. Pore formation proceeds through these steps: (1) soluble monomer binds to membrane [Cholesterol](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/cholesterol/); (2) monomers oligomerize into a prepore ring; (3) a conformational change converts HB1 and HB2 helices into beta-hairpins that insert into the membrane; (4) the beta-barrel pore is fully formed, allowing ion flux and membrane disruption. The LLO crystal structure revealed that seven ion-coordination clusters involving Na+ ions and water molecules stabilize the pre-pore conformation, acting as a pH sensor that regulates activity. LLO is unique among CDCs for its pH-dependent inactivation at neutral pH, which restricts its activity to the acidic phagosome.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [Listeriolysin O](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/toxins/listeriolysin-o/) (LLO) from *Listeria monocytogenes* — pH- and temperature-dependent CDC; crystal structure at 2.15 A (PDB 4CDB)
- Streptolysin O (SLO) from *Streptococcus pyogenes* — classic CDC used as a model; D4 domain can bind membranes and inhibit full-length SLO
- Perfringolysin O (PLO) from *Clostridium perfringens* — CDC whose D4 domain amplifies haemolytic activity when added separately
- Aerolysin from *Aeromonas hydrophila* — CDC-related toxin with similar pore-forming mechanism
