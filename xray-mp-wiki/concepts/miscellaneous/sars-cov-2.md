# SARS-CoV-2 (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2)

## Overview
SARS-CoV-2 is the betacoronavirus responsible for the COVID-19 pandemic,
first identified in Wuhan, China in December 2019. It is an enveloped,
positive-sense single-stranded RNA virus with a genome of approximately 30 kb.
The virus enters host cells via its spike (S) glycoprotein, which binds to the
angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) receptor. SARS-CoV-2 belongs to the
Coronaviridae family and shares approximately 79% genome sequence identity
with SARS-CoV.


## Mechanism/Details
The SARS-CoV-2 spike protein has been extensively characterized by cryo-EM
and X-ray crystallography. The RBD adopts two main conformations: "up"
(ACE2-accessible) and "down" (inaccessible). The furin cleavage site at the
S1/S2 boundary (PRRAR) distinguishes SARS-CoV-2 from SARS-CoV and contributes
to its enhanced transmissibility. Multiple neutralizing antibody epitopes have
been mapped on the spike, including the RBD and the N-terminal domain (NTD).

The main protease (Mpro, 3CLpro) and papain-like protease (PLpro) process the
viral polyprotein. Structural studies of Mpro have guided the development of
antiviral inhibitors such as nirmatrelvir (PF-07321332). The SARS-CoV-2
envelope protein (E) forms a cation-selective ion channel (viroporin) that is
important for viral assembly and pathogenesis, and the membrane protein (M)
is the most abundant structural protein, shaping the virion envelope.
