# Axillary Malodour Production Pathway

## Overview
Axillary (underarm) malodour is produced by specific skin bacteria metabolizing odourless precursor molecules secreted in apocrine sweat. The major malodour component 3-methyl-3-sulfanylhexan-1-ol (3M3SH) is produced when Staphylococcus hominis and related staphylococci take up the dipeptide-conjugated precursor S-Cys-Gly-3M3SH via the POT family transporter PepT_Sh (SH1446). Once inside the cell, a dipeptidase removes [Glycine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/glycine/), and a C-S beta-lyase (such as TnaA) cleaves the carbon-sulphur bond to release the volatile thioalcohol, along with pyruvate and ammonia for bacterial nutrition.


## Mechanism/Details
Step 1: The glutathione-conjugate SG-3M3SH is transported by ABCC11 into apocrine secretory vesicles, where GGT1 cleaves the terminal glutamyl residue to produce S-Cys-Gly-3M3SH. Step 2: S-Cys-Gly-3M3SH is secreted onto the skin surface via apocrine secretion. Step 3: PepT_Sh (SH1446) actively transports S-Cys-Gly-3M3SH into S. hominis, coupled to the inwardly directed proton gradient. Step 4: Intracellular dipeptidase cleaves [Glycine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/glycine/) from S-Cys-Gly-3M3SH. Step 5: C-S beta-lyase (TnaA or equivalent) cleaves the Cys-3M3SH bond, releasing 3M3SH, pyruvate, and ammonia. Step 6: The volatile thioalcohol 3M3SH diffuses or is exported out of the cell, producing the characteristic axillary odour.
