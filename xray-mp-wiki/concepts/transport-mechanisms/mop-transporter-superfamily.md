# MOP Transporter Superfamily

## Overview
The multidrug/oligosaccharidyl-lipid/polysaccharide (MOP) exporter superfamily is a group of transporters that includes the multidrug and toxic compound extrusion (MATE) family, the mouse virulence factor (MVF) family, and the eukaryotic oligosaccharidyl-lipid flippase (OLF) family. MOP transporters share a core architecture of 12 transmembrane helices arranged in two lobes (N-lobe and C-lobe) and function via an alternating-access mechanism. Members transport diverse substrates including drugs, lipid-linked oligosaccharides, and peptidoglycan precursors across cellular membranes.


## Mechanism/Details
MOP transporters are believed to utilize a shared rocker-switch alternating-access mechanism. The N-lobe and C-lobe rotate about a hinge region (typically on TM7) to alternate between inward-facing and outward-facing conformations. This was first demonstrated structurally in MATE transporters and later supported by the inward-facing structure of MurJ (a MVF family member), which showed a bent TM7 (at Ser/Thr residues) acting as the pivot point. The MurJ structure revealed that the rocker-switch motion translocates large substrates such as lipid II (~1900 Da) across the membrane, with the undecaprenyl tail remaining associated with a hydrophobic groove during the transition. This mechanism is distinct from the PglK ABC transporter, which flips lipid-linked oligosaccharides using only an outward-facing state.
