# Bcs1 Folded Protein Translocation Mechanism

## Overview
Bcs1 is a mitochondrial AAA+ ATPase that translocates folded proteins across the mitochondrial inner membrane, primarily the Rieske iron-sulfur protein (ISP) for Complex III assembly. Unlike canonical AAA unfoldases that thread unfolded polypeptides through a narrow central pore, Bcs1 forms a homo-heptamer that creates a large substrate-binding cavity (~35 A diameter, ~28,000 A^3 volume) sufficient to accommodate folded protein domains. Cryo-EM structures of mouse Bcs1 in apo, ATPgS-bound, and ADP-bound states (Tang et al., 2020) reveal a nucleotide-dependent conformational cycle. In the apo/ADP-bound state, the cavity is open and accessible to the mitochondrial matrix. ATP binding drives a concerted contraction of the AAA domains via inter-subunit sliding movements, pushing the substrate across the membrane. Bcs1 lacks the conserved pore-loop and sensor-II motifs characteristic of threading AAA proteins, supporting a fundamentally different translocation mechanism. This mechanism may represent a paradigm shift in understanding how large, folded protein domains are transported across biological membranes.

## Mechanism/Details
Bcs1 translocates the ~15 kDa extrinsic domain of the Rieske iron-sulfur protein (ISP-ED) in its folded form across the mitochondrial inner membrane. The mechanism differs from the classical hand-over-hand threading used by hexameric AAA unfoldases. (1) Substrate capture: In the apo/ADP-bound state, Bcs1 adopts an open heptameric conformation with a large cavity (~35 A diameter, ~28,000 A^3 volume) accessible to the mitochondrial matrix. The cavity is negatively charged, complementary to the positively charged ISP. The narrowest constriction is formed by four hydrophobic residues (M121, M123, V124, L128) from the Bcs1-specific domain. (2) ATP binding and cavity contraction: ATP binding to the AAA domains induces a concerted conformational change. The AAA domains undergo a sliding movement where the Arg-finger residue (R343) moves ~19 A from the nucleotide-binding site in the apo state to interact with the gamma-phosphate in the ATP-bound state. This contraction reduces the cavity volume, pushing the substrate toward the intermembrane space. (3) Substrate release: ATP hydrolysis resets the system, releasing the ISP into the pre-complex of Complex III. The TM domain reorganizes during this cycle — ordered in the apo state and disordered in the ATP-bound state — suggesting the TM helices open to release the substrate laterally into the membrane.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Mouse Bcs1 (mBcs1) — Cryo-EM structures of mBcs1 in apo (3.81 A, PDB 6UKP), ATPgS-bound (3.2 A, PDB 6UKS), and ADP-bound (4.4 A, PDB 6UKO) states. X-ray structures of DdN mBcs1 AAA domain (2.2 A, PDB 6U1Y). These structures reveal the heptameric architecture and nucleotide-dependent conformational changes that drive folded protein translocation.
- Human BCS1L — Human ortholog sharing 94% sequence identity with mBcs1. Mutations in BCS1L cause GRACILE syndrome (severe: growth restriction, aminoaciduria, cholestasis, iron overload, lactacidosis, early death) and Bjoernstad syndrome (milder: pili torti, sensorineural hearing loss). Disease mutations map to the Bcs1-specific domain, AAA domain, and their interfaces.
