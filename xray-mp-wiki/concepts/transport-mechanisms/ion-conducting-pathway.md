# Ion-Conducting Pathway

## Overview
The ion-conducting pathway is the structural route through which ions cross
the membrane via ion channels. It comprises the pore, the selectivity filter,
and the gates that control ion access. The pathway is formed by the
arrangement of transmembrane helices (typically 4-6) creating a water-filled
pore through which ions pass at near diffusion-limited rates. The architecture
of the ion-conducting pathway determines the fundamental properties of the
channel: ion selectivity, conductance, and gating mechanisms.


## Mechanism/Details
The ion-conducting pathway has been structurally characterized for
potassium (KcsA, Kv1.2, MthK), sodium (NavAb, NavMs, NavPaS), calcium
(CaVAb), chloride (CLC, bestrophin, CFTR), and TRP channels. Common
features include: (1) an inverted teepee architecture of pore-lining
helices, (2) a selectivity filter at the extracellular end of the pore,
(3) a water-filled cavity in the membrane center, and (4) gates at one
or both ends of the pathway.
