# Substrate-Induced Transition Fit in 5+5 Inverted Repeat Transporters

## Overview
The substrate-induced transition fit mechanism describes how secondary transporters of the 5+5 inverted repeat fold (including APC superfamily, NSS family, and Mhp1/BetP families) couple substrate binding to conformational changes during the transport cycle. According to this mechanism, initial recognition between the substrate and a non-optimal binding site in the ground state (open-to-out or open-to-in apo conformation) triggers a transition to a transient occluded state where the binding site is reorganized to attain optimal complementarity. In the arginine/agmatine antiporter AdiC, proper coordination of the Arg+ guanidinium group with residues Asn101 (TM3) and Trp293 (TM8) via a pi-cation interaction and hydrogen bonding is essential to sample the semioccluded state and transit to the occluded state. The N101A mutation delocalizes the guanidinium group, dramatically reducing the translocation rate (V_MAX ~1% of wild-type) while minimally affecting substrate affinity (K_M ~100 uM vs ~40 uM wild-type). This mechanism parallels the induced fit concept in enzymes and provides a molecular basis for the alternate access model of transporter function.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [AdiC Arginine/Agmatine Antiporter](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/slc-transporters/adic/) — The AdiC-N101A mutant structure at 3.0 A resolution captures an open-to-out Arg+-bound intermediate state. The absence of the Arg+-Asn101 hydrogen bond leads to delocalization of the guanidinium group, which travels freely between Trp293 (TM8) and Trp202 (TM6a). This prevents transition to the occluded state, resulting in a dramatic decrease in turnover rate. The N101D mutation rescues transport activity by restoring hydrogen bonding capacity at position 101.
