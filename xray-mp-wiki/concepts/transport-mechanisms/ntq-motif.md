# NTQ Motif in Chloride Pumping Rhodopsins

## Overview
The NTQ motif (Asn98-Thr102-Gln109) is a conserved amino acid sequence
motif found in chloride-pumping rhodopsins from marine bacteria, including
NmHR/CIR from Nonlabens marinus. It defines a distinct subfamily of
microbial rhodopsins that pump chloride ions inward, in contrast to the
TSA motif of archaeal halorhodopsins. The NTQ motif plays multiple roles
in chloride binding, transport, steric gating, and release throughout the
photocycle of these light-driven chloride pumps.

## Mechanism/Details
The NTQ motif residues have distinct and dynamic functions during the
chloride transport photocycle:

**Asn98 (Steric Gate):** In the resting state, Asn98 coordinates the
chloride-binding site near the protonated Schiff base (PSB). At 20 us
after photoactivation, after the chloride passes over the retinal, Asn98
moves into the chloride-free Cl351 site, acting as a sterically closing
molecular gate that prevents reverse flow of chloride. The side chain
interacts with water molecules Wat484 and Wat407, and is further stabilized
by a new hydrogen bond between Thr102 and the Asn98 backbone carbonyl.
Mutations of Asn98 eliminate or substantially reduce chloride-pumping
activity.

**Thr102 (Multi-role Coordinator):** Thr102 plays three distinct roles
during the transport cycle: (1) In the resting state, its side chain
hydroxyl coordinates the Cl351 chloride at the PSB. (2) At 1 us after
activation, Thr102 undergoes a rotameric change (80 degree rotation) to
assist in transferring the chloride to the transient Cl352 position,
stabilizing the anion-&#x3C0; interaction with the retinal. (3) By 20 us,
Thr102 seals the steric molecular gate by forming a hydrogen bond with the
Asn98 backbone carbonyl. The equivalent residue in the sodium-pumping
rhodopsin KR2 is Asp116, which provides an electrostatic driving force for
the cation, demonstrating substantial evolutionary adaptation of rhodopsins
to different substrates.

**Gln109 (Release Pathway):** Gln109 is positioned at a strategic location
in the chloride release pathway. At 20 us, the Cl353-binding site forms
in a cavity accessed through water molecules Wat401 and Wat402 and is
coordinated by Gln109, Ser54, and Thr243. The equivalent residue in
bacteriorhodopsin is Asp96 (the internal proton donor to the retinal Schiff
base), and in KR2 is Gln123 (involved in sodium transport). This suggests
that pumping rhodopsins share sections of the transport pathway despite
different substrate charge and size.

The NTQ motif is encoded by the NTQ rhodopsin family signature, where
these three residues are found on transmembrane helices B (Asn98) and C
(Thr102, Gln109). The motif is conserved among bacterial chloride-pumping
rhodopsins and distinguishes them from archaeal halorhodopsins (which
use Thr-Ser-Ala/TSA motif) and proton-pumping bacteriorhodopsins.
