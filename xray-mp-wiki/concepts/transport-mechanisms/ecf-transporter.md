# ECF Transporter (Energy-Coupling Factor Transporter)

## Overview
Energy-coupling factor (ECF) transporters are a unique family of membrane transporters responsible for vitamin uptake in prokaryotes. They are distinct from ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporters despite a similar organizational structure. Each ECF transporter comprises four subunits: an S component (substrate-binding protein embedded in the membrane), a T component (transmembrane protein), and two ATP-binding proteins (A and A' components). The S component directly recognizes and binds substrate, unlike ABC transporters which use a separate periplasmic binding protein.


## Mechanism/Details
ECF transporters are organized as a four-subunit complex: the S component (substrate recognition), the T component (transmembrane anchor), and the A/A' components (ATP hydrolysis). The S component does not exhibit sequence homology with any protein of known structure. ECF transporters are classified into two groups: Group I transporters are encoded by linked genes encoding the S component and an S-specific A-T module; Group II transporters share a common A-T module among up to 12 different S components.
The S component of ECF transporters has six transmembrane segments, adopts a previously unreported transporter fold, and binds substrate on the periplasmic side. The transport mechanism involves a gate formed by the L1 loop and conformational changes driven by ATP hydrolysis in the A/A' components. The T-A-A' module provides the energy for substrate transport, with the S component undergoing conformational changes that alternate between outward-open and inward-open states.
The first complete quaternary ECF transporter structure was solved for the [HMP ECF Transporter from Lactobacillus brevis](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/hmp-ecf-transporter-lactobacillus-brevis/) at 3.5 Å resolution (doi/10.1038##nature12045), revealing a horseshoe-shaped EcfT architecture and a horizontal orientation of EcfS within the membrane. The structure shows that all interactions between EcfS and EcfT are hydrophobic (van der Waals), enabling rigid-body rotation of EcfS during the transport cycle. A working model proposes that EcfS rotates between inward-open (resting) and outward-open (substrate-binding) states, driven by ATP hydrolysis in EcfA-EcfA' and transmitted via coupling helices in EcfT.
ECF transporters transport a variety of vitamins including [Riboflavin (Vitamin B2)](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/cofactors/riboflavin/) (vitamin B2), thiamine (vitamin B1), [Cobalamin (Vitamin B12)](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/cobalamin/), folate (vitamin B9), and other essential cofactors. The S components are substrate-specific, while the T-A-A' energy module can be shared among multiple S components in Group II transporters.
Dynamic S-component exchange is a key feature of Group II ECF transporters. In vitro reconstitution of ECF-FoIT2 and [ECF-PanT (Energy-Coupling Factor Pantothenate Transporter)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/ecf-pant/) (both from L. delbrueckii) into proteoliposomes demonstrated that S-components dynamically associate with and dissociate from the shared ECF module during the transport cycle. Competition experiments showed that substrate-bound S-components compete more efficiently for association with the ECF module than apo-proteins. The toppling of the S-component upon association with the ECF module is an example of the elevator-type transport mechanism, where substrate is moved across the membrane by conformational change of the binding protein.
The crystal structure of the [Folate ECF Transporter from Lactobacillus brevis](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/folate-ecf-transporter-lactobacillus-brevis/) (at 3.0 A resolution) captured the complete four-subunit complex (EcfA, EcfA', EcfT, FoIT) in an inward-facing conformation. The EcfA-EcfA' ATPase heterodimer adopts a semi-open conformation with conserved ABC signature motifs (Walker A/B, Q-loop, LSGGQ motif, D-loop, H-switch). The FoIT S-protein binds folate and delivers it to the EcfT transmembrane channel, with ATP binding to the A/A' dimer driving the transition to the outward-facing state.
