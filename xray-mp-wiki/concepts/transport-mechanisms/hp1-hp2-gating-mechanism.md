# HP1-HP2 Gating Mechanism in Glutamate Transporters

## Overview
The HP1-HP2 gating mechanism describes how glutamate transporters control alternating access to the substrate-binding site using two helical hairpins (HP1 and HP2). The first crystal structure of a glutamate transporter homologue (GltPh from Pyrococcus horikoshii) at 3.5 Å resolution revealed a bowl-shaped homotrimer with an aqueous extracellular basin. At the bottom of the basin, three independent substrate-binding sites are each cradled by two helical hairpins (HP1 and HP2) that reach from opposite sides of the membrane, forming extracellular and intracellular gates respectively.

## Mechanism/Details
Glutamate transporters function through an alternating access mechanism mediated by two helical hairpins. (1) The transporter forms a bowl-shaped trimer with a deep aqueous basin extending halfway across the membrane bilayer. (2) At the bottom of each protomer's basin, HP2 (the extracellular gate) covers the substrate-binding site. In the open state, HP2 lifts to allow substrate access from the extracellular solution. (3) HP1 (the intracellular gate) sits below the binding site. When HP1 moves vertically towards the cytoplasm, it opens an aqueous pathway from the binding site to the intracellular side. (4) The tips of HP1 and HP2 meet at the substrate-binding site, approximately halfway across the membrane. A conserved serine-rich motif at the tip of HP1 tiles part of the basin bottom. (5) The movement of HP1 into a crevice on the protein surface, combined with HP2 movement toward the trimer center, ensures that the binding site is never simultaneously open to both sides. (6) Substrate and ion binding (Na+, H+, K+) regulate the relative movements of the two gates, with HP2 undergoing substrate-dependent conformational changes and HP1 moving vertically into the cytoplasm.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- GltPh (Glutamate Transporter Homologue from Pyrococcus horikoshii) — First structural description of the HP1-HP2 gating architecture at 3.5 Å resolution (PDB 2NWX). HP1 and HP2 are structurally related helix-turn-helix motifs (r.m.s.d. 2.4 Å) despite no significant sequence identity. Equivalent residues in GLT-1 (Ala364 and Ser440) form a disulfide bond that blocks transport, confirming the functional importance of HP1-HP2 apposition.
