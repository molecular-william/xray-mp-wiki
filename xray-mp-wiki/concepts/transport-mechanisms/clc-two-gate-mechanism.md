# CLC Two-Gate Transport Mechanism

## Overview
The CLC two-gate transport mechanism describes how CLC-type Cl-/H+ exchangers couple the movement of chloride ions and protons across the membrane using two sequential gates. Glu148 (Gluex) forms the external gate, and Tyr445 forms the internal gate. The two gates are coupled through movement of helix O, which transduces conformational changes to the ion pathway via a direct contact between Ile402 (at the C-terminus of helix O) and Tyr445. This dual-gate mechanism enables the alternating-access transport cycle while preventing simultaneous exposure of bound substrates to both sides of the membrane.

## Mechanism/Details
The CLC exchange cycle involves six states: (1) apo state with inner (Tyr445) and outer (Glu148) both closed; (2) inner gate opens, allowing Cl- binding from intracellular side; (3) Cl- binding causes Glu148 displacement and protonation from outside, triggering inner gate closure; (4) fully loaded outward-facing state with 2 Cl- and 1 H+ bound; (5) Cl- exit to extracellular solution and Glu148 reenters the pathway; (6) proton transferred from Glu148 to Glu203 and released intracellularly. Helix O moves as a rigid body during the cycle, and its movement is transduced to the Cl- pathway via Ile402-Tyr445 contact. Cross-linking helix O to helix Q (e.g., A399C A432C) inhibits transport by constraining this movement. The inner gate (Tyr445) is pH-independent, enabling tight coupling at acidic pHs. CLC channels may have evolved from transporters through mutations that disrupted the inner gate seal.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- CLC-ec1 (E. coli Cl-/H+ antiporter) — Prokaryotic model system where the two-gate mechanism was discovered. Crystal structure of A399C A432C cross-linked mutant at 3.5 A (PDB 4MQX). Glu148 = external gate, Tyr445 = internal gate. Double mutant E148A/Y445A shows channel-like behavior uncoupled from conformational cycle.
- cmCLC (Cyanidioschyzon merolae CLC) — Eukaryotic CLC transporter where Glu148 adopts three distinct conformations (down, middle/up), providing structural evidence for the gate movements.
