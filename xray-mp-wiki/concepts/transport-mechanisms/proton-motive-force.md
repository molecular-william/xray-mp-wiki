# Proton Motive Force

## Overview
The proton motive force (PMF) is the electrochemical gradient of protons (H+) across a biological membrane, composed of two components: the chemical gradient (Delta-pH, the difference in proton concentration) and the electrical potential (Delta-psi, the membrane potential). PMF is generated by respiratory electron transport chains or by light-driven proton pumps (e.g., bacteriorhodopsin) and is harnessed by ATP synthase to drive ATP synthesis (oxidative phosphorylation and photophosphorylation). PMF also drives secondary active transport, flagellar rotation, and other energy-requiring processes at the membrane. The magnitude of the PMF is typically 180-200 mV (inside negative and alkaline) in respiring bacteria and mitochondria, and is expressed in millivolts or as the proton electrochemical potential (Delta-mu-H+). In structural biology, understanding PMF is essential for interpreting the mechanisms of proton-coupled transporters, ion channels, and energy-transducing enzymes.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- ATP Synthase — The PMF drives rotation of the c-ring in F0 domain, which powers ATP synthesis in F1 domain via a mechanical coupling mechanism.
- Lactose Permease (LacY) — LacY uses the PMF to couple the energetically uphill import of lactose with the energetically downhill import of H+ (symport).
