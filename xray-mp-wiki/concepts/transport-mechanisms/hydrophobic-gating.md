# Hydrophobic Gating (Vapor Lock Mechanism)

## Overview
Hydrophobic gating, also known as the "vapor lock" mechanism, is a principle by which ion channels prevent ion permeation through hydrophobic constrictions in the pore. In this mechanism, narrow hydrophobic regions of the pore lumen exclude water molecules, preventing hydration of ions and thus blocking passage. Channel opening involves expanding these constrictions to allow wetting and ion conduction.


## Mechanism/Details
In the closed state of the MscS mechanosensitive channel, the side chains of L105 and L109 from seven TM3a helices project into the central pore lumen, creating two rings of bulky hydrophobic side chains. These hydrophobic residues, combined with the intrinsic hydrophobicity of the TM3a helix surface, prevent water from entering and filling the pore — a state termed "vapor lock." The narrow pore (4.8 Å diameter in MscS) is below the threshold required for spontaneous water filling.

Upon channel opening, the TM3a helices pivot around a conserved glycine hinge (G113 in MscS), moving outward like the plates of a camera iris. This expansion brings the pore diameter to ~13 Å (in the MscS A106V open structure), pulling L105 and L109 away from the pore center. The expanded pore allows water to enter, breaking the vapor lock and enabling hydrated ions to pass through.

This mechanism was suggested by modeling of hydrophobic nanotubes, which show that water filling of narrow hydrophobic pores is an all-or-none transition dependent on pore diameter. The hydrophobic gating principle explains how channels can achieve extremely low leak conductance in the closed state while maintaining rapid, complete opening upon stimulation.

The MscS structure demonstrates that the TM3a helices remain predominantly hydrophobic even in the open state — the main-chain atoms are involved in helical hydrogen bonding and do not present a polar surface. This suggests that pore solvation, not the presentation of polar residues, is the key determinant of the conducting state.
