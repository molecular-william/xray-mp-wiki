# DMT Superfamily (Drug/Metabolite Transporter Superfamily)

## Overview
The drug/metabolite transporter (DMT) superfamily is a large group of membrane transporters ubiquitously found in eukaryotes, bacteria, and archaea. It comprises more than 32 families and includes exporters for a remarkably wide range of substrates, such as toxic compounds, drugs, amino acids, nucleotide sugars, and metabolites. DMT proteins are predicted to contain ten transmembrane segments arranged as two five-transmembrane internal repeats, likely formed by gene duplication. The overall structure is basket-shaped with a large central substrate-binding cavity.


## Mechanism/Details
DMT superfamily transporters share a common architectural blueprint: ten transmembrane alpha-helices organized as two inverted structural repeats (N-half: TM1-TM5; C-half: TM6-TM10) related by two-fold pseudo-symmetry. The two halves surround a central substrate-binding cavity that deeply penetrates the inner leaflet of the membrane. Transport is proposed to occur via an alternating-access mechanism in which the transporter cycles between outward-facing and inward-facing conformations. The conformational change involves bending and straightening of transmembrane helices, with the extracellular and intracellular gates opening and closing in a coordinated manner. The intracellular gate is sealed by conserved hydrophobic and polar interactions that can dissociate during the transport cycle.
