# Neurotransmitter/Sodium Symporter (NSS) Family

## Overview
The NSS (neurotransmitter/sodium symporter) family is a major group of secondary active transporters that use the Na+ electrochemical gradient to drive the transport of neurotransmitters and amino acids across cell membranes. The family includes bacterial amino acid transporters (e.g., [LEUT](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/leut/), [MhsT Multi-Hydrophobic Amino Acid Transporter from Bacillus halodurans](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/slc-transporters/mhsT/)) and human neurotransmitter transporters ([SERT](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/slc-transporters/ssert/), DAT, GAT1, NET) belonging to the SLC6 family. All NSS members share a conserved LeuT-fold architecture with 12 transmembrane helices arranged in two domains, and a transport mechanism involving Na+-coupled alternating access. Key structural features include the Na1 and Na2 ion binding sites, the primary substrate binding site (S1), and the conserved GlyX9Pro motif in TM5 that enables TM5 unwinding and Na2 site solvation during the transport cycle.


## Mechanism/Details
The neurotransmitter/sodium symporter (NSS) family encompasses a large group of prokaryotic and eukaryotic membrane proteins that exploit the energy stored in the Na+ electrochemical gradient to power the uphill transport of their respective substrates. Bacterial NSSs mediate the Na+-coupled uptake of amino acids, while many human NSSs (also known as the SLC6 family) are expressed in presynaptic neurons, where they perform the Na+/Cl--coupled reuptake of neurotransmitters such as [Serotonin (5-Hydroxytryptamine, 5-HT)](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/serotonin/), [Dopamine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/dopamine/), GABA, and [Glycine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/buffers/glycine/) from the synaptic cleft. Human NSS members are therapeutic drug targets for the treatment of depression, epilepsy, ADHD, and are targets for psychostimulants such as [Cocaine](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/ligands/cocaine/) and amphetamine. The LeuT-fold architecture features an inverted pseudo-two-fold symmetry relating two transmembrane domains of 5 helices each, with conserved Na1 and Na2 binding sites and a primary substrate binding site (S1) between the scaffold and bundle domains. A key mechanistic feature is the GlyX9Pro motif in transmembrane helix 5, which promotes TM5 unwinding and cytoplasmic water access to the Na2 site during the transition to inward-facing states, facilitating Na+ release and substrate translocation.
