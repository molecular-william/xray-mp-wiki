# Proton-Exit Channel

## Overview
The proton-exit channel is a structural feature in light-driven proton pumps
(archaeal rhodopsins) that conducts protons unidirectionally from the Schiff
base region to the extracellular bulk solvent. It is distinct from the proton
release complex formed by paired glutamate residues (e.g., Glu194/Glu204 in
bacteriorhodopsin) that constitutes the final step of proton release. The
channel comprises a network of internal water molecules, hydrogen-bonded
amino acid side chains, and an exit portal on the extracellular surface.


## Mechanism/Details
The proton-exit channel is structurally conserved among archaeal
light-driven proton pumps including bacteriorhodopsin, archaerhodopsin-2,
cruxrhodopsin-3, and deltarhodopsin-3. In these proteins, the paired
glutamate residues (Glu194/Glu204 in BR numbering) are separated by an
unusually short distance (~2.3 Å) characteristic of a low-barrier hydrogen
bond. This structure is most clearly observed in crystals grown by the
membrane-fusion method that preserves native lipid-protein interactions.

In schizorhodopsins (inward proton pumps), the proton exit channel is
functionally inverted, with proton release occurring on the cytoplasmic
side rather than the extracellular side, reflecting the opposite direction
of transport.
