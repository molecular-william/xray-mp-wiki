# Ca2+:Cation Antiporter (CaCA) Superfamily

## Overview
The Ca2+:cation (CaCA) superfamily is a large and ancient family of integral membrane proteins that catalyse the exchange of Ca2+ for another cation (H+ or Na+) across biological membranes. Members include the Na+/Ca2+ (NCX) and Ca2+/H+ (CAX) antiporters. In mammals, NCX and related proteins constitute families SLC8 and SLC24, responsible for re-establishing Ca2+ resting potential in muscle cells, neuronal signalling, and Ca2+ reabsorption in the kidney. The CAX family members maintain cytosolic Ca2+ homeostasis in plants and fungi. The superfamily is defined by two short repeating homologous sequences called α-repeats, found in predicted transmembrane regions, which are opposite in topology and believed to have arisen from a gene duplication event.


## Mechanism/Details
The CaCA superfamily operates via an alternating access mechanism. The Vcx1 structure from Saccharomyces cerevisiae (PDB 4KNA, 2.3 Å) revealed the first cytosol-facing, substrate-bound conformation within the superfamily. Comparison with the archaeal mjNCX structure (PDB 3V5U), which represents a periplasm-facing conformation, showed that translational movements of the M1/M6 helices act as a piston-like mechanism. The M2a helix shifts by ~16 Å between conformations. Key acidic residues (Glu or Asp) in the α-repeats coordinate Ca2+ ions at the active site — specifically Glu106 and Glu302 in Vcx1. The transport cycle is driven by the proton motive gradient across membranes, enabling high-throughput Ca2+ exchange.
