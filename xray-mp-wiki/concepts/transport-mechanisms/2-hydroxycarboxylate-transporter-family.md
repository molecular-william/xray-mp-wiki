# 2-Hydroxycarboxylate Transporter (2-HCT) Family

## Overview
The 2-hydroxycarboxylate transporter (2-HCT) family (TC 2.A.24) comprises secondary active transporters that couple the uptake of di- and tricarboxylate substrates (citrate, malate, isocitrate) to sodium or proton symport. Members include bacterial CitS transporters, the human NaCT (SLC13A5), and the Drosophila INDY transporter. These proteins share a conserved structural architecture of two inverted 5-TM repeats with helix hairpins that define the substrate-binding site, and function through an elevator/rotating-bundle mechanism.


## Mechanism/Details
2-HCT family transporters are homodimers of two protomers, each organized into a helix bundle domain and a dimer contact domain. The substrate-binding site is located at the interface between these domains, defined by unwound stretches of two helix hairpins. Transport proceeds via a rigid-body rotation of the helix bundle relative to the static dimer contact domain, an elevator-like or rotating-arc mechanism. In SeCitS, this involves a 31-degree rotation that translocates bound substrates by 16 A across the membrane. The family is structurally related to the NhaP Na+/H+ antiporter family and the bile acid transporter ASBT, despite low sequence homology, suggesting a common mechanistic basis for a large superfamily of secondary transporters.

The name "2-hydroxycarboxylate" refers to the substrate specificity: these transporters recognize 2-hydroxycarboxylic acids (containing a hydroxyl group at the alpha carbon). Endogenous substrates include citrate, malate, and isocitrate. The human member NaCT (SLC13A5) transports citrate and is involved in fatty acid synthesis and glycolysis, making it a potential drug target for metabolic disorders.
