# Glutamate Transporter Family (SLC1/EAAT)

## Overview
The glutamate transporter family (SLC1 in humans, also known as excitatory
amino acid transporters, EAATs) comprises secondary active transporters that
mediate the uptake of glutamate and aspartate into cells. In mammals, EAATs
are responsible for clearing glutamate from the synaptic cleft, preventing
excitotoxicity and enabling proper neuronal communication. The family
includes five human subtypes (EAAT1-5) and numerous prokaryotic homologs
such as GltPh from Pyrococcus horikoshii and GltTk from Thermococcus
kodakarensis.

## Mechanism/Details
Glutamate transporters function through an elevator-type alternating access
mechanism. The transporter is a homotrimer, with each protomer consisting
of a rigid scaffold domain and a mobile transport domain. The transport
domain, which contains the substrate and ion binding sites, moves vertically
across the membrane relative to the scaffold domain, shuttling substrate
from the extracellular to the intracellular side.

Key features of the glutamate transporter family:
- Homotrimeric architecture with a central aqueous cavity
- Each protomer functions independently
- Couples transport of one glutamate/aspartate molecule to co-transport
  of three Na+ ions and one H+, and counter-transport of one K+ ion
- Highly conserved substrate-binding site residues across prokaryotic and
  eukaryotic members
- The substrate binding site binds L-glutamate, L-aspartate, and D-aspartate
  with similar affinity (low uM range)
- D-glutamate has significantly lower affinity (mM range) due to steric
  clashes from the extra methylene group
- Three sodium ion binding sites (Na1, Na2, Na3) are conserved, with none
  directly coordinated by substrate
- The archaeal homologs GltPh (from P. horikoshii) and GltTk (from
  T. kodakarensis) are the best structurally characterized members
- Structures of mammalian family members (EAAT1, ASCT2) confirm
  conserved binding site architecture
