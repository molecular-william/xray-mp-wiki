# Folded Protein Translocation

## Overview
Folded protein translocation is the process by which fully folded (or near-native) proteins are transported across biological membranes. Unlike the general secretory (Sec) pathway that translocates unfolded polypeptide chains, specialized transport systems exist to move folded and even oligomeric proteins across membranes. Key systems include the twin-arginine translocation (Tat) pathway in bacteria and chloroplasts, the BCS1 AAA-ATPase system for mitochondrial complex III assembly, and peroxisomal import pathways.


## Mechanism/Details
Folded protein translocation systems face the unique challenge of moving large, structurally defined substrates through membranes without disrupting their native folds. The bacterial Tat pathway uses a "hitchhiker" or "trapdoor" mechanism where folded substrates are recognized by an N-terminal twin-arginine signal peptide and translocated through a transient pore formed by the TatA oligomer, driven by the proton motive force. The BCS1 AAA-ATPase system in mitochondria uses ATP hydrolysis to mechanically thread the folded Rieske iron-sulfur protein across the inner membrane for insertion into complex III. These systems are fundamentally different from the Sec translocon, which threads unfolded chains through a narrow channel.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- TatABC System (E. coli) — The canonical Tat translocon that transports folded proteins with twin-arginine signal peptides across the bacterial cytoplasmic membrane; substrate range includes redox enzymes and cofactor-containing proteins
- BCS1 — Mitochondrial AAA-ATPase that translocates the folded Rieske iron-sulfur protein across the inner membrane for complex III assembly; uses ATP-driven mechanical force
