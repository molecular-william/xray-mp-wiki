# Lid Domain in Sugar Transport Proteins (STPs)

## Overview
The Lid domain is a conserved structural feature of Sugar Transport Proteins
(STPs) in the Monosaccharide Transporter (MST) superfamily. First identified
in the crystal structure of Arabidopsis thaliana STP10 at 2.4 Å resolution,
the Lid domain is a small extracellular domain that locks the mobile
transmembrane domains through a conserved disulfide bridge (Cys77-Cys449
in STP10). It creates a protected environment around the proton donor/
acceptor pair (Asp42-Arg142), enabling efficient coupling of the proton
gradient to drive high-affinity sugar uptake. The Lid domain contains a
cluster of aromatic residues (Phe55, Phe59, Phe60, Phe79, Phe87, Trp202)
that isolate the proton donor/acceptor site from the extracellular space,
helping maintain the pKa of Asp42 for transport activity across a broad
pH range.


## Mechanism/Details
The Lid domain is positioned on the extracellular side of STP10, covalently
linked to the core C-domain through a disulfide bridge between Cys77 (in the
Lid domain) and Cys449 (in the C-domain). This disulfide bridge is conserved
in all STPs and is essential for maintaining the Lid domain's structural
integrity.

Key functions of the Lid domain:
- **pH tolerance**: The Lid domain creates a microenvironment that isolates
  the proton donor/acceptor pair (Asp42-Arg142), maintaining Asp42
  protonation at physiological apoplastic pH (5-6) and allowing efficient
  transport even during stress-induced apoplastic alkalization.
- **High affinity substrate binding**: Together with the N-domain, the Lid
  domain creates a hydrophobic interaction surface for glucose binding,
  involving Phe39 and Leu43, which contributes to the μM affinity of STPs
  for their substrates.
- **Disulfide bridge regulation**: Breaking the disulfide bridge (C77A or
  C449A mutants) makes the protein increasingly sensitive to alkaline pH,
  functioning only at acidic pH (<5). The equivalent mutation in wheat
  Lr67 reintroduces pathogen susceptibility, highlighting the physiological
  importance of the Lid domain in plant defense.

The Lid domain implies a reevaluation of the transport model for Major
Facilitator Superfamily proteins, suggesting that STPs may have a more
constrained conformational cycle compared to other MFS transporters due
to the Lid domain clamping down the extracellular side.
