# Conformational Dynamics in MFS Transporters

## Overview
[MFS](/xray-mp-wiki/concepts/protein-families/mfs-transporter/) transporters exhibit complex conformational dynamics, alternating between inward-facing and outward-facing states to transport substrates across membranes. The inward-closed conformation, where the cytoplasmic gate is sealed, represents a key intermediate in the transport cycle. The cytoplasmic rim surrounding the substrate recognition pocket plays a critical role in regulating conformational transitions. Electrostatic properties of the rim influence the stability of different conformational states.


## Mechanism/Details
The Major Facilitator Superfamily ([MFS](/xray-mp-wiki/concepts/protein-families/mfs-transporter/)) transporters operate via an alternating-access mechanism, typically described by the rocker-switch model. However, recent studies reveal additional layers of conformational complexity. For the E. coli multidrug transporter [MdfA Multidrug Efflux Transporter](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/mfs-transporters/mdfA/), membrane-embedded forms were found to assume a relatively stable inward-closed conformation, which is distinct from the inward-open conformation seen in crystal structures of crystallization-competent mutants. This inward-closed state is further stabilized by substrate binding and by [DXC](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/additives/deoxycholate/). The cytoplasmic rim, a ring of residues around the entrance to the substrate recognition pocket from the cytoplasm, acts as a conformational determinant. Electrostatic asymmetry of the rim and the introduction of charged residues at specific positions can lock the transporter in inactive conformations. Molecular dynamics simulations reveal that [MdfA Multidrug Efflux Transporter](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/mfs-transporters/mdfA/) fluctuates among various conformations, with the inward-closed state being particularly stable in the membrane environment.
