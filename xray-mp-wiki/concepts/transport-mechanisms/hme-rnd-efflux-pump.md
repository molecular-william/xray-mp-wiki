# HME-RND Efflux Pump (Heavy-Metal Efflux RND)

## Overview
The heavy-metal efflux resistance-nodulation-cell division (HME-RND) subfamily comprises inner-membrane transporters that specifically export toxic heavy metal ions such as Cu(I) and Ag(I) from Gram-negative bacteria. These transporters are part of tripartite efflux complexes that span the entire cell envelope. The first structurally characterized HME-RND member is E. coli CusA.

## Mechanism/Details
HME-RND transporters share the overall architecture of RND superfamily members but have specialized metal-binding sites using methionine and cysteine sulfur ligands rather than the hydrophobic/amphipathic substrate-binding pockets of HAE-RND pumps. They operate through an alternating-access mechanism where periplasmic clefts open and close, coupled with proton-driven conformational changes. They function as homotrimers with each protomer containing transmembrane (DN, DC) and periplasmic (PN1, PN2, PC1, PC2) subdomains. Metal ions are transported via stepwise shuttle through methionine relay networks spanning from the cytoplasm through the transmembrane region to the central funnel.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- CusA (E. coli) — First HME-RND structure. Transports Cu(I) and Ag(I) via methionine relay. Part of CusCBA tripartite efflux complex.
- CzcA — Related HME-RND pump for Zn(II)/Co(II)/Cd(II) efflux. Shown capable of transporting substrates from cytosol.
