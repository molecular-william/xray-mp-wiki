# ABC Transporter Allosteric Regulation

## Overview
ABC transporters can be allosterically regulated through regulatory domains fused to the core transporter subunits. Substrate binding to these domains stabilizes specific conformational states, modulating ATPase activity and transport. This provides bacteria with a mechanism to control transport activity in response to intracellular metabolite concentrations, efficiently allocating cellular energy resources.


## Mechanism/Details
The MetNI methionine ABC transporter illustrates this mechanism. Each MetN subunit contains a C-terminal C2 (ACT) domain that dimerizes to form an eight-stranded β sheet. Methionine binds near the C2 dimer interface (near residues Met301 and Met312), stabilizing the inward-facing, ATPase-inactive conformation of the transporter. By sterically interfering with NBD (nucleotide-binding domain) association, the ATP-driven engine that powers transport is disrupted, thereby providing a molecular mechanism for transinhibition — where increasing levels of internal methionine inhibit further methionine uptake.

This mechanism parallels observations in other ACT domain-containing proteins, such as phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase, where serine binding to allosteric sites at ACT domain interfaces propagates conformational changes to distinct catalytic domains. The C2 domains in MetNI show a ~15° twist between their free and transporter-bound forms, demonstrating the flexibility required for allosteric regulation.

Beyond methionine transport, this mechanism is broadly relevant: transporter activity represents a major component of bacterial ATP requirements, and allosteric regulation allows cells to more efficiently control the allocation of cellular energy resources by coupling transport activity to metabolite availability.
