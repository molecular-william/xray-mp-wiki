# M-PPase Ion Pumping Mechanism

## Overview
Membrane-integral pyrophosphatases (M-PPases) are ion pumps that couple the
hydrolysis of inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi) to the translocation of Na+ or H+
across biological membranes. The catalytic cycle involves four intermediates:
resting state, substrate-bound, product-bound, and post-hydrolysis. Pyrophosphate
binding induces a concerted contraction involving movement of TM12, which opens
a gate and exit channel at the periplasmic side while closing the active site.
The gate features an ion triplet (Asp243-Lys707-Glu246 in [TmPPase (Thermotoga maritima H+-Pumping Pyrophosphatase)](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/pumps-atpases/tmppase/)) that determines
Na+ vs H+ selectivity. A coupling funnel of eight conserved charged residues
transduces chemical energy from PPi hydrolysis to ion pumping.


## Mechanism/Details
The binding-change mechanism of M-PPases begins with PPi binding to the
hydrolytic center, coordinated by Mg2+ ions. This induces closure of the
active site via movement of the TM5-TM6 loop and a concerted contraction that
moves TM12 (helix 12), opening both the gate and the periplasmic exit channel.
The conformational change eliminates a high-affinity Na+-binding site at the
top of the gate, releasing Na+ to a low-affinity site in the exit channel
for diffusion into the periplasm. The closed active site prevents back-transfer.
After hydrolysis, the electrophilic phosphate and then the leaving-group
phosphate dissociate, the gate closes, and the protein reverts to its resting
state. Potassium-dependent M-PPases use K+ to coordinate the lower phosphate,
while K+-independent variants use a lysine residue at the equivalent position.
