# Methionine-Mediated Metal Transport

## Overview
Methionine-mediated metal transport is a mechanism where methionine residues, either as pairs or clusters, coordinate Cu(I) and Ag(I) ions via sulfur-metal coordination and shuttle them sequentially through a membrane transporter. This mechanism was first structurally characterized in the E. coli CusA heavy-metal efflux pump, which uses five methionine pairs/clusters to form a relay network for stepwise metal export.

## Mechanism/Details
Cu(I) and Ag(I) ions are soft Lewis acids with high affinity for sulfur-containing ligands. Methionine side chains contain thioether sulfur atoms that can coordinate these monovalent metal ions. In CusA, methionine residues are arranged as: (1) a primary three-methionine cluster (M573/M623/M672) in the periplasmic cleft that opens upon metal binding via a 30° swing of the PC2 subdomain; (2) four methionine pairs (M271/M755, M391/M1009, M403/M486, M410/M501) forming a channel through the transmembrane region. A stepwise shuttle mechanism transfers metal ions sequentially between these sites from the cytoplasm to the periplasmic funnel for extrusion. This mechanism is analogous to methionine-only coordination seen in copper chaperones like CusF, CueR, and Atx1.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- CusA (E. coli HME-RND efflux pump) — Five methionine pairs/clusters form relay: M573/M623/M672 (primary site), M271/M755, M391/M1009, M403/M486, M410/M501. Mutagenesis of any site abolishes Cu/Ag transport.
- CusF (E. coli copper-binding protein) — Uses methionine residues for Cu(I) recognition via cation-π and methionine interactions.
