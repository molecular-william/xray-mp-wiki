# Antibiotic Resistance Mechanisms

## Overview
Antibiotic resistance mechanisms are the diverse strategies employed by bacteria to survive exposure to antimicrobial compounds. These mechanisms are broadly classified into four categories: (1) enzymatic inactivation or modification of the antibiotic (e.g., beta-lactamases hydrolyzing beta-lactam antibiotics, aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes), (2) target site modification or protection (e.g., methylation of 23S rRNA conferring macrolide resistance, mutations in DNA gyrase conferring quinolone resistance), (3) reduced intracellular accumulation through decreased permeability (e.g., loss or modification of porin channels in the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria) and/or increased efflux via multidrug efflux pumps (e.g., AcrAB-TolC in E. coli, MexAB-OprM in P. aeruginosa), and (4) bypass of the antibiotic target pathway (e.g., alternative dihydrofolate reductase conferring trimethoprim resistance). Membrane proteins play central roles in several of these mechanisms, particularly in efflux (RND transporters, MFS transporters), outer membrane permeability (porins), and enzymatic modification of antibiotics at the membrane interface (beta-lactamases tethered to the membrane). The spread of resistance genes via mobile genetic elements (plasmids, transposons) has led to the global emergence of multidrug-resistant (MDR) and extensively drug-resistant (XDR) pathogens.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- AcrAB-TolC Efflux System (E. coli) — A tripartite RND-type efflux pump spanning the inner membrane, periplasm, and outer membrane that exports a broad range of antibiotics including tetracycline, chloramphenicol, fluoroquinolones, and beta-lactams.
- TEM-1 Beta-Lactamase — A periplasmic enzyme that hydrolyzes penicillins and early cephalosporins. Its active site serine attacks the beta-lactam ring, opening it and rendering the antibiotic inactive.
