# Quinol-Fumarate Reductase (QFR)

## Overview


## Mechanism/Details
Quinol-fumarate reductase (QFR) is an anaerobic form of respiratory complex II that
catalyzes the reduction of fumarate to succinate coupled to the oxidation of a
quinol (typically rhodoquinol or menaquinol). This reaction is the reverse of the
aerobic succinate-ubiquinone reductase (SQR) reaction. QFR plays an essential role
in anaerobic respiratory chains, particularly in parasitic helminths (e.g., Ascaris
suum) that inhabit low-oxygen environments. The enzyme is composed of a flavoprotein
subunit (Fp) containing FAD, an iron-sulphur subunit (Ip) containing [2Fe-2S],
[4Fe-4S], and [3Fe-4S] clusters, and two membrane-anchoring cytochrome b subunits
(CybL and CybS) that bind a haem b and the quinone substrate.

The direction of catalysis is governed by the redox potential of the quinone
substrate: rhodoquinol (Em' = -63 mV) favours fumarate reduction, while ubiquinol
(Em' = +110 mV) favours succinate oxidation. QFR-type complex IIs are structurally
similar to SQR-type but exhibit key differences in the membrane anchor region,
quinone binding pocket architecture, and regulatory elements.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [Ascaris suum Quinol-Fumarate Reductase](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/ascaris-suum-quinol-fumarate-reductase/) — First crystal structure of a mitochondrial QFR-type complex II (anaerobic complex II). Determined at 2.85 A resolution (PDB 3VR8, 3VRB), revealing four subunits (Fp, Ip, CybL, CybS) with FAD, three Fe-S clusters, haem b, and rhodoquinone. The structure shows a twisted fumarate conformation enabling hydride transfer and a unique 27-residue N-terminal peptide in CybS.
