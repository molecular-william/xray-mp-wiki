# Metalloprotease Mechanism

## Overview
Metalloproteases are proteolytic enzymes that utilize a catalytic metal ion, typically zinc (Zn2+), at their active site to catalyze peptide bond hydrolysis. The zinc ion is coordinated by three protein ligands (typically two His residues and one Glu, Asp, or His, with a geometry dependent on the metalloprotease clan) and a water molecule. The catalytic mechanism involves activation of the zinc-bound water molecule by a nearby general base (often a glutamate residue), generating a hydroxide nucleophile that attacks the scissile carbonyl carbon. The tetrahedral intermediate is stabilized by the zinc ion (as a Lewis acid) and by an oxyanion hole formed by backbone amide groups. Metalloproteases are classified into several clans (e.g., HEXXH motif-containing zincins, inverzincins, aspzincins) based on their active site geometry and metal-coordinating residues. In membrane proteins, catalytically active metalloprotease domains are found in the membrane-anchored AAA protease FtsH, the insulin-degrading enzyme (IDE), and various sheddases such as ADAM and TACE. Membrane metalloproteases are involved in protein quality control, cell signaling, and extracellular matrix remodeling.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- FtsH AAA Protease — FtsH contains a zinc metalloprotease domain of the aspzincin family with the HEXXH motif. The zinc ion is coordinated by His417, His421, and Glu436, and the catalytic base is Glu417.
- Thermolysin (Bacterial Metalloprotease) — Thermolysin is a prototypical zinc metalloprotease (HEXXH+E motif), with zinc coordinated by His142, His146, and Glu166. It has been extensively used in structural studies of metalloprotease mechanism.
