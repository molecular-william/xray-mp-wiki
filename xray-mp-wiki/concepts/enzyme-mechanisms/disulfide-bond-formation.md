# Disulfide Bond Formation in Bacteria

## Overview
Disulfide bond formation in bacteria is catalyzed by the Dsb system, comprising the inner membrane protein DsbB and the periplasmic oxidase DsbA. DsbB uses the oxidizing power of membrane-bound quinones (ubiquinone/menaquinone) to generate de novo disulfide bonds, which are then transferred to DsbA via a thiol-disulfide exchange mechanism. The crystal structure of the DsbB-DsbA complex (PDB 2HI7) revealed a cysteine relocation mechanism whereby DsbA binding induces spatial separation of DsbB catalytic cysteines Cys104 and Cys130, preventing backward resolution of the intermolecular disulfide and enabling unidirectional electron flow from DsbA to DsbB. DsbB contains four transmembrane helices, two periplasmic loops harboring essential cysteine pairs (Cys41-Cys44, Cys104-Cys130), and a membrane-associated horizontal helix. The quinone-binding site is located near the N-terminus of TM2, adjacent to Cys44 and Arg48, forming a reaction center for disulfide bond generation.
