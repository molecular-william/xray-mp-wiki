# Ser-His Catalytic Dyad

## Overview
The Ser-His catalytic dyad is a minimal catalytic apparatus for peptide bond hydrolysis found in a growing family of serine proteases and hydrolases, including the signal peptidase I (SPase I) family of membrane proteins and the Lon protease family. Unlike the classical Ser-His-Asp catalytic triad of chymotrypsin-like serine proteases, the dyad lacks the third aspartate residue; the histidine alone acts as the general base to activate the serine hydroxyl for nucleophilic attack on the scissile carbonyl carbon. The absence of the catalytic aspartate changes the electrostatic environment of the active site, and the source of the negative charge needed to stabilize the tetrahedral intermediate varies: in some cases a backbone carbonyl group serves as the oxyanion hole, while in others a water molecule or additional structural elements provide stabilization. Ser-His dyad proteases include the bacterial type I signal peptidase (LepB), which cleaves signal peptides from secreted proteins at the periplasmic side of the inner membrane. The dyad mechanism is less efficient than the triad but may have evolved independently in multiple protease lineages.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- E. coli Signal Peptidase I (LepB) — LepB uses a Ser90-Lys145 catalytic dyad (not Ser-His) in the membrane-associated domain, with Lys145 acting as the general base for Ser90 activation. This Lys-based dyad is functionally analogous to the Ser-His dyad.
- Archaeal Signal Peptidase (Spase I) — The canonical membrane-bound signal peptidase uses a Ser-His catalytic dyad with the active site located at the periplasmic or lumenal face of the membrane.
- Lon Protease — The Lon AAA+ protease uses a Ser-His dyad in its proteolytic domain, where Ser679 and His665 form the catalytic dyad, with a water molecule substituting for the missing Asp of the triad.
- Cytomegalovirus Protease — The herpesvirus protease uses a Ser-His catalytic dyad mechanism, distinct from the classical serine protease triad, for viral capsid maturation. The active site Ser and His residues are located in a shallow groove on the protein surface.
