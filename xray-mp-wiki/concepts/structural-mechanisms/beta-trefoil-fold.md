# Beta-Trefoil Fold

## Overview
The beta-trefoil fold is a symmetric protein fold characterized by a threefold internal repeat of a beta-sheet structural motif that forms a barrel-like structure with a central alpha/beta core. The fold is constructed from approximately 12 beta-strands arranged into three structurally similar "trefoil" subdomains, each containing four strands arranged in a beta-hairpin and two beta-strand motif. Beta-trefoil proteins are found in diverse biological contexts including lectins (such as ricin B-chain), cytokines (such as fibroblast growth factors, FGFs), and enzyme inhibitors.


## Mechanism/Details
The beta-trefoil architecture consists of three repeated units (trefoil arms) arranged with pseudo-3-fold symmetry. Each arm contributes four beta-strands: two that form a beta-hairpin and two that form part of the central beta-barrel. Six strands form a six-stranded antiparallel beta-barrel (the "stem"), while the remaining six strands form three beta-hairpins (the "leaflets") that cap the barrel. The fold exhibits remarkable structural plasticity that accommodates diverse loop extensions and insertions between the core beta-strands, enabling a wide range of binding functions including carbohydrate recognition in lectins and receptor binding in cytokines.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Ricin B-Chain — Classic example of beta-trefoil lectin; each trefoil arm contains a carbohydrate-binding site that recognizes galactose-containing glycans
- Human Fibroblast Growth Factor 1 (FGF1) — Cytokine with beta-trefoil fold; the FGF family extensively uses this fold for receptor binding and signaling
