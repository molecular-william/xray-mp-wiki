# Hydrogen Bond Networks

## Overview
Hydrogen bond networks are extended arrays of interconnected hydrogen bonds within a protein that collectively mediate long-range structural communication, proton transfer, and allosteric regulation. In membrane proteins, hydrogen bond networks are particularly important because the low-dielectric environment of the lipid bilayer enhances their energetic contribution. These networks often involve ordered water molecules, polar side chains, and backbone carbonyl and amide groups arranged in chains or clusters that span functionally critical regions. Classical examples include proton wires (Grotthuss mechanism chains) in proton pumps and channels, where a network of hydrogen-bonded water molecules and protonatable side chains enables rapid proton conduction via sequential bond rearrangement without requiring diffusion of the proton itself. The structural plasticity of hydrogen bond networks allows them to dynamically reorganize during functional cycles, enabling state-dependent modulation of protein activity.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Bacteriorhodopsin — The proton release channel contains a hydrogen bond network involving water molecules, Asp85, Glu194, and Glu204 that conducts the released proton to the extracellular surface.
- Influenza A M2 Proton Channel — A hydrogen bond network of water molecules and the His37 tetrad enables proton conduction through the channel via a Grotthuss-like mechanism.
