# PIF Motif (Pro-Ile-Phe)

## Overview
The PIF motif (Pro-Ile-Phe) is a conserved structural microswitch located in the ligand-binding pocket of class A G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), formed by residues at positions 5.50 (Pro), 3.40 (Ile), and 6.44 (Phe) in the Ballesteros-Weinstein numbering system. This motif acts as a "rotamer toggle switch" that senses agonist binding and relays the activation signal from the orthosteric binding pocket to the conserved DRY motif and NPxxY motif in the intracellular region.


## Mechanism/Details
In the inactive state of class A GPCRs, the PIF motif adopts a specific conformation with the aromatic side chain of Phe6.44 packed against the binding pocket. Upon agonist binding, the side chain of Phe6.44 undergoes a rotameric change, disrupting stabilizing interactions with neighboring residues. This conformational rearrangement couples to the outward movement of TM6 and the breaking of the ionic lock at the DRY motif. The PIF motif is considered one of three conserved microswitches (along with DRY and NPxxY) that together control the transition between inactive and active GPCR states.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Human M2 Muscarinic Receptor — The PIF motif (P2065.50, I1663.40, F3906.44) was identified as a key activation microswitch in inactive (PDB 3UON) and active (PDB 4MQS) crystal structures
- Beta2-Adrenergic Receptor — PIF motif rearrangement upon agonist binding couples ligand recognition to TM6 motion required for G protein coupling
