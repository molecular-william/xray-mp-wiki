# Carbohydrate-Binding Module (CBM)

## Overview
Carbohydrate-binding modules (CBMs) are non-catalytic protein domains found within carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZymes) that facilitate substrate recognition and binding to polysaccharides. CBMs enhance the catalytic efficiency of their linked enzymes by bringing the catalytic domain into close proximity with its target substrate and concentrating enzymatic activity on specific carbohydrate surfaces. They are classified into over 80 families based on amino acid sequence similarity.


## Mechanism/Details
CBMs bind to specific carbohydrate substrates through a combination of hydrogen bonding, van der Waals interactions, and stacking interactions between aromatic amino acid side chains (typically tryptophan, tyrosine, and phenylalanine) and the sugar rings. CBMs are classified into three types based on binding mode: Type A (surface-binding modules that bind to crystalline polysaccharides), Type B (glycan chain-binding modules that bind to individual polysaccharide chains), and Type C (small sugar-binding modules that bind to mono-, di-, and tri-saccharides). The beta-sandwich fold is the most common CBM architecture, though other folds including beta-trefoil and OB-fold variants exist.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- CBM2a from Cellulomonas fimi — Type A CBM that binds to crystalline cellulose via a planar surface of aromatic residues; prototypical surface-binding module
- CBM4-1 from Rhodothermus marinus — Type B CBM that binds to xylan through a groove-shaped binding site; demonstrates glycan chain recognition
