# GPCR Ionic Lock

## Overview
The GPCR ionic lock is a conserved salt bridge interaction between the arginine of the DRY motif (R3.50) at the cytoplasmic end of TM3 and a glutamate residue (E6.30) on TM6 of class A G protein-coupled receptors. This electrostatic interaction stabilizes the inactive conformation of the receptor and must be disrupted for full activation and G protein coupling.


## Mechanism/Details
In the inactive state, the positively charged guanidinium group of Arg3.50 forms a salt bridge with the negatively charged carboxylate of Glu6.30 (or Asp6.30 in some receptors), tethering TM6 to TM3 at the cytoplasmic surface. This interaction is part of a larger hydrogen-bonding network that includes adjacent residues. Upon agonist binding, TM6 undergoes an outward rigid-body rotation of approximately 6-14 A at the cytoplasmic end, breaking the ionic lock. The breaking of the ionic lock is a hallmark of GPCR activation, allowing the intracellular cavity to open for G protein coupling. Some constitutively active GPCR mutants lack this ionic lock, while some GPCRs (e.g., rhodopsin) have a slightly different arrangement using homologous residues.


## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Beta2-Adrenergic Receptor — The ionic lock between R131(3.50) and E268(6.30) is observed in the inactive carazolol-bound structure (PDB 2RH1) but broken in the active nanobody-stabilized structure (PDB 3P0G)
- Rhodopsin — R135(3.50) interacts with E247(6.30) in the dark-adapted inactive state; light activation breaks this interaction
