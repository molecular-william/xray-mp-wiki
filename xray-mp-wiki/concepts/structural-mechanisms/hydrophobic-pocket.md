# Hydrophobic Pocket

## Overview
A hydrophobic pocket is a three-dimensional cavity within a protein that is lined predominantly by non-polar amino acid side chains (e.g., Leu, Val, Ile, Phe, Trp, Met). In membrane proteins, hydrophobic pockets are often found within the transmembrane domain where they serve as ligand-binding sites, substrate translocation pathways, or allosteric regulatory modules. The hydrophobic effect provides the driving force for ligand binding within these pockets, as the displacement of ordered water molecules upon ligand entry increases the overall entropy of the system. GPCR orthosteric and allosteric binding sites are classic examples, where endogenous ligands, drugs, and modulators bind within hydrophobic pockets formed by the transmembrane helix bundle. The shape, volume, and chemical character of these pockets are key determinants of binding specificity and affinity, making them central targets for rational drug design.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Beta-2 Adrenergic Receptor (Beta2AR) — The orthosteric binding pocket for catecholamines is formed by TM3, TM5, TM6, and TM7, with key hydrophobic contacts from Phe289, Phe290, and Trp286.
- Adenosine A2A Receptor — The antagonist ZM241385 binds in a deep hydrophobic pocket between the extracellular loops and the TM bundle, with the triazolotriazine core stacked between Phe168 and Phe190.
- CB1 Cannabinoid Receptor — The ligand-binding pocket accommodates the hydrophobic tail of cannabinoid ligands via extensive hydrophobic contacts with residues in TM2, TM3, TM5, and TM7.
