# Membrane Fusion

## Overview
Membrane fusion is the process by which two distinct lipid bilayers merge into a single continuous membrane, a fundamental event in biological processes including synaptic vesicle exocytosis, viral entry, intracellular vesicle trafficking, fertilization, and cell-cell fusion. Fusion proceeds through a series of intermediate states: approach and docking of the two membranes, formation of a hemifusion stalk (where only the outer leaflets merge), expansion into a fusion pore, and eventual full fusion. The process is catalyzed by specialized fusion proteins that overcome the large activation energy barrier (~40 kT) associated with bringing two negatively charged membrane surfaces into close proximity. SNARE proteins mediate most intracellular membrane fusion events in eukaryotes via a zippering mechanism that forces opposing membranes together. Viral fusion proteins (e.g., influenza hemagglutinin, HIV gp41) use large-scale conformational changes triggered by low pH or receptor binding to drive fusion during host cell entry. Class I, II, and III viral fusion proteins share the common feature of a fusion peptide or loop that inserts into the target membrane.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- SNARE Complex — The neuronal SNARE complex (synaptobrevin, syntaxin, SNAP-25) drives synaptic vesicle fusion through progressive zippering of the four-helix bundle from the membrane-distal to membrane-proximal region.
- Influenza Hemagglutinin (HA) — The prototypical class I viral fusion protein. Low pH in the endosome triggers a large conformational rearrangement that extends a fusion peptide and then folds back into a trimer-of-hairpins, bringing viral and endosomal membranes together.
