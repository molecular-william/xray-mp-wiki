# Right-Handed Coiled-Coil Architecture in Rotary ATPases

## Overview
The peripheral stalk of rotary ATPases adopts an unusual right-handed coiled-coil architecture that enables conformational flexibility essential for the enzyme function. The stalk tail, formed by subunits E and G, extends 120 Angstroms as a right-handed parallel coiled-coil. Its architecture features a hendecad (11-mer) repeat at the N-terminus transitioning to a quindecad (15-mer) repeat at the C-terminus, creating a tightening pitch. This architecture allows the stalk to flex radially to accommodate catalytic head wobbling while maintaining azimuthal stiffness to counteract rotation torque.

## Mechanism/Details
The peripheral stalk tail of rotary ATPases consists of a right-handed parallel coiled-coil formed by subunit G and the N-terminus of subunit E, extending 120 Angstroms. The N-terminal region features a hendecad (11-mer) repeat producing a large pitch and nearly parallel helices, while the C-terminal region switches to a quindecad (15-mer) repeat at residue Ala66 of subunit G, tightening the pitch. This arrangement creates an elbow-like kink in subunit G that brings its C-terminus to the opposite side of subunit E relative to the N-terminus. The right-handed coiled-coil architecture allows the peripheral stalk to flex radially while constraining azimuthal motion, enabling torque counteraction. Normal mode analysis confirms flexion (mode 7) and twisting (mode 8) motions that accommodate the wobbling of the catalytic head during rotary catalysis. The flexibility is inherent within the subunit sequences and conserved across all rotary ATPase subtypes.
