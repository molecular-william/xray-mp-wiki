# Salt Bridge (Ion Pair)

## Overview
A salt bridge (or ion pair) is a non-covalent interaction between oppositely charged amino acid side chains within a protein, most commonly between a basic residue (arginine, lysine, or histidine) and an acidic residue (aspartate or glutamate). Salt bridges combine an electrostatic (Coulombic) interaction with a hydrogen bond, and in the low-dielectric environment of the membrane interior they can contribute significantly to protein stability (3-5 kcal/mol). In membrane proteins, salt bridges serve diverse functional roles including stabilizing tertiary and quaternary structure, gating ion channels, mediating substrate binding in transporters, and enabling conformational switching during transport cycles. The stability of a salt bridge depends on its solvent accessibility: surface-exposed salt bridges are less stabilizing due to competing water interactions, while buried salt bridges within the membrane core are highly stabilizing but relatively rare due to the energetic cost of desolvating charged residues. Networks of interconnected salt bridges (salt bridge networks) are often found at functionally critical interfaces.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- KcsA Potassium Channel — A critical salt bridge between Arg64 and Asp80 in the C-terminal domain stabilizes the tetrameric assembly and regulates channel inactivation.
- Bacteriorhodopsin — The Schiff base counterion complex involves salt bridge interactions between the protonated retinal Schiff base, Asp85, and Asp212.
- Voltage Sensor Domain of Kv Channels — Salt bridges between positively charged arginine residues in S4 and negatively charged residues in S1-S3 (the gating charge transfer center) stabilize the voltage sensor in different activation states.
