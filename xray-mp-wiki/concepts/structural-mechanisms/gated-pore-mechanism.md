# Gated-Pore Mechanism

## Overview
The gated-pore mechanism is an alternating access transport mode in which a transporter alternates between two gates — one extracellular and one intracellular — to move substrates across the membrane. Unlike rocker-switch transporters that undergo large domain movements, gated pores achieve alternating access through smaller conformational changes involving rotation of broken transmembrane helices.


## Mechanism/Details
In gated-pore transporters such as the sodium-solute symporter (SSS) family member vSGLT, the protein is composed of two V-shaped domains intertwined in an antiparallel manner. The substrate-binding site is located at the center of the membrane at two broken helices, one from each domain. Alternating access is achieved by rotating the cytoplasmic portion of two broken helices from the substrate-binding site.

The gated pore resembles an ion channel with two gates instead of one — a thick hydrophobic gate on the extracellular side and a thinner gate on the cytoplasmic side. The structure of vSGLT (Faham et al., 2008) revealed the substrate-bound, inward-facing (Ci) conformation, showing galactose and Na+ bound at the center of the core helices, closed off to the extracellular space by a thick hydrophobic gate and with a large cavity on the cytoplasmic side.

Because the conformational changes required for transport in a gated pore are typically smaller than in rocker-switch transporters, the less dynamic nature of gated pores may offer advantages for crystallization experiments. Gated pores can often display uncoupled, channel-like ion conduction, which has been observed in several neurotransmitter sodium symporter (NSS) proteins.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- [vSGLT](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/slc-transporters/vsglt/) — vSGLT from Vibrio parahaemolyticus was crystallized in the inward-facing (Ci) conformation, demonstrating the gated-pore architecture with two broken helices and a thick hydrophobic extracellular gate.
- [LeuT](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/leut/) — LeuT from Aquifex aeolicus was crystallized in the outward-facing (Co) conformation and exhibits the gated-pore architecture with a vestibule for substrate concentration.
