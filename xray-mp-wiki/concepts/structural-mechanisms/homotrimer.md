# Homotrimer

## Overview
A homotrimer is a protein complex composed of three identical subunits (protomers) that associate through non-covalent interactions to form a three-fold symmetric assembly. In membrane proteins, homotrimeric assemblies are common among channels, transporters, and receptors, where the central three-fold axis often defines a functional pore or substrate translocation pathway. The trimeric architecture allows for the formation of a central pore large enough to accommodate ion or substrate passage while maintaining structural integrity within the lipid bilayer. Well-characterized homotrimeric membrane proteins include the aquaporin water channels, the influenza A M2 proton channel, and the P2X purinergic receptors. The trimeric interface is typically stabilized by a combination of hydrophobic packing, hydrogen bonds, and sometimes inter-subunit disulfide bonds or bound lipids at the inter-subunit boundaries.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Aquaporin-1 (AQP1) — Each AQP1 monomer forms an independent water pore, but the tetrameric assembly (homotetramer, not homotrimer) is a common misconception — AQP1 is actually a tetramer. Many other membrane proteins, however, do form true homotrimers.
- P2X4 Purinergic Receptor — The ATP-gated P2X4 receptor (PDB 4DW1) is a homotrimer with each subunit contributing to the central ion channel pore. Three ATP binding sites are located at the subunit interfaces.
- Influenza A M2 Proton Channel — The M2 proton channel from influenza A is a homotetramer, with the four His37 residues forming the proton gating mechanism.
