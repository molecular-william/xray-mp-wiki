# Hydrogen Bonding in Membrane Proteins

## Overview
Hydrogen bonding is a fundamental non-covalent interaction in which a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to an electronegative donor atom (e.g., N, O, or S) interacts with another electronegative acceptor atom bearing a lone pair of electrons. In membrane proteins, hydrogen bonds play critical roles in stabilizing secondary and tertiary structure, mediating substrate recognition and binding, facilitating proton and ion transport, and enabling inter-subunit communication. The low dielectric environment of the lipid bilayer amplifies the energetic contribution of hydrogen bonds relative to aqueous solution, making them particularly important for maintaining the folded state of transmembrane helices and for stabilizing transient intermediates in transport cycles. The positioning and orientation of hydrogen bond donors and acceptors in transmembrane segments are subject to strong evolutionary constraints, as unsatisfied backbone amide or carbonyl groups in the membrane interior incur a significant energetic penalty.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Bacteriorhodopsin — The retinal Schiff base forms a hydrogen bond with a water molecule and Asp85 during the photocycle, critical for proton transport.
- KcsA Potassium Channel — The selectivity filter is stabilized by a network of hydrogen bonds between backbone carbonyl oxygen atoms and the permeating potassium ions.
- Aquaporin-1 — The selectivity filter (ar/R region) uses hydrogen bonds with backbone amides and the conserved Asn residues to exclude proton passage while permitting water permeation.
- Influenza A M2 Proton Channel — The His37-Trp41 tetrad uses hydrogen bonding and cation-pi interactions to gate proton transport across the viral membrane.
