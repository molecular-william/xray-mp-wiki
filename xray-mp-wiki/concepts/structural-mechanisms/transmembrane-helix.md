# Transmembrane Helix

## Overview
A transmembrane helix (TM helix) is a segment of an integral membrane protein that traverses the lipid bilayer, typically composed of 20-30 hydrophobic amino acid residues arranged in an alpha-helical conformation. TM helices are the fundamental structural units of alpha-helical membrane proteins, which constitute the majority of integral membrane proteins in all domains of life. The hydrophobic stretch of residues that defines a TM helix is enriched in non-polar amino acids (Leu, Ile, Val, Ala, Phe) with characteristic aromatic residues (Trp, Tyr) frequently found at the membrane-water interfacial regions, where they serve as membrane-embedded anchors. TM helices can be tilted, kinked (often at Pro or Gly residues), or irregular to accommodate functional requirements such as ligand binding, ion conduction, or conformational changes. The topology (orientation and number) of TM helices is a key determinant of membrane protein architecture and function, and is classified by databases such as PDBTM and TOPDB.

## Examples in Membrane Protein Work
- Bacteriorhodopsin — Contains seven transmembrane helices (A-G) arranged in a bundle around the retinal chromophore, a classic fold for microbial rhodopsins.
- KcsA Potassium Channel — Each subunit contains two TM helices (inner and outer), with the pore loop between them forming the selectivity filter.
