# Structural Phospholipids

## Overview

Structural phospholipids are lipid molecules that are tightly bound to membrane proteins and are often carried through purification and crystallization. These lipids occupy specific binding sites at protein-protein interfaces or within transmembrane domains and can be critical for structural integrity and function.

## Phosphatidylcholine at KirBac Subunit Interfaces

In KirBac1.1 (PDB: 1P7B), well-ordered phospholipid density was observed at the junction of inner, outer, and slide helices at subunit interfaces:

- **Outer site** (10.5σ in electron density maps): Assigned as the tertiary amine moiety of phosphatidylcholine (PC), though it may equally represent the head of an anionic lipid
- **Inner site** (12.5σ): Assigned as a phosphate group
- **Hydrocarbon tails**: Insufficiently ordered for modeling, but weak electron density consistent with their presence alongside aliphatic side chains of inner and slide helices

## Structural Role

The phospholipid head group at the helix junction connects adjacent subunits, stabilizing the tetrameric assembly:

- **Latched arrangement**: Phospholipid present; Arg137 stabilizes spacing, making two hydrogen bonds to the βH-βI loop of the adjacent subunit. The well-ordered head group (P, O, C, N atoms) bridges adjacent subunits.
- **Unlatched arrangement**: No ordered phospholipid present; subunits spaced apart at the apex. Arg137 still stabilizes spacing via hydrogen bonds.

The critical residues at the phospholipid binding site are two polar residues separated by a small hydrophobic residue — a conserved motif in Kir channels.
