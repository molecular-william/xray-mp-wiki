# Cardiolipin

## Overview

Cardiolipin (CL), also known as diphosphatidylglycerol, is a unique anionic
phospholipid composed of two [Phosphatidylglycerol](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/lipids/phosphatidylglycerol/) units connected by a [Glycerol](/xray-mp-wiki/reagents/additives/glycerol/)
backbone. It constitutes approximately 5% of the total phospholipid content in
most enterobacterial membranes (alongside PE at ~75% and PG at ~20%). CL plays
an essential role in outer membrane biogenesis, bacterial pathogenesis, and
resistance to cationic antimicrobial peptides in Gram-negative bacteria. CL is
also found in mitochondrial inner membranes (~20% of total lipids) where it is
required for mitochondrial function.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Outer membrane biogenesis

In Gram-negative bacteria, cardiolipin is transported from the inner membrane
(where it is synthesized) to the outer membrane by the [PbgA (YejM) Cardiolipin Transport Protein](/xray-mp-wiki/proteins/enzymes/pbgA/) transporter. This
transport is regulated by the PhoPQ two-component system and is essential for
outer membrane remodeling, particularly during infection.


### Mitochondrial function

In mitochondria, CL constitutes approximately 20% of total inner membrane lipids
and is essential for mitochondrial function. CL externalization to the outer
mitochondrial membrane acts as an elimination signal for mitophagy.
