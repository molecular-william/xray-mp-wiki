# Tramadol

## Overview

Tramadol is a synthetic opioid analgesic that also acts as a norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (NRI). It structurally resembles the antidepressant desvenlafaxine and contains a methoxyphenyl ring, a cyclohexanol group, and a dimethylamino group. Tramadol is highly specific to norepinephrine/serotonin transporters over the dopamine transporter, displaying approximately 50-fold greater affinity for NET over DAT. The methoxyphenyl ring binds primarily at subsite C of the primary binding site, which determines NET-specific inhibition.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Treatment of chronic pain

Tramadol is used clinically for treatment of moderate to moderately severe pain, including neuropathic pain and fibromyalgia, due to its dual mechanism of mu-opioid receptor agonism and norepinephrine reuptake inhibition


### Structural studies of neurotransmitter transporters

Co-crystallized with Drosophila melanogaster dopamine transporter (dDAT_NET) to reveal subsite C binding mode of NET-specific inhibitors

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

- High NET specificity (50-fold greater affinity for NET over DAT)
- {'Dual mechanism': 'mu-opioid agonism plus norepinephrine reuptake inhibition'}
- Methoxyphenyl ring at subsite C determines NET specificity

### Disadvantages

- Weaker transport inhibition compared to SNRIs like duloxetine (Ki 15.9 uM vs 69 nM)
- Lack of aromatic moiety near subsite B compromises affinity
- Lower affinity for competitive displacement of nisoxetine (Ki 5.3 uM)
