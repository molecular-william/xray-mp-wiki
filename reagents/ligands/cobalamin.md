# Cobalamin (Vitamin B12)

## Overview

Cobalamin (vitamin B12) is the largest and most complex B vitamin. It consists
of a corrin ring chelating a central cobalt ion, with a
5,6-dimethylbenzimidazole (DMBI) base coordinating the alpha-axial position
and a variable beta-axial ligand. Biologically active forms include
methylcobalamin (Met-Cbl) and adenosylcobalamin (Ado-Cbl). Cobalamin serves
as a cofactor for enzymes involved in methyl group transfer and radical-mediated
reactions, including L-methionine synthase and ribonucleotide reductase.

## Properties

- **Chemical name**: Cyanocobalamin (CN-Cbl, industrial form)
- **Class**: Corrinoid vitamin

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Ligand for B12 transporters

Cobalamin is the transported substrate for ECF-CbrT and the classical
BtuCDF ABC transporter. ECF-CbrT binds CN-Cbl with Kd of 9.2 nM and
transports it with Km of 2.1 nM. The transporter also accepts
OH-Cbl, Met-Cbl, and Ado-Cbl as substrates, showing promiscuity toward
the beta-axial ligand.


### Co-factor for L-methionine synthase

In E. coli, MetH uses cobalamin as cofactor for L-methionine synthesis.
This property enables growth complementation assays where Cbl-dependent
growth of metE knockout strains is used to demonstrate transport activity.
