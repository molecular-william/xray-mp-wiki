# D-Aspartic Acid (D-Aspartate)

## Overview

D-aspartate is the D-enantiomer of the amino acid aspartate. Unlike most
naturally occurring amino acids which are the L-form, D-aspartate is found
in mammalian tissues and is considered a putative neurotransmitter and/or
neuromodulator. It binds to and activates N-methyl-D-aspartate receptors
(NMDARs) and is taken up by excitatory amino acid transporters (EAATs).

## Properties

- **Chemical name**: D-Aspartic acid
- **Class**: D-amino acid / neurotransmitter

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Substrate for glutamate transporter homolog GltTk

D-aspartate is transported by GltTk with identical Na+:substrate
coupling stoichiometry (3:1) as L-aspartate. The Km for transport is
1.1 uM and the Kd for binding at 500 mM NaCl is 374 nM. The crystal
structure of GltTk with D-aspartate at 2.8 A shows a nearly identical
binding mode to L-aspartate, with minor rearrangements in the
substrate binding site.


### Substrate for mammalian EAATs

Mammalian excitatory amino acid transporters (EAAT1-5) transport
D-aspartate with similar affinity to L-aspartate and L-glutamate
(low uM range). This uptake is important for D-aspartate signaling
in the nervous system.
