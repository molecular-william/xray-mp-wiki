# BPTU

## Overview

BPTU [1-(2-(2-(tert-butyl)phenoxy)pyridin-3-yl)-3-(4-(trifluoromethoxy)phenyl)urea] is a novel non-nucleotide antagonist of the human P2Y1 receptor, discovered by Bristol-Myers Squibb. It substantially reduces platelet aggregation with minimal effect on bleeding, making it a promising antithrombotic agent. BPTU binds to an allosteric pocket on the external receptor interface with the lipid bilayer, making it the first structurally characterized selective GPCR ligand located entirely outside of the transmembrane helical bundle.


## Properties

- **Chemical name**: 1-(2-(2-(tert-butyl)phenoxy)pyridin-3-yl)-3-(4-(trifluoromethoxy)phenyl)urea

- **Class**: Non-nucleotide P2Y1 receptor allosteric antagonist

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### P2Y1 receptor structural studies

BPTU was used as the co-crystallized ligand in the crystal structure of human P2Y1R at 2.2 A resolution (PDB 4M49). The structure revealed an allosteric binding site on the external receptor interface with the lipid bilayer, outside the transmembrane bundle. The urea group forms bidentate hydrogen bonds with L102(2.55), while the phenoxy and pyridyl groups make hydrophobic contacts with helices II and III.


### Allosteric modulation of nucleotide binding

BPTU exerts allosteric effects on 2MeSADP dissociation from P2Y1R. In kinetic binding assays, 10 uM BPTU accelerated the dissociation of tritiated 2MeSADP from membrane preparations of Sf9 cells expressing P2Y1R construct 2. This effect was abolished in the A106(2.59)W mutant, confirming that BPTU binding at the allosteric site modulates the orthosteric nucleotide site.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to P2Y1 receptor

The urea group forms two hydrogen bonds with the mainchain carbonyl of L102(2.55), available because P105(2.58) precludes intrahelical hydrogen bonding. The pyridyl group forms hydrophobic interactions with A106(2.59) and F119. The phenoxy benzene ring wedges into a cavity between helices II and III, interacting with T103(2.56), M123(3.24), L126(3.27), and Q127(3.28). The ligand likely enters the pocket via the lipid bilayer due to its high lipophilicity (HPLC logP = 5.7).
