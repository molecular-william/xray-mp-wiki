# Tamoxifen

## Overview

Tamoxifen is a triphenylethylene-based selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM) developed as a treatment for breast cancer over 40 years ago, currently prescribed to more than 200 million patients worldwide. Beyond its estrogen receptor (ER) activity, tamoxifen was shown to bind to a previously unidentified drug binding site within the NavMs voltage-gated sodium channel. Tamoxifen inhibits sodium currents with an IC50 of 830 nM against NavMs and 2.4 uM against human Na_v1.2, acting by stabilizing the non-conducting inactivated state of the channel.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to NavMs voltage-gated sodium channel

Tamoxifen binds to both inner and outer sites near the intracellular gate of the NavMs sodium channel. The inner site involves hydrogen bond interactions with M222 (intracellular gate residue), K226, and E227. The binding pocket shares geometric features with the estrogen receptor tamoxifen binding site, including hydrogen bonds with an aspartic acid at one end and a glutamate at the other. Tamoxifen occupancy impairs recovery from the inactivated state, resulting in fewer channels available for reopening.
