# Carboxyatractyloside (CATR)

## Overview

Carboxyatractyloside (CATR) is a toxic inhibitor of the mitochondrial ADP/ATP carrier (AAC) that locks the carrier in the cytoplasmic-open state (c-state). It is a glycoside produced by the fungus *Atractylis* species.

## Properties

- **Chemical class**: Glycoside (tractyloside derivative)
- **Membrane permeability**: Membrane-impermeable (cannot cross lipid bilayers)
- **Effect**: Binds to the cytoplasmic-facing conformation, trapping the c-state

## Mechanism of Action

CATR binds in the central cavity of the AAC when it is in the cytoplasmic-open conformation. Because it is membrane-impermeable, it can only access the binding site from the cytoplasmic side, selectively trapping the c-state.

## Use in Structural Biology

- CATR-inhibited ScAac2 structure solved (PDB: 4C9H, c-state, ~3.0 Å)
- Comparison of CATR-inhibited c-state with BKA-inhibited m-state revealed the profound conformational changes underlying the transport mechanism
- The two structures together provide a complete view of the [[alternating-access]] mechanism
