# Sodium Ion (Na+)

## Overview

Sodium ion (Na+) serves as a negative allosteric modulator in many class A G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), stabilizing the inactive conformation and reducing agonist binding affinity. In GPCRs, a highly conserved aspartate residue at position 2.50 (D2.50) serves as the key coordination site for a structurally bound Na+ ion within the transmembrane bundle. The Na+ binding pocket is located near the orthosteric ligand-binding site and its occupancy is mutually exclusive with high-affinity agonist binding. Additionally, sodium ion serves as an essential co-transport ion for secondary active transporters such as the Vibrio parahaemolyticus Sodium-Galactose Transporter (vSGLT) and as a coupling cation for the melibiose permease (MelB). MelB catalyzes electrogenic symport of galactosides coupled to Na+, Li+, or H+, with Na+ being the primary coupling cation. All three coupling cations compete for a single binding site with a protein/sugar/cation stoichiometry of unity. Na+ binding requires four to six coordination numbers from two to three helices, and the small ionic radius of Na+ requires coordination ligands in closer distance.
