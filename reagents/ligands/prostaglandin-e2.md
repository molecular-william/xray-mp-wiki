# Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2, Dinoprostone)

## Overview

Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2, dinoprostone) is the most abundant prostanoid in the human body and a major bioactive lipid mediator. It is synthesized locally from arachidonic acid by cyclooxygenases (COX-1 and COX-2) and prostaglandin E synthases. PGE2 exerts its physiological actions through four G protein-coupled receptors: EP1, EP2, EP3, and EP4. It plays crucial roles in labor induction, inflammation, pain, fever, atherosclerosis, cardiac ischemia, asthma, and cancer. PGE2 is the endogenous agonist of the EP3 receptor and served as the reference ligand in two independent crystal structures of the human EP3 receptor.


## Properties


## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Endogenous agonist of the EP3 receptor

PGE2 is the natural endogenous ligand of the EP3 receptor. Crystal structures
of EP3 bound to PGE2 (Morimoto et al., 2.9 A) and to the synthetic analog
misoprostol-FA (Audet et al., 2.5 A) revealed that PGE2 binds in a completely
enclosed binding pocket formed by residues from TM1, TM2, TM3, TM5, TM6, TM7,
and ECL2. The ligand carboxylate forms a salt bridge with Arg333(7.40), and
the E-ring hydroxyl coordinates a structured water molecule in the pocket.
PGE2 has a long residence time on EP3, consistent with the enclosed binding
pocket acting as a lid.


### Key interactions in the EP3 binding pocket

PGE2 interactions with EP3 include: (1) Salt bridge between the ligand
carboxylate and Arg333(7.40); (2) Hydrogen bonding with Tyr114(2.65),
Thr206(EL2), Ser336(7.43), and Gln339(7.46); (3) Hydrophobic contacts with
P55(1.39), M58(1.42), L329(7.36), V332(7.39), and L298(6.51); (4) A structured
water molecule coordinates the E-ring hydroxyl. The binding affinity (Kd) of
PGE2 for wild-type EP3 is approximately 3-5 nM as measured by radioligand
binding assays.


### Metabolism and inactivation

PGE2 is rapidly metabolized in vivo by 15-hydroxyprostaglandin dehydrogenase
(15-PGDH), which oxidizes the 15-hydroxyl group to form 15-keto-PGE2, a
much less active metabolite. This metabolic instability is a key limitation
for the therapeutic use of natural prostaglandins and motivates the
development of synthetic analogs such as misoprostol.
