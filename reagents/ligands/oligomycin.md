# Oligomycin

## Overview

Oligomycin is a macrolide antibiotic that potently inhibits the mitochondrial F1Fo ATP synthase by binding to subunit-c of the Fo domain. It blocks proton translocation through the c-ring, thereby inhibiting both ATP synthesis and ATP hydrolysis. The commercial preparation is a mixture of oligomycin A, B, and C (approximately 60% oligomycin A).

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### ATP synthase inhibitor

Oligomycin binds to the surface of the c10 ring at the interface between two adjacent subunit-c molecules, contacting helix 2 of both chains. Binding locks the essential carboxyl of Glu59 in a semiclosed conformation, preventing proton access to the aqueous half-channel formed by subunit-a.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to Subunit-c of F1Fo ATP synthase

Single H-bond via bridging water molecule between Glu59 carboxyl, Leu57 carbonyl, and oligomycin ester carbonyl (O36); numerous van der Waals interactions with residues including Phe64, Ala56, Ala60, and Leu63

- **Key residues**: Glu59, Phe64, Leu63, Ala56, Ala60, Leu57 (yeast numbering)

## Advantages and Disadvantages

### Advantages

- Potent and specific inhibitor of mitochondrial ATP synthase
- Defines a common drug-binding site on the c-ring for multiple antibiotics
- Useful for studying ATP synthase function and proton translocation

### Disadvantages

- Does not inhibit bacterial ATP synthase due to sequence divergence
- Mixture of A/B/C analogues complicates structure-activity analysis
