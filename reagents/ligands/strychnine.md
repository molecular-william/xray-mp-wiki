# Strychnine

## Overview

Strychnine is a potent indole alkaloid antagonist of the glycine receptor (GlyR), a pentameric ligand-gated ion channel in the Cys-loop receptor family. It exerts its toxic effects by competitively blocking glycine binding in the central nervous system, leading to muscle spasms, convulsions, and potentially death. Strychnine remains in use as a rodenticide. The 3.0 Å X-ray structure of human GlyRα3 bound to strychnine revealed the molecular basis of antagonist-induced inactivation in Cys-loop receptors.

## Use in Membrane Protein Work

### Antagonist-induced inactivation of glycine receptors

Strychnine binds to the orthosteric site of the glycine receptor α3 homopentamer with high affinity (Kd 43-52 nM by SPR and ITC). Binding induces expansion of the orthosteric binding pocket, opening of loop C, inward displacement of the M2-M3 loop, tilting of the pore-lining M2 helix towards the pore axis, and closure of the ion channel pore. This represents the first crystallographic snapshot of a pLGIC in the antagonist-induced inactive state.

## Binding Mode

### Binding to Glycine receptor α3

Strychnine binds at the subunit interface between the principal and complementary subunits in the orthosteric binding pocket of the extracellular domain. A key interaction is a hydrogen bond between the backbone carbonyl of Phe159 (complementary subunit) and the tertiary nitrogen of strychnine. The complementary subunit interface presents a positive electrostatic potential that interacts with the partial negative charge of the lactam oxygen of strychnine.

- **Pocket volume**: Expanded orthosteric pocket; loop C adopts an open conformation upon strychnine binding
